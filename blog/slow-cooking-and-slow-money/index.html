Slow Cooking and Slow Money: Kakeibo in the Kitchen

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

There is a small donabe on my stove this morning with root vegetables simmering in it — daikon cut into thick rounds, carrot, a single piece of kombu at the bottom, a cup of dashi, a spoonful of soy, a smaller spoonful of mirin. The lid is on crooked, the way it has been on crooked for six years, because the donabe chipped once when I moved apartments and has never quite sat flat since. The burner is on the lowest setting my stove allows. The vegetables will be ready in about forty-five minutes. I have no particular plans for those minutes. I will not stand over the pot.

This essay is about two household practices that look unrelated from the outside — slow cooking and kakeibo, the Japanese ledger method — and that turn out, when you have done both long enough, to be the same practice wearing different clothes. They are related in a way I did not see for a long time, and which I now believe is the quiet centre of what we call the slow life.

What the donabe knows that the microwave does not

Nimono — simmered dishes — are the grandmother food of Japan. You make them by putting hardy vegetables and a modest amount of protein into a seasoned broth, bringing it to a low simmer, and then leaving it. The heat must be low enough that the surface of the liquid trembles rather than bubbles. The dashi penetrates the daikon slowly, millimetre by millimetre. A rushed nimono is not a nimono. It is a stew that was not waited for.

What the donabe knows, and what the microwave does not, is that some transformations require time as an ingredient. You cannot substitute pressure, heat, or cleverness. The daikon absorbs the broth at the pace the daikon absorbs the broth. You can interfere, and the dish will be worse. You can hurry, and the dish will be worse. You can try a technological shortcut — a pressure cooker, an induction blast — and you will produce an edible thing that is not the thing.

A household that cooks nimono regularly learns something the wider culture is trying very hard to unteach: that patience is not a personal virtue. It is a technical requirement for certain outcomes. You do not simmer out of piety. You simmer because the daikon is, in fact, only going to become daikon-as-nimono if you let it.

What the ledger knows that the app does not

Kakeibo, the 120-year-old Japanese household ledger practice I have written about at length, works on almost exactly the same principle. You log each purchase by hand. You glance at the totals weekly. You sit down at the end of the month and answer four honest questions in writing. And you do this for month after month, without expecting a dramatic shift, because the dramatic shift is not where the method works.

A financial app can produce a beautiful pie chart of your spending within thirty seconds of connecting your bank account. A spreadsheet can auto-categorise a year of transactions in an afternoon. Both of these are, in the specific sense the donabe understands, shortcuts around the thing that was supposed to happen. The thing that was supposed to happen is the slow re-acquaintance with your own life. The pie chart, however accurate, is a representation of that re-acquaintance, not the re-acquaintance itself.

What the ledger knows is that attention, like the broth entering the daikon, happens at a pace that cannot be forced. You cannot look at six months of transactions in one evening and produce the same quality of noticing that six months of one-transaction-at-a-time produces. The information is the same. The knowledge is not.

The shared principle: some practices are time-shaped

I came to this comparison slowly. I had been keeping kakeibo for a couple of years and cooking nimono for longer when I noticed, one Sunday, that the two practices were competing for the same hour of my week. I was simmering a pot of kabocha while I worked through the previous week's ledger entries at the kitchen table, and the donabe was on the burner behind me producing a low steady warmth that I associated, quite clearly, with the same feeling the ledger was producing. Both practices were asking for the same kind of attention: low, steady, non-anxious, not goal-directed in the short term.

The philosopher Crispin Sartwell has written about aesthetic categories that are fundamentally durational — categories of experience whose meaning is inseparable from the length of time you sit inside them. A wabi-sabi bowl is one such category. A tea ceremony is another. I would add that a simmered dish and a handwritten ledger are two more. They share a property: you cannot sample them. A minute inside a forty-minute simmer is not one-fortieth of a nimono. It is not a nimono at all.

Sōtō Zen has a phrase for the orientation this requires — shikantaza, just sitting — and while I would not claim a pot of daikon is meditation, the attentional move is related. You are present to a process you did not invent and cannot hurry. You are a participant, not a driver.

What the daikon teaches about the budget

Four things, in my experience, that the kitchen teaches the ledger more clearly than any money book I have read.

One. Small heat, long time. A rolling boil breaks the daikon. A tracked spending sprint in January, followed by abandoning the system in February, breaks the ledger. Both are ruined by intensity. Both require the lowest sustainable setting you can actually maintain for the whole cook.

Two. Do not lift the lid too often. Every time you check the donabe, you release heat and delay the dish. Every time you re-audit your budget mid-month in a panic, you make new rules that undo the slow work the old rules were doing. Kakeibo explicitly limits you to a Sunday glance and a monthly reflection. This is not laziness. It is cooking discipline.

Three. The seasoning goes in at the right moment, not all at once. A nimono seasoned too early has flat vegetables and over-salted broth. A budget that imposes every new rule at the start of month one — a savings rate, three new categories, a subscription audit, a coffee ban — collapses by week three. Add one thing. Let it work. Add the next when the first has settled.

Four. The dish rests. A nimono served straight off the heat tastes thin. Cooled, returned, and gently re-warmed, it tastes like itself. The same is true of a monthly reflection. The answers I write on the last evening of the month are different — and truer — than the answers I could have written on the last afternoon. The rest is part of the cook.

Where the app economy fails both practices

I want to be careful here, because I am not anti-technology and I do not think a phone is a moral failure. But there is a pattern worth naming. The app economy has, in the last decade, produced a suite of tools that promise to do the slow practices faster. Meal kits that simulate home cooking in twenty minutes. Budgeting apps that auto-categorise your spending without your having to look at any transaction in particular. Both classes of tool have grown because they sell a real convenience. Both, in my experience, also produce a particular kind of hollowness — a sense that the household task has been performed but the household has not been touched.

The wabi-sabi instinct, applied here, is not to reject the convenience but to notice what the convenience is costing. A meal kit is fine for a Tuesday when you have nothing to give the kitchen. It is a poor substitute for the Sunday pot that was supposed to teach you what daikon does in dashi. A budgeting app is fine for visibility into which credit card was charged by which subscription. It is a poor substitute for the monthly sit-down that was supposed to teach you what you have been paying attention to with your life.

The tools are not the problem. Treating them as equivalent to the practices they shortened is the problem.

A combined Sunday, end to end

My slow-money-slow-cooking Sunday, for illustration. Your shape will differ; the principle is the point.

Mid-morning I walk to the small greengrocer three streets over. I buy what looks good: this week, daikon, carrot, a bunch of mitsuba, a small piece of salmon. I note the amounts in my paper kakeibo when I get home, while the kettle heats. Twelve hundred yen for the vegetables and fish, fifteen seconds to write it down. The money and the food enter my attention at the same time.

Early afternoon I start the nimono. The vegetables are cut while I answer the four monthly questions, which I do once a month in that same seat, at that same table. The donabe goes on. For the next forty-five minutes I do not cook and I do not budget. I sit at the table with tea and a book. The pot simmers behind me. The ledger is open on the table but I am not writing in it.

At some point the kitchen smells of dashi and something warm. I eat the nimono that evening, and again for lunch on Monday, and it is better on Monday because it has rested. The ledger entries for the week are already done because I did them as I went. I have spent maybe ninety minutes of Sunday on both practices combined. Neither felt like work.

This is not a discipline. It is a shape that household life takes when the practices are allowed to do what they were meant to do.

For readers who want to start somewhere

If you are drawn to this idea but unsure where to begin, I would offer the smallest possible entry points. Cook one simmered dish a week — root vegetables in dashi is a forgiving place to start. Keep a paper ledger for one month. Do them on the same day if you can. Do not try to be good at either.

The printable kakeibo journals I sell on Etsy are built around this Sunday rhythm — a monthly reflection page, a weekly glance page, a daily log that takes under a minute — and several readers over the years have told me that pairing the journal with a standing slow-cook afternoon was the combination that finally made both practices stick.

I write more about slow household practices and the quiet economies they produce in my weekly Substack. The donabe and the ledger are recurring characters.

What slow money and slow food are both really asking

Both practices, in the end, are asking the same thing. They are asking whether you are willing to let a transformation happen at the pace the transformation needs. The daikon will not become daikon-as-nimono any faster than it becomes daikon-as-nimono. Your household finances will not become a life you recognise any faster than you can look at them, month by month, one transaction at a time.

The slow life, as a phrase, has been thinned by marketing. Underneath the thinning, though, there is a small technical truth: some of the things we want most from our households — nourishment, clarity, the sense of a life lived at a pace that is ours — are durational. They cannot be rushed, and the attempt to rush them does not produce a worse version of the same thing. It produces a different thing, which does not feed you in the same way.

The donabe is quiet behind me. The ledger is open on the table. Both are doing their work. Neither needs me for a while. The slow life is, in large part, the patience to let them.
