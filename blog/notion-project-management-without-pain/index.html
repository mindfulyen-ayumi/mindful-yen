Notion Project Management Without the Pain

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The phrase project management arrives in a solo creator's life like a well-meaning uncle: full of advice, dressed for a boardroom, and faintly surprised that the kitchen table is the office. I have spent years politely declining that uncle's suggestions. What I use instead, and what I recommend to readers who write in asking how I keep the publication, the Etsy shop, and the Substack moving in the same direction, is a Notion project management setup that has almost nothing in common with the software the word usually implies.

There is no Gantt chart. There is no sprint. There is no burndown. There is, instead, a small database with three states, a weekly rhythm that I keep more or less religiously, and a habit of closing the laptop when the work for the day is done. That is the entire system. The rest of this essay is why.

Why Gantt charts fail at the kitchen table

A Gantt chart is a beautiful thing when you have six people and a release date. It shows dependencies. It shows slack. It shows the shape of a month the way a sonata shows the shape of a quarter hour. I understand its appeal; I worked in publishing for years, and I watched senior editors draw them by hand on large sheets of paper, and the room was always quieter afterwards.

At the kitchen table, a Gantt chart does something different. It makes the operator feel observed by an imaginary manager. Every bar becomes a promise to a supervisor who does not exist. The solo creator starts dressing the chart for a meeting that is not on the calendar, and the dressing consumes the hour that should have been the work. I have done this. I recognise it in other people's workspaces. It is the single most common failure mode of Notion project management for one person, and the remedy is not a better chart. The remedy is to stop charting.

What actually needs to be tracked, for a one-person studio, is much smaller: what am I doing today, what is waiting, and what is done. Three states. That is sufficient for almost any solo practice I have ever seen, and the readers who write in having tried more elaborate systems almost always describe the same feeling — that the system became a second job, and the first job quietly suffered.

The three-state task system

The database has one property that matters: state. The three states are Today, Waiting, and Done. That is the whole vocabulary. There is no "in progress," because "in progress" is a state that invites decoration. There is no "blocked," because "blocked" in a solo studio is almost always a polite word for "I have not decided." And there is no "backlog," because a backlog is a graveyard with better typography.

Each task is a row. Title, state, and a one-line note if it needs one. That is all. The "Today" view shows me the handful of items in that state and nothing else. The "Waiting" view I open perhaps twice a week, on Sunday and Wednesday, to see what has matured enough to move into Today. "Done" is a filtered view I look at once a month, during the monthly reflection, because the record of what was actually finished turns out to be a better planning tool than any forecast.

The constraint that makes this work is the one I resisted longest. Today cannot contain more than five rows. If a sixth row wants to be added, something else has to move back to Waiting. Five is not a magic number; it is simply what fits on the screen without scrolling, which is my test for whether a day is honest. A day you have to scroll is a day you are lying to.

The weekly rhythm that replaces the Gantt

The thing that a Gantt chart pretends to do — show the shape of the month — is actually done, in my workspace, by a weekly rhythm. Six days have shapes; one does not.

Monday is for writing. Not editing, not research, not admin. A draft in motion, or a draft begun. If Monday produces a draft, the week has already earned its keep. Tuesday is for editing whatever Monday produced, and for answering the reader inbox. Wednesday is for the publication's other work — Etsy listings, Substack scheduling, the slow domestic rhythm of keeping the shop alive. Thursday is for research and reading, which is the least urgent-feeling activity and therefore the one most easily displaced. I protect it the way I protect Monday. Friday is for the monthly and weekly reflection work, and for the finish of whatever is publishing that week. Saturday is quiet. Sunday is the review and the setup of the week ahead, and then Sunday becomes quiet too.

A rhythm is not a schedule. It bends. A week where Monday is taken by an emergency simply pushes writing to Tuesday, and editing to Wednesday, and the shape survives because it is a shape and not a grid. A Gantt chart breaks when a bar moves; a rhythm breathes. This distinction, which sounds mystical when written down, is in practice the difference between a Notion project management setup that you open on a Sunday evening with a cup of tea and one that you avoid all weekend because it will make you feel behind.

What about larger projects

Readers who have read this far are often waiting for the part where I admit that a three-state system cannot handle a book manuscript, a site redesign, or a six-month launch. I will admit the opposite. It handles them better than anything else I have tried, provided one rule is observed: large projects are not tasks; they are collections of tasks, and only the next task belongs in the database.

When I was redesigning Mindful Yen last spring, the project lived on a single Notion page with a short plain-English description, a list of the decisions I still needed to make, and a link to whichever task was currently in Today. The page was not a project plan. It was a thinking page. The database only ever held the one next thing. When that thing was Done, I opened the project page, decided what the next thing was, and put that in Today. A site redesign, which would have produced an intimidating Gantt chart, produced instead about forty small tasks over six weeks, done one at a time, on the days the rhythm allowed.

This is not a productivity hack. It is simply what a one-person studio actually does when nobody is watching. The project-management software industry has, for perfectly good reasons, optimised for teams. When a solo operator adopts team software, the software imports assumptions about coordination that do not apply, and those assumptions become friction. The three-state system is the smallest thing that still works.

The friction that is actually useful

Not all friction is bad. The kakeibo practice, which I have written about at length in the kakeibo Notion template essay, depends on a particular useful friction — the small resistance of having to log a purchase before spending is normalised. The three-state task system has a similar useful friction: the act of moving a task from Waiting to Today requires a small decision, and that decision is the management.

A Gantt chart tries to pre-decide. It asks you, on Monday, what you will do on Thursday. For a team, this is coordination; for one person, it is fiction. The useful friction of the three-state system is that the Thursday decision is made on Thursday, when you actually know what Thursday looks like. This is less efficient in theory and more efficient in practice, for the same reason that weather forecasts past three days are worse than asking someone to look out of the window.

What I removed to get here

The workspace I use today is the result of removing, not adding. Here, briefly, is what came out, and why — in case you are trying to simplify your own.

Out went priorities. I had a High-Medium-Low field for about a year. Every task eventually became High, because the alternative was admitting that something did not matter. Removing the field forced me to either keep a task in Today or move it to Waiting, which is a more honest question than how to rank a pile.

Out went deadlines on internal tasks. External deadlines — a Substack post scheduled for Saturday, an Etsy listing promised by Friday — still exist and still live on the editorial calendar I described in the solo-creator workspace essay. But internal deadlines, the ones I was setting for myself to force myself to work faster, were a form of self-management I had absorbed from corporate life and never examined. Removing them did not make me slower. It made me more truthful.

Out went tags. I used to tag tasks by project, by platform, by type. The tagging was more work than the tasks. I replaced tags with a short prefix in the title — MY: for Mindful Yen, SS: for Substack, ET: for Etsy — which is searchable and takes no maintenance. This is the only piece of apparent amateurism in the workspace, and it is deliberate.

A small note on tools and longevity

I use Notion because it is the tool that has, for me, stayed out of the way longest. I am aware this is not a universal experience. If you have tried Notion and found it heavy, the three-state system described here works on paper, on an index card in a pocket, in a plain text file, or in any tool you prefer. The system is not the software. The software is the software. A closing thought, from a Sunday evening

The quiet test I apply, when I am tempted to add a new field or a new view or a new database, is whether the addition will be the thing I see on a Sunday evening when I open the workspace with a cup of tea. If the answer is no, the addition fails. What I see on a Sunday evening is what the workspace is, regardless of what it contains. A good workspace is one that looks the same on a Sunday evening as it did a month ago, because a month ago was also a Sunday evening, and the rhythm was the same.
