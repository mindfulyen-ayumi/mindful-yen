Ikigai and Career Pivots: What Three Years of Quiet Change Actually Looked Like

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

When people ask me how I moved from editing into writing, I have two answers. The short one is that I was an editor at a mid-sized Tokyo publisher for nearly a decade, got quietly burnt out, took a long time to notice, and spent three years making a slow, unglamorous transition into writing full-time. The long one is the subject of this essay, because the short version — as every career-change article I have read concedes between the lines — leaves out almost everything that was actually difficult.

I want to write about what ikigai meant to me at each turn of that three-year reset. Not because my story is exemplary; it is not. But because the Western discourse on career change has been thoroughly colonised by a particular image — the four-circle Venn diagram, the find your passion injunction, the thirty-day sprint to a new identity — and the lived reality of a pivot, at least mine, looked nothing like that image. It looked, instead, like a series of very small adjustments to what I considered worth getting up for, spread over a period long enough that I did not notice I was changing until I had already changed.

Year one: the burnout I mistook for a personality trait

The first year of the pivot was not a pivot at all. It was a year of being exhausted in a job I still, intellectually, believed I loved. I was editing mostly non-fiction for a Tokyo house that had treated me well. I had colleagues I respected. On paper, I was succeeding. I was also waking up at 4 a.m. twice a week with a specific tightness behind my sternum, reading manuscripts on the commute with a kind of low-grade dread, and finding that I had almost nothing to say on the phone to friends when they asked how work was going.

I did not call this burnout for nearly nine months. I called it being bad at my job lately, or a rough quarter, or simply tired. The Japanese workplace gives you a rich vocabulary for effort and endurance and almost no vocabulary for the quiet failure of meaning. You can say you are busy. You can say the project is hard. There is no easy phrase for the work has stopped mattering to me and I do not know how to tell anyone.

What ikigai meant to me that year, if I am honest, was almost nothing. The word had gone thin. I could not have told you what got me out of bed. Mieko Kamiya, in her 1966 Ikigai-ni-tsuite, describes precisely this condition — not depression exactly, but a kind of ikigai-poverty, where the felt sense of meaningfulness has drained out of daily life without anything dramatic having happened to replace it. She is careful to distinguish this from clinical illness. It is, she writes, simply a state that can arrive in any life, and the task is to notice it rather than to manage it harder.

I started noticing mine in month ten, when a friend asked me, over tea, what I had read recently that I loved. I could not answer. Not because I had not read anything — I read constantly, professionally — but because I could not remember the last time I had read anything that was mine. Everything had been for work. That tea, which I had not planned as a turning point, was one.

Year two: the very slow permission

The second year of the pivot I call, now, the year of permission. I did not quit my job. I did not announce a change. I did not post on social media about reinvention. I did one thing: I started writing, in the mornings, for no one, for forty minutes before I went to the office. That was it. That was the whole of year two's structural change.

The writing was bad. It was diaristic, shapeless, frequently abandoned mid-paragraph. Some mornings I wrote about the light on the kitchen counter. Some mornings I wrote complaints about manuscripts I was editing that I could not say out loud. Some mornings I wrote nothing and sat with the cup of tea. The forty minutes was the commitment; the output was incidental.

What changed, slowly, was that the question what was today worth — the small Japanese sense of ikigai, not the Venn-diagram version — started being answered, in month four and then month five and then month six, by the morning writing. Not by the job. The job was where I went after the thing that mattered. This was not a realisation I welcomed. It was, in fact, somewhat destabilising. I had built my adult identity around being a good editor, and the slow discovery that my ikigai had migrated to a forty-minute unpaid practice was not a crisis I had asked for.

Ken Mogi, in The Little Book of Ikigai, describes a similar arc in the professional lives of several of the Japanese craftspeople he interviewed — the sense that vocation is something that reveals itself in small, unexamined hours before it announces itself in the CV. I did not read Mogi until later. But his observation matches what I experienced. The pivot was happening in the forty minutes. The other nine hours of the working day had not yet caught up.

Year three: the structural change no one saw coming

By the third year I had accumulated — without naming them as such — two pieces that had quietly taken on weight. One was a short essay on a retired binder I had interviewed, which I had pitched on a whim to a small literary magazine and which had been accepted. The second was a longer, unfinished manuscript on Tokyo neighbourhoods that I had been writing in the mornings for almost eighteen months and which was, I had begun to suspect, actually a book.

The structural change — the part that looks, in retrospect, like a career pivot — happened in about six weeks of year three. I reduced my editing hours to three days a week. I took the other two days to write. I had a difficult conversation with my boss in which she was, to her enormous credit, more generous than I had any right to expect. I did not resign dramatically. I did not burn anything. I simply began subtracting editing hours and adding writing hours until, by the end of year three, the ratio had inverted.

What ikigai meant at this turn was, for the first time in four years, a question I could answer easily. The mornings had become the evenings. The writing had become the work. The editing, which I still do selectively and still enjoy, had become the thing I do on the side. Nothing about this felt like finding my passion in the way the diagram promises. It felt like noticing, over a long period, where my attention had been going anyway, and slowly rearranging the calendar around that noticing.

What I would not call this

I want to be careful about three things, because career-change essays routinely misrepresent their own shape and I do not want to add another such essay to the pile.

First, this was not a leap. There was no single brave jump. There were, at most, fifty small decisions made over three years, most of them reversible, most of them unremarkable. A forty-minute morning. A pitched essay. A conversation with a trusted friend. A reduction from five days to four. The cumulative arithmetic of these is a career pivot; individually, none of them was one.

Second, it was not led by passion. I did not wake up in month one burning to be a writer. I woke up in month one exhausted, and the writing was the thing I did because it was the only thing that did not deplete me further. The passion — to the small extent that word applies — arrived later, as a consequence of the practice, not a prerequisite for it. The four-circle diagram, as I have argued elsewhere, has this exactly backwards.

Third, it was not financially heroic. I am not going to pretend I did this without savings, without a modest inheritance that covered an emergency fund, or without a spouse whose income stabilised the transition. These things mattered. A lot of career-change writing hides them; I would rather name them. The method I use for keeping personal finances legible during a transition like this is the Japanese kakeibo, which I have written about separately.

What the pivot asked of ikigai

Looking back, what the three years asked of the word ikigai was that it be allowed to be small and to change. Year one's ikigai was barely present. Year two's ikigai was a forty-minute practice no one knew about. Year three's ikigai was a working rhythm that had arranged itself around the forty minutes without my permission. None of these is a grand arrival. All of them are different versions of the same small question — what, today, was worth it — answered honestly and followed slowly.

If there is a lesson I would offer to readers considering a pivot, it is this. Do not try to find your new vocation before you have noticed where your attention is actually going. Most of the advice you will read is aimed at people trying to manufacture a new identity. The Japanese practice is aimed at people trying to notice the identity that has, in small hours, been quietly arriving. These are very different projects. The manufacturing project usually fails. The noticing project usually succeeds, given enough time.

I write more about this kind of slow, unglamorous professional transition in the weekly Mindful Yen Substack, including longer notes on what the first six months of the writing practice looked like in detail.

Final thought

The career pivot sold by the English-language self-help industry is a thirty-day sprint, a bold leap, a four-circle alignment. The pivot I actually lived was three years of small adjustments made largely in the dark. They are not the same thing, and I suspect my version is the more common one — simply less well documented, because it does not photograph as well. The ikigai at each turn was modest. The cumulative effect, three years on, was not. That is, I think, how most real pivots happen. Quietly, slowly, and mostly without witnesses.
