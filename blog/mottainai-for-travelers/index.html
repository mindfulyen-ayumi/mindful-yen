Mottainai for Travellers: How I Packed for Three Months in a Carry-On

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

In the autumn of 2024 I left my apartment in Tokyo for ninety-two days, across four countries — the United Kingdom, Portugal, France, and a short detour through Slovenia — with one soft-sided carry-on bag that weighed, empty, 1.4 kilograms, and, full, 8.6. Into it went everything I used for the three months: clothing, laptop, cables, a small paperback library that rotated through bookshops in three cities, a notebook, one pair of spare shoes, and the small toiletry kit I have been refining for a decade. I came home with the same bag, four grams heavier. This essay is an honest account of how the packing was done, where the discipline came from, where it broke, and what it means to pack as a small expression of mottainai.

I am writing this partly because readers have asked for it, and partly because the English-language internet's treatment of minimalist packing has, in the last few years, drifted toward a slightly aesthetic performance that I do not recognise. The version I want to write is closer to the Japanese inheritance I actually grew up with — quieter, more practical, less photographed, and honest about the mistakes.

The frame: what I was trying to honour

Packing mottainai-fully is not packing minimally. Minimal packing is usually a vanity project — the aesthetic of the eight-item capsule wardrobe, the curated five-item toiletry photograph. Mottainai packing is different. It asks a specific question of each object: does this object deserve the three months I am about to put it through, and do I deserve the object? The second half of the question is the part most minimalist packing skips. You are not only choosing what to carry; you are choosing what you can be trusted to steward for ninety-two days in unfamiliar climates and tired bodies.

This reframing removes a lot of the clutter from the packing decision. The question is no longer what is the smallest number of things I can survive on. It is what is the right set of things to travel alongside as companions for three months. A companion you will resent by week three is the wrong object even if it weighs nothing. A companion you will use daily and feel grateful for is the right object even if it weighs more than its minimal equivalent.

The list, as it actually was

I will not pretend the list was optimised. It was a version of a list I have been iterating for eight years, refined by previous trips. The core of it:

Clothing. Three merino base layers, two pairs of trousers (one darker, one lighter), one dress that worked for dinner and for travel days, four pairs of wool socks, underwear for five days, one long cardigan, one packable rain shell, one soft scarf that doubled as a blanket on trains. No formal wear beyond the dress. No dedicated exercise clothing — I used the base layers.

Shoes. One pair of walking shoes worn on the plane. One pair of soft leather flats in the bag for warmer cities and restaurants. No third pair. I had tried a third pair on a previous trip and come home with it almost unworn.

Toiletries. A small zip pouch. A bar of soap in a tin, a bar of shampoo in a second tin, a toothbrush, toothpaste, a single small jar of oil that served as moisturiser and occasional hair oil, a razor, sunscreen in a fifty-millilitre tube, a comb. Nothing else. I replaced the sunscreen once, in Lisbon.

Work. A thirteen-inch laptop, charger, one small USB-C hub, a pair of wired earbuds, a short extension cable with two sockets, an adapter block. That was it. No tablet. No second device. No camera — my phone did everything I needed.

Notebook and reading. One A5 notebook, two pens, and a paperback at any given time. When I finished a paperback I traded it at a used bookshop in the next city and bought the next one. The paperback population in my bag stayed at one for the whole trip.

Small things. A cotton handkerchief, a fabric tote folded flat, a small washi paper envelope holding receipts, a collapsible one-litre water bottle, a spare fabric mask, earplugs, a sleep mask, a small first-aid pouch with plasters and paracetamol. Passport and one card wallet.

That is the whole list. Eight and a half kilograms.

The discipline underneath

The discipline was not in the packing week. It was in the years before. The objects on that list are objects I have tested individually across many trips. The merino base layers I have owned for five years. The notebook is the eighth of its kind. The leather flats are the second pair of the same model. Each one earned its place on a previous trip by being the thing I reached for and did not regret.

This is the mottainai angle that is easy to miss. You do not pack well by agonising in the week before departure. You pack well by having paid attention across the preceding years to which of your objects have genuine mottai and which are decorative. By the time the packing week arrives, the decisions have mostly been made by the objects themselves — some have raised their hands over the years, and those are the ones you take.

The corollary is that a first serious attempt at this kind of packing is almost always mediocre. I packed badly for three years before I packed well for the first time. The early trips produced the information the later ones ran on. If you are attempting this for the first time, expect the first trip to be instructive rather than exemplary. The second will be better. The third will be the one where the ritual clicks.

Where the discipline broke

I want to be honest about the mistakes, because honest packing writing is rare and the genre deserves some.

I over-packed the cardigan. The long cardigan I brought was heavier than it needed to be. A lighter version existed in my closet and I had chosen this one because it was warmer, forgetting that I would spend most of the trip in climates where the base layers plus the rain shell were adequate. I wore the cardigan perhaps six times in ninety-two days. It is the object I would remove from the list for a next version.

I under-packed socks. Four pairs of wool socks was one pair too few. By the second week I was doing a small hand-wash rotation that, while not burdensome, created a low-level friction every four days. The right number was five. Five pairs would have added perhaps eighty grams. That is the right trade.

I packed a pair of earrings I did not wear once. They were small. They weighed nothing. They were also pure optimism — the idea that I would attend an evening that needed earrings, which I did not. Small items escape the mottainai test more easily than large ones, and this is a trap. The weight is not the point. The attention is.

I over-packed cables. I brought a second charging cable "just in case." The first cable did not fail. The second cable travelled four countries in a zip pouch and came home untouched. Redundancy is almost never mottainai-compatible; it is a bet that the object you already trust will fail, and if you trust the object correctly, the bet is wasteful.

What I bought en route, and why

I did not arrive in London with everything I would need. I never do. Buying small things along the way is part of how the packing stays light. I bought a warmer scarf in Porto when November turned cold (the one I had brought was thinner), a tin of French hand cream in Lyon (the oil I had packed worked for skin but not for hands in the dry hotel-heated air), and a small packet of washi envelopes in Tokyo on the way home through a connection in Haneda, because I had run out.

The scarf came home with me. The hand cream was used up. The envelopes I am still using.

Buying along the way is only mottainai-compatible if you carry home what you bought. Buying as a tourist practice — acquiring things in each city as evidence of having been there — is the opposite of mottainai. The things go into a drawer at home and are not used. The rule I follow is simple: I buy an object abroad only if I was going to have to buy its equivalent at home anyway. A scarf I would have bought in Tokyo is acceptable. A souvenir I would not have bought is not.

What came home

The bag came home four grams heavier. The additions: the Portuguese scarf (which replaced, on the way, the lighter Tokyo one that I gave to a colleague in Lisbon who needed it more than I did), a small carved wooden bird from a market in Ljubljana (gifted, so not a purchase), and the residue of a Parisian notebook I had started and finished over the course of November. The paperbacks had all been traded or donated along the way. The toiletries were used up and replaced in kind. The cables and electronics returned untouched by loss.

The bag itself came home softer at the corners than it had left, which is the shape of a bag that has been used rather than paraded.

The quiet principle

If there is a principle underneath this that I would want to leave with a reader, it is not pack less. It is pack things you will have a relationship with. The distinction sounds soft; it does most of the work.

A bag of eight items you have a relationship with is lighter, in the way that matters, than a bag of three items you do not. Weight in kilograms is a proxy for the real measurement, which is whether you resent your bag by week three. A bag you resent is a heavy bag. A bag you trust is a light one, even at eight kilograms.

This reframes the mottainai question around travel quite specifically. The objects you carry should deserve the trip, and the trip should deserve the objects. Both are real requirements. A hundred-pound jacket you never trust because you are afraid of damaging it is not mottainai-compatible; a thirty-pound jacket you actually wear in the rain is. Travel mottainai is a relationship question, not a weight one.

The return scene

When I came home from that trip in the first week of 2025, I did something I now do after every long trip. I unpacked the bag on the living room floor, left everything laid out for a full day, and did not put it away until the evening. The delay was deliberate. Each object in turn got to be looked at, thanked in a small wordless way, and then returned to its place. The scarf from Porto went into the coat cupboard. The paperback that had come home with me — a Slovene-to-English translation of a Drago Jančar novel — went onto the shelf. The notebook went into the archive box. The bag itself I hung by the door, empty, where it has been waiting since.

That is, in the end, what travel mottainai means to me. Not what you take. What you bring home, and how you let it rest.
