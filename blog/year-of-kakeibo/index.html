A Year of Kakeibo: What I Spent, What I Saved, What I Learned

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I have kept kakeibo for longer than the year this essay describes, but there is something about a complete annual cycle that makes it the honest unit of reflection for this practice. A month tells you about a month. A year tells you about a household. What follows is the account of one such year in my own Tokyo household — roughly, though not exactly, the twelve months ending in March — with the numbers softened but kept true to shape. I have rounded most figures to the nearest ten thousand yen for readability. The arc is what matters; the precise decimals would only get in the way of it.

I want to be clear about what this essay is and is not. It is not a case study meant to prove that kakeibo produces particular savings outcomes. It is not a template you should expect your own year to match. It is a single household's twelve months, recorded in pencil in a paper journal, read back across a long quiet afternoon in April, and written up here because readers keep asking me what a year of the practice actually looks like from the inside.

The starting picture

I entered the year with a median monthly self-payment of about three hundred and ten thousand yen, paid from my freelance buffer account into my main account on the first of each month. My fixed costs — rent on a small one-bedroom near the eastern edge of central Tokyo, utilities, health insurance, pension contributions, transit pass, three subscriptions I had decided to keep — came to roughly a hundred and sixty thousand yen. My target savings figure was forty thousand yen per month. The remainder, around a hundred and ten thousand yen, was my monthly spending envelope, distributed across the four kakeibo categories.

These numbers will read as small to many Anglophone readers. Tokyo rents for an unfashionable neighbourhood are much lower than one expects from Western coverage, and the yen's purchasing power for daily expenses — grocery runs, lunches, train fares — remains comfortable, even after the last two years of inflation. The ratios are what travel. The absolute numbers are local.

What the spending actually looked like

Across the year, my spending across the four canonical kakeibo categories settled into a shape that surprised me in several small ways.

Survival spending — groceries, daily meals, transit beyond the pass, basic household goods — came in at around forty-eight thousand yen per month on average, with a remarkable stability across the year. The range was narrow: the lowest month was forty-four thousand, the highest was fifty-three thousand. Survival turns out to be, for a single-person Tokyo household, almost a fixed cost in disguise. The knob I could turn here was small.

Optional spending — lunches out, coffees, small discretionary purchases, clothes — averaged about thirty-five thousand yen per month, with much more variance. The lowest month was twenty-two thousand yen; the highest was fifty-one thousand. The high months were clustered in late autumn and early spring. Looking back at the entries, the pattern was obvious: seasonal transitions produced small clothing expenses, and the social calendar of late autumn included several unplanned dinners. I had not seen this pattern until I read the year back.

Culture spending — books, magazines, a monthly film, concert tickets, the occasional museum — averaged about eighteen thousand yen per month. The figure was higher in the second half of the year than the first, and deliberately so. The reflection after the sixth month had flagged that I had been starving this category in favour of optional spending that produced less lasting value. I rebalanced. The second half of the year is where that rebalancing shows up.

Extra spending — the category for irregular, genuinely one-off expenses — averaged about nine thousand yen per month, but was very unevenly distributed. Three months had essentially zero extra spending. Two months had extra spending above thirty thousand yen each, both tied to specific events: a gift for a friend's wedding and an unplanned medical expense. This is the category where averaging is most misleading. The mean is not the right number to carry forward. The right number is a planning cushion large enough to absorb the occasional spike.

What I saved

The forty-thousand-yen monthly target held for nine months of the year and was missed, by small amounts, in three. Two of the missed months were the heavy extra-spending months described above. The third was a month when my buffer account had a genuinely lean quarter and I had to reduce the self-payment figure, which compressed the savings line along with everything else.

The year's total savings came in at around four hundred and twenty thousand yen, about twelve percent below the aspirational four hundred and eighty thousand that twelve on-target months would have produced. For readers used to Western personal-finance benchmarks, this savings rate — roughly eleven percent of gross income — may seem modest. In the Japanese freelance context, in a low-growth environment, with a buffer account and a tax account running alongside the kakeibo, it is a durable figure. The kakeibo's real question is not whether the rate is high. It is whether the rate is held across years.

Three things that surprised me

Reading the year back produced three specific surprises, which I want to record because they are the sorts of patterns that are only visible at the annual scale.

The first was the stability of survival spending. Before keeping kakeibo I would have told you that my grocery and daily-meal spending was one of the more variable parts of my household budget. The year's data showed the opposite. Within a range of about twenty percent, survival is essentially fixed. This freed me, over the course of the year, to stop watching this category closely. The attention it had been consuming was better spent elsewhere.

The second surprise was the relationship between seasonal weather and optional spending. The high-spending months were the transitional ones — November and March — not the cold months or the hot months I would have guessed. The pattern was real and repeatable. Knowing this in advance, I have since started a small optional-spending buffer for November and March specifically, which takes pressure off those months without changing the annual total.

The third surprise was the value of the culture rebalancing. Moving a few thousand yen per month from optional to culture, starting in the seventh month, produced a disproportionately large improvement in my subjective sense of the year's quality. A long rainy Sunday spent with a book bought for fifteen hundred yen out-performed, by a wide margin, three separate afternoons spent on small optional purchases I could not now recall. The kakeibo had been showing me this in monthly data for half a year. It took reading the full annual cycle to see it clearly.

What the reflections produced

Twelve monthly reflections, read back in one sitting, make a document that does not exist in any other financial practice I am aware of. It is not a ledger. It is a diary of how a household thought about money over a year. Mine ran to about fourteen thousand Japanese characters across the twelve entries — roughly the length of a long essay — and re-reading it was, I am aware how this sounds, emotionally affecting.

What I saw in the sequence was a slow change in what I was paying attention to. Early in the year the reflections were dominated by small anxieties about specific overspends. By the middle of the year the reflections had shifted to questions of category balance and structural habits. By the final months the reflections were less about money at all and more about whether the categories themselves still mapped onto the life I was actually living. That arc — from anxiety to structure to re-examination — is, I suspect, a fairly typical kakeibo year. It is certainly the arc my grandmother's ledger shows across her own early years, if I read her annotations right.

The document I ended the year with is stored, in paper form, in a drawer near my kitchen table. It is not shared. It is not digitised. It is the single most valuable thing the practice produced. I expect to re-read it again in a few years, and to find that the person writing the entries was someone I recognise but do not exactly remember being.

Tools, honestly

A brief note on what I used. The paper journal I describe is a standard A5 ledger from a stationery shop in Ginza that I replace each January. The digital side — daily log, running totals, quarterly rolling averages — lives in a Notion workspace I have iterated on for years, which handles the calculation while the paper handles the reflection. Neither tool is essential; the practice is older than either. But the hybrid has proven durable for me across the year the essay describes.

The separate piece on kakeibo on paper vs Notion vs sheets goes into more honest detail about what each medium hides and reveals, for readers considering the trade-off.

What a year of the practice is actually for

If you had asked me, at the start of the year I am describing, what I expected a year of kakeibo to produce, I would probably have said: better savings rate, clearer sense of spending categories, more control. What the year actually produced was something different. The savings rate was fine, the categories were clearer, and control is not the word I would use now. The word I would use is attention. A year of kakeibo is a year of paying attention to the small economic texture of one's own life, and the attention turns out to be the practice's product, not its precondition.

This is, I think, what Hani was describing in her 1951 editorial — the passage I mentioned in the piece on her history. The ledger teaches you to see clearly, decide soberly, and improve over time, and the capabilities do not stay in the ledger. They move outward. A year is how long it takes for the outward movement to become visible.

If you would like monthly company through your own year of the practice, I write a short note most weekends on the Mindful Yen Substack, including the reflections that have surfaced in my current ledger. It is not a substitute for your own practice. It is a neighbouring table in the same café.

Your year will not match mine. The shape — month one effort, month three behavioural shift, month six quietening, month twelve revelation — tends to recur. The specific numbers, the specific surprises, the specific rebalancings, are yours. Keep the ledger. Write the reflections. Read them back in one long afternoon a year from now, with tea, in a quiet room. That afternoon is what the practice is for.
