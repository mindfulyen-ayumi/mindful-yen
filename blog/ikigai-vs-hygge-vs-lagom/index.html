Ikigai vs Hygge vs Lagom: Why the Comparison Fails

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Sometime around 2017, the comparison began appearing in glossy magazines and lifestyle sections on both sides of the Atlantic. Ikigai vs Hygge vs Lagom. Three "untranslatable" words from three small or distant cultures, lined up neatly in a sidebar, each given a one-sentence definition and an accompanying photograph: a candle-lit Copenhagen kitchen, a minimal Stockholm interior, an Okinawan elder smiling at the camera. The implicit promise of the format was that the discerning reader could pick the one that suited them — Japanese for purpose, Danish for cosiness, Swedish for moderation — and import it into their living room by the weekend.

I want to make a careful argument against this comparison, not because comparing words across cultures is illegitimate, but because this particular comparison is built in a way that flattens all three of the words involved. The flattening makes for an easy magazine sidebar and an essentially useless guide to any of the actual concepts. If you have only encountered ikigai through this kind of three-way line-up, you have, I would gently say, encountered something that is not really ikigai, alongside something that is not really hygge and something that is not really lagom.

What each word actually means in its own language

Let me start with the rough corrections, language by language.

Ikigai (生きがい), in everyday Japanese, is closer to the small reason a particular day was worth getting up for than to the grand career-purpose framework the four-circle Venn diagram suggests. The serious Japanese book on the subject, Mieko Kamiya's Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966), distinguishes between ikigai as object and ikigai-kan as the felt sense of meaningfulness, and treats the second as the more durable. Ken Mogi's later Awakening Your Ikigai (2017) describes the everyday usage in Japan as small, daily, and unrelated to one's job. None of this is captured by the one-sentence sidebar definition a Japanese sense of life purpose.

Hygge, in Danish, is most accurately rendered as a quality of social cosiness — the feeling of being safely and warmly together with a small group of close people, often around food and candles, often in winter. The Danish writer Meik Wiking's The Little Book of Hygge (2016) is the most-cited English-language treatment, though Danish ethnographers have written about hygge for decades and many of them have noted that Wiking's version is somewhat smoothed for export. The serious academic source is the anthropologist Jeppe Trolle Linnet, whose work on hygge as a marker of class belonging and Danish national identity is, frankly, more useful than the Anglophone wellness version. Hygge is not, in actual Danish usage, a one-word slogan. It is a complicated social practice with rules about who is invited, what is discussed, and what is excluded.

Lagom, in Swedish, is an adverb and adjective meaning roughly just the right amount — neither too little nor too much. It is folk-etymologically, though probably not actually, derived from the Viking phrase laget om, "around the team," referring to the passing of a shared cup such that everyone took only their fair share. The English-language books on lagom — there were several in the 2017–2018 wave — present it as a wellness lifestyle prescription, which it is not. In actual Swedish usage it is a structural cultural value about moderation, fairness, and not standing out, with a dense relationship to Swedish social democracy and the Jantelagen (Law of Jante) tradition. It is closer to a political-cultural temperament than to a personal practice.

You can see, even from these short paragraphs, why lining the three up is misleading. They are not the same kind of thing. Ikigai is closer to a phenomenology of meaning. Hygge is closer to a social practice. Lagom is closer to a cultural-political temperament. They cannot be slotted into the same template because they are not the same shape.

Why the comparison-format took hold anyway

The reason the three-way comparison became so popular has, I think, very little to do with the words themselves and a great deal to do with a particular Anglophone reader who emerged around 2015 and was acutely felt by 2017.

That reader was tired. Tired of long working hours, of the post-financial-crisis economy, of the constant performance-management of the digital self. The reader wanted to believe that somewhere — Tokyo, Copenhagen, Stockholm — other societies had quietly worked out a better arrangement, and that the better arrangement could be summarised in one untranslatable word. The publishing industry, sensing the appetite, produced book after book on this premise. The format was lucrative. By 2018 there were book-length treatments of fika (Swedish), friluftsliv (Norwegian), sisu (Finnish), wabi-sabi (Japanese), and several others, each promising the same thing in different costume.

I do not blame the readers. The exhaustion was real and the longing for a quieter arrangement was honourable. What was unfortunate was the format. By promising a one-word answer, the books had to flatten the words to fit. The Danish hygge that arrived in English by 2017 was not the hygge any thoughtful Dane would have recognised. The Swedish lagom was not really lagom. The Japanese ikigai, as I have written elsewhere, was a 2014 wellness graphic with a Japanese label glued onto it.

The structural problem with importing single words

The deeper issue is that none of these words live alone in their home cultures. Each is woven into a thick fabric of other practices, institutions, and historical conditions, and the fabric is what does most of the work the single word gets credit for.

Hygge in Denmark is supported by short working hours, near-universal childcare, dense social trust, dark winters that genuinely call for indoor candle-lit gatherings, and a small homogeneous national culture in which the rules of who hygge is shared with are quietly understood. Lift the word out of that fabric and try to install it in a Brooklyn apartment with sixty-hour working weeks and atomised neighbours, and what you get is candles and an Instagram aesthetic. The candles are nice. They are not hygge.

Lagom in Sweden is supported by progressive taxation, a strong public sector, a long political tradition of solidarity, and the Jantelagen norm against individual ostentation. Lift the word out and try to install it as a personal lifestyle in a culture that valorises maximisation and individual distinction, and what you get is a Pinterest board of beige interiors. The interiors are pleasant. They are not lagom.

Ikigai in Japan, particularly in the Okinawan instance the Western retelling has leaned on most heavily, is supported by moai mutual-aid groups, multi-generational households, slow-food traditions, visible elder roles, and a religious-ritual life that includes regular ancestral observance. I have written about this in more detail in a separate piece on the Buettner Blue Zones reporting. Lift the word out and try to install it as an individual self-actualisation practice, and what you get is a Venn diagram on a poster. The poster is decorative. It is not ikigai.

What a more honest comparison would actually do

I am not against cross-cultural learning. I am against the format that pretends three words from three cultures are interchangeable lifestyle modules.

A more honest comparison would do something like the following. It would describe each word inside its own context, at length, with the social and historical conditions that make it possible. It would note the parts of each fabric that are exportable and the parts that are not. It would resist the temptation to extract a single takeaway from each. It would, if it had to make a recommendation, recommend less the importing of foreign words than the patient examination of one's own context — what fabric is already available where one lives, what is missing, what could be slowly built.

Most of the lessons of hygge, read carefully, point toward the slow re-establishment of small dense social ties one has the structural support to maintain. Most of the lessons of lagom point toward institutional and personal moderation that resists the maximisation logic of contemporary economic life. Most of the lessons of ikigai, on my reading, point toward a quiet weekly noticing of small daily reasons, distributed across many anchors, with no single one of them asked to be a complete answer. Each of these is recognisable in the others, and each is its own thing. The convergence is at the level of underlying human needs — for connection, for moderation, for meaning — rather than at the level of importable practice.

A reading list, for readers who want the originals

If you would like to engage with these concepts seriously rather than through the magazine-sidebar version, the reading list I would offer, in order of usefulness, is roughly as follows.

For ikigai: Mieko Kamiya, Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966; available in Japanese, with partial English summaries in academic literature). Ken Mogi, Awakening Your Ikigai (2017; popular but Japanese-authored and free of the four-circle diagram). Gordon Mathews, What Makes Life Worth Living? How Japanese and Americans Make Sense of Their Worlds (1996; an anthropology monograph, the best English-language academic treatment I know of).

For hygge: Jeppe Trolle Linnet's academic work on hygge as social and class practice (search his name in journals on Scandinavian studies and consumer culture). Meik Wiking, The Little Book of Hygge (2016), is the popular reference; treat it as the smoothed-for-export version. Anything by the Danish anthropologist Judith Friedman Hansen on Danish national character will deepen the picture considerably.

For lagom: Lola Akinmade Åkerström, Lagom: The Swedish Secret of Living Well (2017), is a more thoughtful treatment than most of the wave it appeared in, partly because the author is Nigerian-Swedish and brings a useful cross-cultural eye. For the political-cultural background, any standard introduction to the Swedish welfare state and Jantelagen will do more than most lagom-branded books.

For all three, the umbrella reading I would recommend is anthropological rather than self-help. The discipline that takes culture seriously as a thick fabric, rather than as a set of detachable modules, is the discipline that has the most useful things to say about whether and how any of these concepts can travel.

Subscribers to the Mindful Yen Substack get a periodic list of these kinds of secondary sources. There is no rush. The reading is the practice.

Final thought

The three-way comparison flatters its readers and flattens its subjects. Ikigai is not Japanese hygge. Hygge is not Danish lagom. Lagom is not Swedish ikigai. Each word is a small visible feature of a much larger fabric, and the fabric is what does the work. The honest move, as a reader in any of the cultures these words are imported into, is to stop shopping for foreign nouns and to start examining the fabric one already has — what is dense, what is thin, what could be slowly rewoven. That work is less photogenic than a candle-lit kitchen or a Venn diagram. It is also, I think, the actual lesson the three traditions, taken together, would like to teach.
