Notion vs Obsidian: One Year of Using Both

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Last spring, after some months of mild dissatisfaction with both tools when used alone, I decided to keep Notion and Obsidian running side by side for a full year. The experiment was not meant as a comparison piece. It was meant as an honest test of whether the two tools were doing different jobs or merely the same job in different costumes. A year later, I can say with some confidence that they are doing different jobs, that I have kept both, and that the choice between them is not really a choice — it is a question of which kind of writing you are doing this morning.

This essay is about that year. If you are deciding between the two tools, you can try the Notion workspace I use here → while you read. Obsidian is free and downloads in about thirty seconds, so the test is genuinely cheap to run yourself.

The premise: not all writing is the same writing

The reason a single tool tends to feel like the wrong tool, eventually, is that the word "writing" covers at least three quite different activities. There is structured writing — newsletters, essays scheduled for publication, transactional records like a kakeibo ledger. There is exploratory writing — half-formed notes, reading marginalia, the slow accumulation of thoughts that may or may not become essays. And there is operational writing — the editorial calendar, the reader inbox, anything that exists to coordinate other work.

A tool optimised for one of these three is, by structural necessity, slightly bad at the other two. Notion is excellent at structured and operational writing and noticeably awkward at exploratory writing. Obsidian is excellent at exploratory writing and slightly clumsy at the operational kind. After a year of trying to fold one into the other, I have stopped trying. The two tools occupy different rooms in the workspace, and the rooms have different purposes.

What I keep in Notion

Notion holds the spine of the studio. The editorial calendar, the reader inbox, the asset library, the kakeibo ledger, the monthly reflection database. These are documented in the solo creators essay and the kakeibo essay, so I will not repeat the structure here. The point is that everything in Notion has a defined shape — a row in a database, a property, a relation. The shape is the value. Without it, the same information would be impossible to read across months.

Notion is also where I draft anything intended for publication once it is past the rough stage. I write the first messy version of an essay in Obsidian, but the moment it starts to want a structure — sections, links, a publish date — I move it into Notion. The move is the cue that the piece has started to be a piece rather than a thought.

What I keep in Obsidian

Obsidian holds everything before structure. Reading marginalia. Half-paragraphs that occurred to me on the train. Quotations transcribed by hand from books I am unlikely to write about for months. Loose chains of association — a note on a Hani essay leading to a note on Mueller's handwriting study leading to a note on my grandmother's penmanship. None of these would survive the request to fit a database row. They would be flattened by it.

The tool that makes Obsidian useful is the one its enthusiasts overstate slightly: bidirectional links between plain Markdown files. The link is not magic. What the link does is permit a note to belong to several emerging clusters at once without you having to decide, today, which cluster it belongs to. Notion can technically do this with relations. In practice, the friction of creating a relation in Notion is high enough that you stop, and the friction of typing two square brackets in Obsidian is low enough that you do not. The cumulative effect of that small difference, over a year of notes, is large.

The handoff between the two

The interesting question is not which tool is better. It is what the handoff between them looks like. In my workflow, the handoff is a single, deliberate moment — the moment a thought in Obsidian has acquired enough structure to deserve a row in Notion. Before that moment, copying it into Notion would be premature. After that moment, leaving it in Obsidian means it never becomes a piece of work.

The cue I use is when the note in Obsidian has, by itself, accumulated three or four bidirectional links to other notes. That density is the indicator that a topic has crossed from a passing interest into a small territory. Once the territory exists, I open Notion and create a row for it in the editorial calendar with a one-sentence pitch. The Obsidian note remains. It becomes the source material for the eventual essay rather than the essay itself.

What each tool is genuinely bad at

Notion's weaknesses

Notion is slow with very long documents. Anything over about three thousand words begins to feel slightly sluggish in the editor, particularly on older laptops, and the lag interrupts the rhythm of writing in a way that matters. I now write very long pieces in iA Writer and paste them into Notion only at the editing stage. This is a workaround. It is not a deal-breaker, but it is a reminder that Notion is, at its heart, a database tool with a writing surface attached, rather than a writing tool with a database attached.

Notion is also, structurally, an online tool. The mobile app and the web app are usable offline for a short time, but the experience assumes a connection. For someone who writes on trains and in cafés with intermittent wifi, this matters. Obsidian's plain-Markdown-on-disk model means it works the same whether the connection is present or absent, and over a year of cafés I came to value that property more than I expected to.

Finally, Notion's data is yours, but in the sense that you can export it, not in the sense that it lives in plain files on your machine. The export works — I have tested it — but the dependency on Notion as the active editor is real. If you want true file-on-disk longevity for your writing, Notion is the wrong tool. This is the case for keeping exploratory writing in Obsidian regardless of how you feel about the tool.

Obsidian's weaknesses

Obsidian has no databases in the Notion sense. There are plugins that approximate the behaviour, and several of them are competent, but the experience is closer to assembling the feature than using it. For anything that needs reliable tabular structure — a kakeibo ledger, an editorial calendar, a reader inbox — Obsidian is the wrong tool. I have seen people try, and the systems they end up with are admirable engineering and unpleasant daily software.

Obsidian also asks more design decisions of you, sooner. The default workspace is intentionally sparse, and a new user is required to make taste choices about themes, plugins, folder structures, and tag conventions before the tool feels like it is doing anything. Notion, for all its flaws, presents a workable default surface within five minutes of signup. For a reader without strong opinions about software, that difference matters.

Finally, Obsidian's collaboration story is much weaker than Notion's. Vaults can be shared via syncing services, but real-time collaboration in the Notion sense does not exist. For a one-person studio this is not a problem. For anything involving even one other person, it becomes one quickly.

What I changed about my Obsidian setup over the year

I started the year with a heavy Obsidian setup — fifteen plugins, a Dataview-driven dashboard, a custom CSS theme borrowed from a popular Reddit thread. By the third month I had removed the dashboard, because I was opening it ritually and not actually using it. By the sixth month I had pared the plugins down to four. By the end of the year I was running with the default theme and three plugins.

The pattern mirrored what happened in Notion in earlier years and in the dashboard piece I wrote about a quiet Notion dashboard. Removal is the most reliable improvement available to a personal workspace. Each removed thing is a small return. Each added thing is a small tax. The tax compounds; the returns compound. After a year of small choices in this direction, both Notion and Obsidian feel like rooms I want to enter rather than control panels I have to manage.

What I changed about my Notion setup over the year

Less, because Notion was already on the third year of its long subtraction. The largest change was removing the few exploratory notes I had been keeping in Notion. They had never settled comfortably there, and once Obsidian was running in parallel, the right home for them became obvious. The Notion side of the workspace became cleaner the moment its job was narrowed to structured and operational writing only.

How I would advise someone choosing today

If you are choosing between the two and have to pick one, here is the rough rule. If most of your writing is operational or structured — calendars, ledgers, project tracking, anything that benefits from being read across months — pick Notion. If most of your writing is exploratory — notes, marginalia, the slow accumulation of half-thoughts — pick Obsidian. If you genuinely have both kinds of writing in roughly equal amounts, run them both. The combined cost is one Notion free account and one free desktop app, and the tax of running two tools is much smaller than the tax of forcing the wrong tool to do the wrong work.

The question I would not advise spending much time on is which tool is "better." Both are mature. Both will exist in five years. Both have export paths if they do not. The interesting question is which kind of writing you do, and that question is much faster to answer than the comparison reviews suggest.

Letters from readers about the two-tool setup

"Doesn't running two tools double the maintenance?" In my experience, no. Each tool's maintenance burden is roughly proportional to the work it is doing, and splitting the work across two tools meant I stopped trying to bend either of them. Total maintenance went down, not up.

"How do you avoid duplicating notes between them?" The handoff is one-directional. Things move from Obsidian to Notion when they cross the structure threshold, and they do not move back. The Obsidian note remains as source material. The Notion row becomes the work. There is one canonical home for each piece of writing at each stage.

"Do you sync them somehow?" No. Sync would defeat the point. The friction of moving a note from one tool to the other is precisely what makes the handoff meaningful. If the move were automatic, the structure threshold would never be a real decision, and the structure threshold is the most useful thing the two-tool setup gives me.

What this looks like at the desk

On a typical morning I open Obsidian first, with tea. I read what I wrote yesterday, which is usually a paragraph or two of marginalia from whatever book I am working through. I add a few sentences. If a small cluster of notes has reached its three-link density overnight in my head — sometimes it does — I open Notion and add a row to the editorial calendar with a one-sentence pitch. Then I close Notion. The rest of the morning is in Obsidian, or in iA Writer for whichever long piece is currently in flight.

In the afternoon, when the work is more operational, Notion does most of the lifting. The reader inbox gets cleared. The kakeibo ledger gets updated. The monthly reflection, on the first Sunday, gets typed up from the paper version. Obsidian is closed during all of this, because the operational mode and the exploratory mode work badly when they share a screen.

Final thought

A year of running both has taught me that the question is not Notion or Obsidian. The question is whether the writing you are doing right now wants structure or wants room. Once you can tell the difference — and a year of paying attention is the cheapest way to learn it — the choice of tool becomes obvious in each individual moment, and the larger choice between them stops mattering.
