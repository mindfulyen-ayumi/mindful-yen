Notion Templates I Paid For: An Honest Review

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Over the past two years I have paid for seven Notion templates, ranging in price from roughly nine hundred yen to about twelve thousand yen at the high end. I am, by background, an editor. The instinct to evaluate the structure of a published thing is one I have stopped trying to suppress. What follows is an honest accounting of those seven templates — which disappointed me, which earned their price, and the pattern that, after seven purchases, separates the two with what feels like reasonable reliability.

I should say at the top: my own free template, the one linked in the kakeibo Notion template essay, is not on this list because I built it. If you want a starting point that I can vouch for in detail, you can begin there →. The reviews below are about templates I bought, used, and either kept or quietly archived.

The pattern, stated up front

To save anyone reading the rest only for the conclusion: the templates that disappointed me were almost uniformly the ones with the most beautiful preview images. The templates that earned their price were almost uniformly the ones whose preview images were so plain that I hesitated before buying. The correlation is not perfect, but after seven purchases it is striking enough that I have stopped buying any template whose preview is dominated by gradients, large emoji headers, or photographs of laptops in cafés.

The reason, I think, is structural. A beautiful preview rewards visual design effort. A useful template rewards database design effort. The two skills are real, and they are different, and the marketing surface of the Notion template economy reliably rewards the first. Templates that look extraordinary in screenshots are, in my experience, very often hollow. Templates that look almost embarrassingly plain in screenshots are very often the ones built by someone who actually used the workspace before they sold it.

The seven templates, in chronological order

1. The "Aesthetic Life Planner" — disappointed

Bought early in my second year, partly because the preview screenshots were unusually pretty. The template arrived with seventeen pages, four dashboards, more than thirty emoji icons, and a colour palette that used eight of Notion's twelve colours. The first time I opened it on my laptop the page took several seconds to render. I used it for two weeks. I closed it.

What I learned: the cost of a template is not its price. The cost is the ongoing tax of opening it every day. A template that is heavy enough to lag is heavy enough to make me avoid it, and a workspace I avoid is worse than no workspace at all. The principle I now apply, after this purchase, is the same one I described in the quiet dashboard essay: every visual element is volume, and a workspace that is shouting is one I will not return to.

2. The "Ultimate Second Brain" template — disappointed

This was the most expensive purchase on the list. The template was sold as a complete personal knowledge management system based on a popular methodology. It arrived with eleven interlinked databases, a tutorial document of about forty pages, and a set of tags I never managed to keep consistent for more than three weeks. After two months I deleted it and went back to a much smaller setup.

The disappointment here was not the template's quality, which was high. It was the mismatch between the template's complexity and my actual life. A second brain designed for a knowledge worker producing dozens of notes per week is not the right shape for a writer producing a few essays per month. The template was solving a problem I did not have, in a way that imported a great many problems I did not previously have either. I would not now buy any template whose tutorial is longer than its actual setup.

3. A small "weekly review" template — worth it

About four hundred yen, sold by an independent designer through a personal site. The template was a single Notion page with five short prompts and a small archive view. There were no dashboards. There were no images. The entire thing fit on one screen.

I used it as the starting point for the weekly review template I describe in the weekly review essay. Most of what I now use is my own adaptation, but the original gave me the shape of the practice in a way that would have taken me months to arrive at on my own. Worth several times what I paid.

4. A "freelance invoicing" template — disappointed

Bought in the early days of running the studio's finances in Notion. The template included a client database, an invoice generator, and a payments tracker, all interlinked. The structure was technically impressive. In practice, invoicing is a job that benefits from being done in a dedicated invoicing tool, and shoehorning it into Notion meant my invoices took longer to send and looked worse when sent. I now use a simple invoicing service and copy monthly totals into the kakeibo ledger by hand. That arrangement, described briefly in the solo creators essay, is much faster.

5. A "reading tracker" template — disappointed

The template that prompted me to write the reading log essay. It had thirteen properties per book and a dashboard showing reading speed in pages per day. I used it for four months and noticed I was reading less than I had been before I bought it. The tracker had quietly converted reading from a private pleasure into a small performance, and the performance was depressing the underlying activity. I removed it and built the four-property version that has since become the only reading log I have managed to keep.

6. A "quiet writing" template — worth it

Bought late last year from a small studio whose work I had admired for some time. The template was almost embarrassingly minimal: a single page, three sections, one accent colour, the default font. I almost did not buy it because the preview was so plain. After six months of use it has become, in revised form, the structure I use for any new long-form draft. The cost of the template was less than the cost of an hour of editing time it has saved me on a typical piece. Worth it many times over.

7. A "small business CRM" template — partially worth it

This is the only one of the seven I would call a partial success. The template provided a competent CRM structure that I then aggressively pared down — from twelve properties to four — and which became the basis for the minimal CRM I describe in the tiny-studios CRM essay. The base template was over-built for a one-person studio, but the underlying database design was sound. Worth the price, with the caveat that I had to remove most of it before it became useful.

What separates the templates that earned their price

Looking at the three templates I would buy again, the common features are easy to name.

  • Small. All three fit in roughly one page or one small set of pages. None of them tried to be a workspace. Each tried to be a single tool, well-designed.
  • Plain. All three used the default font and either no accent colour or a single muted one. The visual restraint was not a stylistic preference; it was an indicator that the maker had spent their effort on structure rather than surface.
  • Built by someone who used them. All three came with a short note from the maker describing how they used the template themselves, in concrete terms. The disappointing templates almost always came with marketing copy describing what the template could do, in abstract terms. The difference is detectable from the product page, if you read for it.

What separates the templates that disappointed

The disappointing templates also had common features.

  • Many databases. Five or more. The number of databases in a template is roughly inverse to the probability that the maker uses it daily. A maker who actually runs their workspace will, over time, have removed databases that did not earn their place. A maker building a template to sell has the opposite incentive: more databases look like more value.
  • Heavy visual design. Custom emoji icons, photographic banner images, multiple accent colours. The visual layer is what makes the preview attractive and what makes the daily use exhausting.
  • Long tutorials. A template that requires forty pages of explanation is one whose structure is not legible from the structure itself. A good template should be readable in five minutes.

What I would now do before buying any template

Three small checks, which would have saved me roughly half of what I have spent on templates.

First, read the maker's most recent essay or post about how they themselves use the template. If there is no such writing, do not buy. If the writing is in marketing language rather than concrete daily-use language, do not buy. The presence of an honest, specific user-of-the-template essay correlates strongly with the template being worth its price.

Second, count the databases. Three or fewer is a good sign. More than five is a warning. The high-end second-brain templates are exceptions in theory and, in my experience, not exceptions in practice.

Third, look at the preview screenshots and ask: is this the kind of page I want to open every morning? Beautiful pages photograph better than they live. A page that looks slightly underwhelming in a screenshot is often a page you can sit with for years.

What I would not buy again, regardless of the maker

Two categories I have removed from my consideration entirely. The first is "all-in-one life OS" templates, which combine work, finances, reading, fitness, and habits into a single workspace. The premise is unworkable. Different parts of life want different cadences and different tools, and forcing them into one workspace flattens the differences. I have written more about this in the Notion vs Obsidian essay.

The second is any template marketed primarily through screenshots of itself on a laptop in a café. The genre is so saturated and so reliably indicates more visual design effort than database design effort that the screenshot itself has become, for me, a soft signal to move on.

Letters from readers about template purchases

"Should I buy any template at all?" Honestly, only if you have spent a few weeks trying to build the equivalent yourself and have failed in a specific way. Until then, the friction of building from scratch is itself instructive. The free template I link to in the kakeibo essay is more than enough to start with for kakeibo, and the solo creators essay describes the rest of my workspace in enough detail that you can replicate it without paying anyone.

"Do you sell templates?" The Notion template I link to is free. The Mindful Yen Etsy shop sells paper companions — kakeibo journals, weekly review notebooks — but no Notion templates. I have considered selling one, and have so far decided not to, because the templates I would sell are small enough to publish as essays instead.

"What's the most you would ever pay for a Notion template?" Probably around fifteen hundred yen, for something small and clearly built by someone who uses it daily. Anything above that, in my experience, is paying for marketing rather than for design.

Final thought

The Notion template economy is large enough now that the average buyer will, like me, accumulate a handful of disappointing purchases before they learn to read the signals. The signals are visible from the product page, if you know to look. Small, plain, written about honestly by someone who uses it. That is most of the test. Almost everything that fails it will fail in your workspace too.

If you want to start free, the workspace I keep is here. If you want longer letters about the small economics of running a one-person studio, the Mindful Yen Substack is where I write them. The best template, in the end, is usually the one you would have built yourself given a quiet weekend.
