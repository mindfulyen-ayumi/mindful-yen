Restaurant Leftovers in Tokyo: Why Doggy Bags Barely Exist

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

At a small tempura place near Shinjuku-sanchōme last autumn I watched a visiting American couple finish most of a set meal, look at the two remaining shrimp and a small pile of rice, and ask the waiter, politely, if they could take the rest home. The waiter — a man in his fifties, an apron creased from a dozen years of the same gesture — paused for longer than he meant to. He then said, in careful English, that it was not possible. He did not say why. The couple, confused but gracious, apologised and left the shrimp where they were. I finished my own meal two tables away and thought about how to explain the pause.

Japan has mottainai. Japan also throws out roughly five million tonnes of edible food per year, according to the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries' most recent figures. A very visible share of that waste happens in restaurants. The gap between the ethic the country names and the food the country bins is one of the sharpest contradictions in modern Japanese daily life. This essay is an honest look at why Tokyo doggy bags barely exist, what the country does instead, and what the tempura waiter was actually thinking during that pause.

The short answer: food safety law

The most boring part of the answer, and therefore the part usually left out of travel blog pieces, is regulatory. Japanese food hygiene law places responsibility for the safety of prepared food on the establishment that prepared it, and does so with fewer of the "customer assumes risk" carve-outs that exist in, for example, US state codes. If a customer takes food home from a Tokyo restaurant, eats it four hours later in non-refrigerated transit, and is hospitalised with food poisoning, the restaurant is the legally responsible party. This is not a hypothetical. There have been cases. The industry remembers them.

Most Tokyo restaurants, from the chain izakaya to the counter-seat kappō, have therefore developed a default policy of not permitting leftover takeaway. It is rarely written down. It is universally enforced. The waiter at the tempura place was not performing a cultural ritual when he paused; he was running through the mental flowchart his manager had walked him through on his first week, and arriving at no.

The longer answer: omotenashi friction

The regulatory answer is sufficient to explain the rule but not the feeling around the rule. The feeling is about omotenashi — the Japanese practice of anticipatory hospitality, in which the host takes responsibility not only for the guest's immediate satisfaction but for their comfort all the way through the encounter and, in a soft sense, afterward.

Allowing a guest to carry a plastic box of half-finished food through the rain to a train platform violates omotenashi on several dimensions at once. The box is aesthetically wrong — it makes a gift of hospitality into a transaction. The food's quality will degrade before the guest eats it, meaning the host has provided, at a delay, a worse version of themselves. The guest may smell of the food on the train, a small public embarrassment the host has implicitly caused. None of these consequences sit well with a hospitality culture that has spent several centuries refining the opposite instinct.

When you understand this, the tempura waiter's pause becomes legible. He was not judging the American couple. He was calculating which of two failures to commit — a small refusal now, or a larger failure of care later — and choosing the smaller one. The refusal was, in its own strange way, an act of hospitality.

The mottainai problem the refusal creates

This is where the contradiction bites. The two shrimp and the rice went into the bin. The bin was collected at one in the morning. The contents were incinerated, as most Tokyo commercial food waste is. Somewhere in that sequence, mottainai is being violated — not by the customer, who would have honoured the leftovers, and not by the waiter, who was following his training, but by the system that produces both of their behaviours.

Japanese newspapers run an article about this contradiction roughly every eighteen months. Civic campaigns run intermittently. A scheme called mottainai doggy bag was trialled by the Consumer Affairs Agency in 2017, promoting a standardised "customer-risk-acknowledged" takeaway form. It had a modest initial uptake. It is not widely used in 2026. The underlying tension — between regulatory caution and mottainai — has not been resolved. It has been, in the Japanese administrative style, quietly deferred.

What Tokyo did instead: small-dish ordering

The interesting thing, to me, is that Tokyo's restaurant culture responded to the doggy-bag gap not by fixing the doggy-bag gap but by evolving a different structure that reduces the problem upstream. That structure is the small-dish izakaya format.

The izakaya menu — and the related kappō, oden, and yakitori formats — is built around plates the size of a cupped palm. You do not order a main course; you order five to eight small dishes over the course of the evening, arriving in sequence, each calibrated to one or two bites per person. The structure was not designed as a food-waste solution. It emerged from a different logic — sake pacing, social rhythm, kitchen efficiency. But it has the incidental effect of making leftovers rare. You rarely over-order because you order as you go. You rarely leave half a plate because the plates are small enough to finish.

I grew up with this format and did not understand it as a waste-prevention system until I spent a year in the United States and watched American portion sizes produce, reliably, a third-of-a-plate of leftover food at the end of every restaurant meal. The American doggy bag exists because the American portion makes it necessary. The Tokyo izakaya plate rarely produces leftovers at all. The structures are different solutions to the same problem, and the Japanese one, for all its regulatory friction, may be more efficient in aggregate.

The honest edge: convenience stores

I cannot write this essay without mentioning konbini. A Tokyo 7-Eleven throws away, on a typical day, a bin's worth of unsold prepared food — onigiri, bentō, sandwiches, fried chicken. The industry total is several hundred thousand tonnes per year. This waste is not small-dish-format-preventable. It is a direct consequence of a supply system that prioritises full shelves over full stomachs and treats a nearly-expired rice ball as a brand risk rather than a piece of food.

The word mottainai appears, sometimes, on the discount stickers the chains have begun applying in the last three hours before closing. I appreciate the gesture. It is not enough, and the konbini chains know it is not enough. Reforms are slow. A 2021 amendment to Japan's food-loss legislation has begun to apply pressure upstream — at distributors and manufacturers — but the shelf-full-or-nothing logic of Japanese convenience retail has not yet been dismantled, and may not be in my lifetime.

So when I say Tokyo does small-dish ordering well, I want to be specific: the mid-range restaurant sector does it well. The konbini sector does not. The country contains both.

What a careful traveller can do

If you are reading this as a visitor planning a Tokyo trip, the practical shape of a low-waste restaurant week looks something like this.

Eat at izakaya and small-dish places for dinner rather than large-portion Western-style restaurants. Order one or two plates at a time, wait, order again. The waiter is not irritated by this — it is how the format is meant to work. Be honest about your appetite; staff will slow the kitchen to match you.

If you are buying from a konbini, prefer the late-evening discount shelves, which are usually near the register. A sticker with a yen amount in red, applied within the last three hours, is the konbini's small apology for the system. Accepting it is a kindness to the system.

If you do end up with leftovers at a restaurant and the waiter pauses, do not push. The pause is not a negotiation. It is a small refusal performed out of care rather than stinginess. Accept it, eat what you can, and adjust the next evening's order downward.

I have written more about the practical side of Tokyo food shopping and small daily habits in the Mindful Yen Substack, which goes out weekly and is free. For the theory underneath this piece, the essay on mottainai itself is the foundation.

The closing scene

After the American couple left, the waiter at the tempura place quietly cleared their table. He carried the two shrimp and the small pile of rice to the kitchen. I could not see what happened next. I would like to believe he ate them on his break — that the shrimp found their way, belatedly, to the mouth they were made for, and that mottainai was narrowly preserved.

I do not know if he did. Probably not; staff meals in most Tokyo kitchens are a separate kettle, for hygiene reasons. The shrimp probably went into the bin. The contradiction held. The word kept its distance.

That is the honest version. I would rather write the honest version than a country-flattering one. A country that has the word and does not always live it is still worth reading about; it is only the glossy versions that are not.
