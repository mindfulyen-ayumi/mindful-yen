Mottainai in a Marriage: Two Different Instincts

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

We had the conversation on a Sunday in late February, at the low kotatsu table in the living room, after the lunch plates had been cleared and before my husband had to leave for a work thing in the afternoon. He had made the hojicha. I had the kakeibo ledger open in front of me, and he had his own notebook — a soft-cover graph-paper one from Itoya — on his side of the table, turned at a slight angle. We had not planned to have a long talk about money. We had planned to do the monthly reflection together, as we do on the last Sunday of each month, and the reflection had, unexpectedly, turned into something larger.

My husband and I have been married for seven years. We both grew up in households where money was discussed plainly, which is not the most common Japanese experience — a 2022 survey by the Japan Securities Dealers Association found that under 30 percent of Japanese households report regular conversation between spouses about household finances. We are, by that measure, unusually talkative about money. What we discovered, that Sunday, was that talking a lot does not mean agreeing. We had two different mottainai instincts, and we had not named the difference in seven years.

I want to write about that conversation carefully, because the shape of it is, I think, useful to other couples — including couples outside Japan — who are trying to live a careful financial life together and finding, as we did, that "careful" does not mean one thing.

My mottainai: the object

My mottainai, as I have tried to describe in earlier essays, is oriented toward objects. When I look at the half-tin of matcha on the shelf, or the grey wool coat with the thinning cuff, or the book I bought on a Tuesday and have not yet read, the small internal voice that arrives is the voice of the thing itself, asking not to be abandoned. I buy slowly because each purchase is a small relationship I will have to honour. I finish what I have because the finishing is the only way to close the relationship respectfully. This is the posture I have written about in the essay on the word and the one on the wardrobe.

I had assumed, silently, that my husband shared this orientation. We had lived in the same apartment for seven years without a fight about money. He did not over-buy. He did not waste. He kept things for a long time. The outward behaviour looked identical to mine. I had inferred, from identical behaviour, an identical interior.

His mottainai: the time

What came out, that Sunday, was that his mottainai is oriented not toward objects but toward time. He has an almost allergic aversion to the sense that time has been wasted — his own, or anyone else's. He will happily keep a shirt for ten years, not because he feels the shirt has mottai, but because he cannot stand the sense that the twenty minutes he spent buying it in 2016 would be betrayed by treating it as disposable. He will mend something for forty minutes, not because the thing has its own life, but because the hour in the shop where it was made, and the hour in the shop where he chose it, and the thousands of minor human decisions that produced it, have a right not to be disposed of casually. His mottainai is about the labour condensed inside the object. Mine is about the object itself.

This sounds like a small philosophical difference. It is not. It produces measurably different decisions. When we were looking at a kitchen kettle we had used for five years that had begun to whistle more harshly than it used to, I wanted to replace it — the kettle was, in my sense, complete; its small life had reached the end. He wanted to repair it — the hours of labour inside it, across its supply chain, had not been honoured yet. We had the same word in our heads and the word was pulling us in two different directions.

The discovery, and the small relief of it

What the Sunday conversation produced, over perhaps ninety minutes, was not a resolution. It was a naming. We now had language for a difference that had been quietly present for seven years — he would feel a low-grade disappointment when I discarded something that was still materially sound; I would feel a low-grade resistance when he wanted to repair something I had already internally let go of. Neither of us had been able to say, before that Sunday, why the disappointment or the resistance was arriving. The feelings had been too small to surface on their own. They had simply accumulated.

The relief, when the language arrived, was real. It turns out a great deal of quiet marital friction is not about disagreement but about shared vocabulary that masks unshared meaning. We had been using the same word for two different instincts, and the instincts had been pulling at each other underneath the flat surface of our agreement.

Why this matters beyond our kitchen

I have come to think that most couples who have been together for more than a few years have a version of this. They have shared language about money — "we are careful," "we do not waste," "we save" — that conceals two different underlying architectures. Sometimes the architectures are compatible. Sometimes they are quietly at war.

Western personal finance writing, when it addresses couples at all, tends to focus on the visible level: joint accounts versus separate, who pays for what, the power dynamics of one higher-earning partner. These are real questions. They are also, I suspect, almost never the actual source of a couple's money tension. The actual source is usually two different emotional architectures, both invisible to their owners, producing friction whose cause neither partner can name.

The Japanese household, in its conventional form, often papers over this problem by assigning household finance entirely to one partner — historically the wife — who keeps the kakeibo and runs the household money with quiet authority. This works, socially, as long as the two partners have genuinely similar architectures, or as long as the non-ledger partner is happy to cede authority entirely. When the two architectures actually differ, though, and one partner is simply managing alone around the other's unexamined instincts, the gap becomes the place where resentment settles. We were not in that situation, but I know several couples who were, and the kakeibo could not rescue them because the problem was upstream of the ledger.

How we have started to hold both

Since that Sunday — it is now four months ago — we have done two small things.

The first is that we have started to name which mottainai is showing up, when we notice friction. When I suggest discarding something, my husband will sometimes ask, softly, "time-mottainai or object-mottainai?" and I will pause and answer. Usually I realise that what I am feeling is object-mottainai and I have reached its limit, and he is feeling time-mottainai and has not. Naming the two does not resolve the disagreement. It does dissolve the frustration that surrounds it. We are no longer confused about why we disagree. We are just disagreeing, clearly, about a thing we now understand.

The second is that we have started to divide small decisions according to whose mottainai is more actively engaged. Decisions about the wardrobe, the kitchen, the daily fabric of the apartment — those tend to come to me, because object-mottainai is more active there. Decisions about how to spend weekends, how to structure work hours, whether to travel or stay — those tend to come to him, because time-mottainai is more active there. This is not a rigid division. It is a slight tilt in whose instinct leads, and it has reduced the small daily negotiations to something much quieter.

What we have not solved

I should be honest about what is still open. Several decisions genuinely straddle both architectures and produce real, ongoing tension. The kettle was one. A used desk from a second-hand shop in Nakano is another; he wants to repair its slightly wobbly leg because the desk's labour deserves honouring, and I want to return it to the shop because the desk's life, as far as I am concerned, was already complete before it reached us. These small standoffs continue, and they will continue. Naming the architectures did not abolish them. It just moved them from under-the-surface friction into the open.

I am not sure, finally, whether this is a solvable problem. It may be that two long-married people simply accumulate a set of small, named, unresolved tensions, and that the work of the marriage is not to resolve them but to keep them named and keep them civil. That is what we are trying to do with these two mottainai instincts. I do not know if it is enough. I know it is more honest than what we had before.

Final thought

If you have a partner, and if you live with the quiet sense that you "both agree about money" but have occasional small disagreements whose origin you cannot quite trace, I would offer this. Sit down at a low table on a Sunday afternoon. Make tea. Open whatever money document you keep. And ask each other: when you feel the small pull of do not waste this, what exactly is it that you do not want to waste? The object? The labour? The time? The possibility?

You may find, as we did, that you have been using one word for two things. You may find that naming the two makes a large number of small frictions explicable. You will not solve anything that afternoon. You may, quietly, have moved into a slightly more spacious marriage by the time the hojicha has gone cold.
