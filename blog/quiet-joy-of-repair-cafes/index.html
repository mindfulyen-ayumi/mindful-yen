The Quiet Joy of Repair Cafés: A Saturday in Tokyo

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The lamp had been sitting on the floor of my hallway for four months. It was a brass desk lamp with a green enamel shade, bought second-hand in Jimbōchō maybe eight years ago, and one Tuesday evening in February it had simply stopped working — the bulb still good, the cord intact, but the switch producing nothing when I pressed it. I had put the lamp in the hall intending to deal with it, and then I had walked past it roughly five hundred times.

On a Saturday in late April I finally carried it, wrapped in an old tenugui, to a small repair café held once a month in the community room of a ward office three stops from my apartment. I paid nothing to enter. I was handed a number written in pencil on the back of a used envelope, offered a cup of hōjicha from a thermos, and asked to sit on one of six folding chairs near the window while I waited for a volunteer to be free.

What a Japanese repair café actually is

The repair café movement began in Amsterdam in 2009, when the journalist Martine Postma organised the first one as a response to what she felt was the absurdity of a European consumer economy in which a kettle cost less to replace than to diagnose. The model has since spread to something like 2,500 locations worldwide, and it arrived quietly in Japan in the mid-2010s, where it found a ready audience. Japanese households already have a long, living relationship with mottainai — the ethic against waste — and with the smaller, less moralised idea of setsuyaku, thoughtful frugality. Repair cafés did not introduce anything new. They gave a name and a room to something grandmothers had always done in the kitchen.

The one I go to is run by three retired engineers and a woman in her sixties who was, in her working life, a textile repair specialist at a department store. They bring their own tool rolls. The ward office provides the room and the thermos. Attendees bring broken things — kettles, rice cookers, radios, bicycle lights, a sewing machine once, a hair dryer that smelled faintly of burnt cotton. Nothing is promised. The volunteers will look, diagnose aloud, and either fix the thing in front of you or explain, gently, why it cannot be fixed by them that afternoon.

The hour with the lamp

My number came up after about forty minutes. The engineer who took my lamp was in his early seventies, wearing a navy apron over a grey work shirt, and his hands were unhurried in a way I have come to associate with people who have fixed things professionally for a long time. He asked when it had stopped working. He asked whether anything had happened — had I dropped it, had the cord been stepped on, had there been a storm. He turned the lamp over and looked at the underside of the base the way a doctor looks at a chart.

The switch, he said eventually, was almost certainly the problem. These old brass lamps used a rotary switch with a small internal contact that oxidised over decades. He could open it. He would need twenty minutes. I could wait, or come back.

I waited. He worked at the long table with a cloth laid beneath his tools and a small jeweller's loupe on a stand. Three other volunteers were working at their own stations. The woman who had been a textile repair specialist was stitching a hem on a child's winter coat at the table behind me, and her seam was invisible even from close up. The room made almost no sound. A kettle boiled somewhere. A child asked a question and was answered softly.

When the engineer called me back he had the lamp reassembled. He showed me the old contact piece, a small corroded disc the size of a lentil, and the new one he had cut from a sheet of brass shim stock using a pair of snips. He turned the switch. The bulb came on with the yellow, low warmth I remembered. He refused any payment. I put a thousand yen in the donation jar on the way out, which is the rough convention.

Why this is a wabi-sabi practice, not a nostalgia one

It would be easy to read repair cafés as sentimental — a charming rebellion against modernity, old men with screwdrivers, tea in a thermos. That reading would miss the point. The wabi-sabi tradition, as Leonard Koren describes it in his 1994 book and as Andrew Juniper develops it more philosophically, is not about preserving old things because they are old. It is about recognising that things bear the marks of time and use, and that those marks are part of what the thing is. A repaired lamp is not a lamp restored to its original state. It is a new thing — a lamp that has been broken once, and whose history now includes the hour it spent on a Saturday afternoon in a ward office with a retired engineer.

The philosopher Crispin Sartwell, writing on everyday aesthetics, argues that the objects we live with accumulate a kind of weathered meaning that factory-new objects cannot have. My lamp, with its new contact and its forty-year-old brass, is more itself now than it was before it broke. The repair is part of its grain.

This is also close to the Sōtō Zen idea of mujō — impermanence not as tragedy but as the condition in which things become beautiful. A thing that cannot break cannot be mended, and a thing that cannot be mended cannot have a story. The lamp's Tuesday-night failure was, in a small way, what made Saturday possible.

The economics, quietly

I am wary of moralising frugality, but the numbers are worth naming. A comparable brass desk lamp, new, would have cost me somewhere between 18,000 and 35,000 yen depending on the shop. A cheap replacement from a large electronics retailer would have cost 4,000 yen and would have looked, and felt, like nothing. The repair cost me a thousand yen in the donation jar and about three hours of a Saturday, most of which I spent drinking hōjicha and watching a woman stitch a hem.

The slow-money framing I have written about in the kakeibo essays applies here. Money is not only spent in the moment of purchase. It is spent in the long tail — in the disposal of the thing you replaced, in the slow accumulation of furniture you do not quite love, in the small erosion of the feeling that the objects in your home have anything to do with you. Repair interrupts this.

Finding one near you, wherever you are

Repair cafés are easier to find than most people assume. The global directory at repaircafe.org lists active locations in roughly forty countries. In Japan, the shūzen kafe (修繕カフェ) network is loosely coordinated and most events are posted on ward office websites, neighbourhood bulletin boards, and occasionally on local LINE groups. In the US and UK, public libraries increasingly host monthly repair sessions; in Germany and the Netherlands, they are effectively civic institutions.

If there is nothing nearby, the adjacent practice is almost as good: find the one older neighbour, relative, or local small-shop owner in your life who knows how to fix things, and bring them a broken object. The transaction is similar. The thing you are paying for, in money or in time or in attention, is not the repair. It is the permission to take the object seriously.

What the lamp is doing now

The lamp is back on the low table by the window where it used to live. It casts the same yellow light on the same stack of books it was casting in January. The only visible difference is a small scratch on the underside of the base where the engineer rested it against the corner of the table; you cannot see it unless you pick the lamp up, and I am the only person who ever picks the lamp up.

But I know the scratch is there. And when the lamp eventually fails again — because it will, because mujō is the condition of all things with moving parts — I will know where to take it. That knowledge is a small form of security that a new lamp, bought next-day from a warehouse and delivered in a box of recyclable filler, does not offer.

The quiet joy of the repair café is not the repair itself. It is the slow reminder that the objects in your life have insides, that the insides can be looked at, and that there are people within a train ride of you who know how to look.
