Wabi-Sabi and Aging: A 35-Year-Old Reflection

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I found my first grey hair on a Tuesday morning in March, in the bathroom mirror, while I was tying my hair back for the day. It was at the right temple, fine, almost translucent rather than properly white, and I noticed it because the morning light from the small frosted window was hitting the mirror at an angle that made every strand visible at once. I held the hair between my thumb and finger for perhaps ten seconds. Then I let it go. I did not pull it out. I tied my hair back, brushed my teeth, and went to make coffee. The hair is still there, somewhere, growing in among the others, three months later.

This is an essay about turning thirty-five, about the first small visible weathering of a body, and about what the wabi-sabi tradition has to say to a woman who has been told, by every magazine she has ever read, that ageing is a thing to be fought.

What I expected, and what arrived

I expected, in the abstract, to mind the grey hair. I had spent enough of my twenties absorbing the standard messaging — anti-ageing creams, retinol routines, the careful policing of forehead lines — that I assumed the first physical evidence of mid-life would arrive with a small wave of grief. It did not. What arrived was something quieter, harder to name, and considerably more interesting. Curiosity, mostly. A small wave of recognition, as if I had been expecting to meet this hair for a long time and was now meeting it. And, somewhere underneath, a kind of relief — the relief of an actual fact replacing a long-imagined dread.

The dread of ageing, in my experience, is much worse than the ageing. The grey hair is one hair. The face I saw in the mirror that morning was, by any honest measure, the same face I had seen the morning before. Nothing collapsed. The day proceeded.

The wabi-sabi reading of a weathering body

The wabi-sabi sensibility was, from its beginnings, partly a response to ageing. Sen no Rikyū's tea practice in the sixteenth century deliberately preferred the cracked tea bowl, the irregular bamboo whisk, the iron kettle that had darkened with use. The aesthetic was an answer to a particular Buddhist question: if everything is impermanent — mujō — and decay is inevitable, what does beauty look like inside that recognition rather than against it?

The wabi-sabi answer is that beauty is precisely the visibility of time. A young face is beautiful in one way; a face that has lived for fifty years and shows it is beautiful in another way, and the second is not a degraded version of the first. It is a different category. The cherry blossom and the cherry tree are not in competition. The blossom is loved partly because it falls; the tree is loved partly because it does not.

Andrew Juniper, in his book on the tradition, makes the point that wabi-sabi is the only aesthetic in human history that has actively preferred the older object. Every other tradition I know of — Greek classicism, Renaissance ideals, contemporary advertising — has located beauty in youth and treated ageing as decline. The Japanese tradition, alone, treats ageing as the thing that finishes the object. A new bowl is unfinished. A bowl that has been used for a hundred years is complete.

I am not a bowl. The metaphor only travels so far. But the underlying instinct — that visible time can be a form of beauty rather than a subtraction from it — has been quietly useful to me in my thirty-fifth year.

My mother's hands

My mother is sixty-eight. She lives in the prefecture I grew up in, in a small house with a garden, and she still does most of her own cooking, gardening, and laundry by hand. Her hands show all of it. The skin is thinner now than it was when I was a child; the veins on the back are more visible; the joints of her index fingers have begun to thicken slightly with the early arthritis that runs in our family. There are two small brown age spots near her left wrist that have appeared in the past few years. The nails are short, unpolished, and very clean.

I notice her hands more now than I used to. When I was young they were simply hands — the hands that braided my hair, the hands that wrote my school lunches. In my twenties they were the hands of a parent I loved and did not particularly look at. In my thirties, with my own hands beginning to show the first faint changes I associate with hers, I have begun to look. They are beautiful. I do not mean this sentimentally. I mean that the specific composition of her hands — the marks of sixty-eight years of work, weather, garden, kitchen, child — produces a kind of quiet visual density that her hands at thirty-five did not have. There is more to look at. There is more to read.

This is the wabi-sabi reading of the body, made local. It is not abstract philosophy. It is what my mother's hands look like in afternoon light when she is folding laundry on the kitchen table.

What I have stopped doing

Since the grey hair, several small habits have fallen away. I do not say I have eliminated them; they have simply quieted. I share them not as a programme but as data.

I have stopped reading magazine pieces about anti-ageing. The genre, looked at directly, is mostly the same article rewritten — a list of products, a tone of low-level alarm, an implicit suggestion that you are running out of time. None of this is information. I read other things now.

I have stopped using the small magnifying mirror I bought in my late twenties. It was sold to me as a tool for careful skin care. It was also a tool for noticing every pore at five times the size anyone else would ever see them. I gave it away. The regular bathroom mirror, at the regular distance, shows me the face I actually have.

I have stopped photographing myself in flattering light only. The small daily-photo habit I kept on my phone, in which I unconsciously selected the best of three or four shots, has been replaced by no photo at all on most days, and a single unselected shot on the days I do take one. The composite face that the flattering selections produced was a kind of slow self-deception. The single shot is more honest, and oddly more reassuring, because it sets a baseline that does not have to be defended.

I have not stopped looking after my skin. I wash my face, use sunscreen, sleep enough, drink water. The basic care is unchanged. What has gone is the project of resisting, which was always the more exhausting layer.

The thirty-five-year mark, considered honestly

Thirty-five is an interesting age in Japan. It is the age at which the cultural script begins to acknowledge that you are no longer young in the conventional sense — the marriageable-age conversations have receded, the career has either consolidated or has not, the body has begun to give the small early signals of its later self. There is a softness in how older Japanese women treat thirty-five. It is the age at which you are quietly admitted into a different room.

I have noticed, in the past year, that the women I most want to be like as I age are not the ones who look the youngest for their years. They are the ones who look unmistakably their age and are wholly at ease in it. The ease is the thing. It is not produced by a skincare regime or by acceptance speeches written for oneself in the mirror. It seems to be produced, in the women I have watched closely, by a combination of long good sleep, daily walks, real friendships, and a quiet refusal to be drawn into the ageing-as-emergency narrative. The skin is a side effect.

I think also of the Japanese pottery tradition called kintsugi — the practice of mending broken ceramics with gold-dusted lacquer, so that the breaks are not concealed but emphasised. Kintsugi is often invoked in English-language self-help in a sentimental register, but the actual practice is more austere than the sentimental version. The mended bowl is not pretending the break did not happen. It is also not making a tragedy of it. The break is simply part of what the bowl now is, treated with care, treated with gold rather than glue, allowed to be visible. A face at thirty-five, fifty, seventy is the same kind of object. The lines are the gold.

What I would tell my younger self

Not very much, actually. The dread of ageing in my twenties was real, and there is no version of advice that would have removed it. Telling a twenty-six-year-old that the first grey hair will not be terrible is exactly as useful as telling a twenty-six-year-old that they are pretty. The information does not land.

What I would do instead, if I could, is hand my younger self the cup that sits on my desk now — the small uneven Mashiko ceramic cup with the dripped glaze and the hairline crack — and let her hold it for a minute. The cup is more beautiful than any new cup I could buy this afternoon. It is more beautiful for specific reasons that have to do with the eleven years it has been used, the small mark of the kiln, the way the glaze pooled. The cup does not need to be told it is beautiful. It does not have to defend itself.

That cup is, in my mind, the closest thing to a working model of how to age well. Not as a metaphor I think about. As an object I look at every morning while I drink tea. I have written elsewhere about why one such object on a desk is enough, and the same principle holds here. The model does not have to be argued. It has to be in the room.

A note on the small things

I keep a small section of my Mindful Yen shelf for printable cards — single-page reminders of small ideas like the ones in this essay — and the wabi-sabi card I use most often is one with the line from Sōtō Zen that "the body is borrowed; treat it well." I do not know if I treat it especially well. I do try, in my small daily way, not to treat it as an enemy.

I write more about these slow domestic shifts in my Substack, and the weekly letter is often the place I work out, by writing them down, the small recognitions that arrive in the bathroom mirror or at my mother's kitchen table.

The grey hair is still there. I expect, by next March, there will be two. Then ten. Then a small streak. Then, eventually, a head of hair the colour of my mother's, which is the colour of her mother's. The lineage is part of what I look forward to.
