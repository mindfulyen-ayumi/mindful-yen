Wabi-Sabi Photography: Why I Stopped Editing My Phone Pictures

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

There is a photograph on my phone, taken last Sunday morning at 6:42, of a single wet hydrangea leaf in the narrow strip of garden behind my apartment. The leaf is dark green, still beaded from overnight rain, and the light behind it is the particular flat grey of a Tokyo June sky before the sun burns through. The photo is not sharp. The focus sits on the wrong vein. A small piece of moss at the edge of the frame is blurred in a way a better photographer would not have allowed. I have not edited it. I will not edit it. It is one of the best pictures I have taken in two years.

For about five years before this one, I edited every phone picture I took. Not heavily — I was not chasing the oversaturated Instagram look of 2017 — but thoroughly. A little warmth, a little contrast, a small drop in the highlights, sometimes a crop. I used one of the common editing apps. I thought of myself as restrained. I was wrong about that, and this essay is about what I was actually doing, why I stopped, and what a wabi-sabi approach to phone photography looks like now.

What I was doing when I edited

The first thing I had to admit, when I finally stopped, was that the editing had not been about the photograph. It had been about me. Each small adjustment was a quiet correction of reality toward a version I thought would reflect better on the person who had taken the picture. Warmer light than the morning actually gave me. More contrast than the flat sky had. A crop that hid the air-conditioning unit next door. In aggregate, across years and several thousand photos, I had been building a slow archive of a life that did not quite exist.

This is not, I should say, a moral failing specific to me. The tools are designed for exactly this work. The default editing sliders in most phone apps push an image toward a statistical average of what the platform has learned people like. When I moved my highlights slider by fifteen percent, I was not expressing taste; I was agreeing with a consensus I had not consciously chosen.

What finally stopped me was not an ethical realisation. It was a practical one. I was looking at old photos on a train one afternoon and I could not, in several cases, remember whether the morning had actually looked like the picture, or whether I had made it look like that in the fifteen seconds after I took it. The edited photo had overwritten the memory. This felt, in a way I found hard to shake, like a small theft.

The wabi-sabi position on photography

Wabi-sabi, in the careful sense used by Leonard Koren and Andrew Juniper rather than the cliché of "perfectly imperfect," is an aesthetic tradition that values the weathered, the natural, the incomplete, and the honestly particular. It is rooted in the Sōtō Zen recognition of mujō — impermanence — and in the tea-ceremony lineage of Sen no Rikyū, who famously preferred a single asymmetrical wildflower in a rough bamboo vase to a formal arrangement in a perfect porcelain one.

Applied to photography, this is not a style. It is a refusal. A wabi-sabi photograph does not try to perfect the moment it records. It tries to get out of the moment's way. The grain in the shadows, the slightly wrong focus, the thing in the corner you did not notice until later — these are not flaws to be removed. They are what distinguishes the photograph from the consensus image the editing app would have produced.

Crispin Sartwell, in his book Six Names of Beauty, makes a related argument: beauty in the everyday sense is not a property of the object but of our relationship to it, and that relationship is damaged by polish. A photograph over-corrected toward a platform aesthetic tells you less about the morning than a photograph left alone.

What changed when I stopped

The first thing I noticed, in the weeks after I deleted the editing app, was that I was taking fewer pictures. This was not because I had become more precious. It was because a meaningful share of the photos I had been taking were photos I knew, at the moment of shutter, I would have to edit before they were shareable. Remove that step and those photos simply did not get taken.

What did get taken were photos I wanted to look at — and only those. A cup of cold sencha in the afternoon light. The patch of moss on the stone step outside my door, which has grown and shrunk through three seasons. The back of my grandmother's hand when she visited in March, holding a book. These photos were often flawed in small ways. The light was rarely flattering. The framing was imperfect. But when I looked at them later, they were the morning, and nothing had been stolen.

The second thing I noticed was that I began to see differently when I was not holding the phone. Because I was no longer pre-composing every moment as a potential edited photograph, my attention loosened. I looked at things longer without deciding whether they were shareable. Some mornings I took no photos at all, and these were often the best mornings. This is related to what I have written about the wabi-sabi home office — the idea that a room, or a morning, is doing quiet work on you, and that the attempt to capture all of it is a form of greed.

A working practice, for anyone who wants to try

If you want to move your own phone photography in this direction, the shape that has worked for me is small and deliberately unimpressive.

First, decide which app you will open. On my phone the camera app is on the home screen. The editing app is not installed. There is no decision to make about whether to edit because the tool is not there. This matters more than willpower does; I know this from budgeting and I trust it here.

Second, take photos of the thing, not of the composition. I have stopped walking around a subject to find the flattering angle. I stand where I was standing and take the picture from there. Sen no Rikyū, asked why he had not rearranged a flower before his guest arrived, is said to have answered that the flower had arranged itself; his job was to notice. The wet hydrangea leaf last Sunday was already where it was. I did not crouch. I did not reframe. I took one picture, looked at it once, and kept walking.

Third, do not post most of them. This is the hardest part. The photos I love most from the last two years are not on any platform. They sit in an album on my phone called mornings, which now contains about four hundred quiet images that mean almost nothing to anyone else and a great deal to me. A photograph does not need an audience to have value. It only needs to have been taken honestly.

For readers who would like the same patience extended to the physical objects in a photograph — the cups, the linen, the small weathered things that give a frame its texture — I keep a few of my own favourites listed on Etsy, alongside the kakeibo journals.

On the "honesty" of phone photography

One objection readers sometimes raise is that a phone photograph is never truly honest — the phone has already made dozens of decisions about exposure, white balance, noise reduction, and computational sharpening before the image reaches me. This is correct. It does not change my position.

The relevant honesty is not absolute. It is relational. The photograph I take and leave alone is more honest than the photograph I take and then push toward an imagined audience's taste. The camera's choices are not mine to make; my choices are the ones I can examine. Declining to make the editing choice is, in the small way available to me, a way of staying in the morning the photograph belongs to.

This is also, incidentally, why I prefer phones to real cameras for this practice. A proper camera invites you to treat photography as a craft. A phone does not. It invites you to treat photography as a small daily noticing. The wabi-sabi tradition has always been more interested in noticing than in craft.

What the garden looks like this morning

I am writing this at the same desk I wrote the home-office essay at, with the same cup, and the hydrangea from Sunday is now fully open, lavender-grey at the edges, drooping slightly where the stem has given up some water. I have not photographed it today. Maybe tomorrow. Maybe not. The garden does not care.

What a wabi-sabi photography practice gives you, in the end, is not better photos. It gives you more morning. The trade is small, and it accumulates slowly, and it is difficult to describe to anyone still inside the editing habit. But if you try the no-filter, no-edit approach for a month — one app deleted, one small album labelled mornings, no social pressure — I think you will find, as I did, that the mornings become noticeably more present. The honest picture and the remembered morning will begin to be the same thing again.
