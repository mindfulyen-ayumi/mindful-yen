The Ikigai Conversation I Have With Myself Every January

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Each January, usually on the second or third of the month, when the New Year crowds at the shrines have thinned and Tokyo is still quiet in the way it only briefly is, I sit down with a pot of tea and have a short conversation with myself. It takes about forty minutes. It is not a ritual I advertise. It is not, strictly speaking, a journalling practice — most years it is three lines of writing and a lot of sitting. I have kept it for nine years running now, and it is the single most useful thing I do in the calendar.

I want to describe it here because readers occasionally ask what my own annual practice looks like, and because I suspect the form of it — short, unambitious, three questions, no resolutions — might be useful to others. I want to keep this essay itself short, in the spirit of the practice. If you are looking for a robust goal-setting framework, this is not that. If you are looking for something smaller, read on.

Why I stopped making resolutions

I made resolutions every January through my early twenties. Some of them worked; most of them did not. The ones that failed failed in a particular way: they were ambitious about the coming year without being honest about the year that had just ended. I would resolve to exercise three times a week, without first asking why I had not exercised three times a week last year. I would resolve to write every morning without first noticing that last year's writing had been happening reliably on Tuesday evenings and had stopped when I tried to move it to mornings.

The failure, in hindsight, was always the same. I was designing the coming year for a version of myself that did not exist, using data from no year in particular. The Japanese approach I eventually drifted toward — and which shows up clearly in Mieko Kamiya's 1966 work on ikigai — starts from a different question. Not who would I like to be, but what, looking carefully at the year that just happened, was actually carrying the weight of the days. Resolutions work forward from an aspiration. The January conversation works backward from evidence. The second is, I have found, significantly more honest and significantly more useful.

The three questions

Here they are. They have barely changed in nine years.

One. What, in the past year, was my ikigai, whether I had named it or not?

Note the phrasing. Not what was I pursuing. Not what were my goals. The question asks what was actually there — what small or large thing was doing the quiet work of making the days worth their weight. Some years the answer is a piece of work. Some years it is a person. One year it was, embarrassingly and truthfully, a particular café and the two hours I spent there most Saturday mornings. The answer is allowed to be small. If I find myself trying to dress it up, I know I am being dishonest.

Two. What did I spend the most energy on that was not my ikigai?

This is the harder question, and the one I sit with the longest. The answer is usually not dramatic — I am rarely spending huge amounts of energy on obviously wrong things — but it is almost always instructive. The gap between where my energy went and where my ikigai actually was is the single most important fact about my year, and most years the gap is larger than I expected.

Three. Given that gap, what is one small structural change I could make for the coming year?

Note the singular. One change. Not a list of five resolutions. One structural shift, chosen because it narrows the gap between question one and question two. Some years the change is tiny — move the writing from mornings back to Tuesday evenings. Some years it is larger — reduce editing hours by twenty percent. The size does not matter. The fit to the two preceding answers does.

Why one change, not five

A note on the singular, because readers push back on it. Why not five changes? Why not a full plan?

The honest answer is that I have tried full plans and they do not survive March. One change, chosen carefully and tied directly to the evidence of the preceding year, has a much higher survival rate. Ken Mogi, in his writing on Japanese approaches to daily practice, emphasises this pillar of starting small — and although he is writing about daily habits rather than annual reviews, the principle generalises. A small intervention, well chosen, does more than a large intervention poorly chosen. The annual practice is not an optimisation exercise. It is a steering correction, and steering corrections are small by design.

If the one change goes well by March, I can add a second. Most years I do not. One change is almost always enough, because the change I choose is almost always more consequential than it looks, because it is tied to the real arithmetic of the preceding year rather than to an aspiration.

What the conversation usually looks like, in practice

To give you a concrete shape, here is roughly what the January conversation sounded like in my own head last year, because it is the most recent and the best-remembered.

Question one. What was my ikigai last year, whether I had named it or not? The honest answer, after sitting with it for twenty minutes, was: the morning writing hour and the weekly phone call with my sister. Not the book I was editing. Not the essays I was publishing. Not, embarrassingly, even the long-form writing I was supposedly building a career around. The hour and the call. That was it.

Question two. What had I spent the most non-ikigai energy on? A particular consulting project that had paid well and drained disproportionately. I knew this as I answered. I had known it, in fact, since about August. The January conversation only surfaced what I had been refusing to look at.

Question three. One structural change. I decided, in that session, that I would not renew the consulting project when the contract ended in the spring. That was the whole change. No grand reinvention. One no, delivered in April, to a client who took it graciously. That single change, traceable directly to a forty-minute conversation in January, was the shape of the following year.

Not every year produces such a clean outcome. Some years the change is nearly invisible from the outside — a small rearrangement of the week, a specific friend I decide to see more regularly, a commitment I decline to renew. What is consistent is the scale of the change (small) and the grounding of the change (in honest evidence from the year that just ended).

How to have this conversation yourself

The instruction is brief. Find forty minutes. Make tea, or something warm that is not a task. Sit somewhere quiet. Ask the three questions in order. Answer each honestly. Do not attempt to write an essay; a few sentences per question is plenty. Do not try to be impressive. The practice is for you and one future reader of yours, next January.

When you are done, archive the answers somewhere you can find them next year. I keep mine in a folder labelled, unimaginatively, January conversations. Reading the prior year's answers before writing the current year's is, I have found, where most of the value compounds. You can see, over three or four years, what has been quietly recurring and what has been shifting. Nothing short of the annual sequence reveals this shape.

If you would like a slightly longer version of this same spirit — one that works over a full month rather than a single afternoon — the thirty-day short-prompt sequence I wrote for people who hate journalling is a natural companion. And for readers working through the slower, multi-year version of the same arithmetic, my essay on career pivots extends the logic across three years rather than one.

Final thought

Resolutions are the genre of January most of us were handed. They ask us to design a better self for the year ahead, using almost no information about the year just past. The three-question conversation is much smaller. It asks what was actually there, what was actually in the way, and what one small thing we could adjust. It is less dramatic than a resolution list and, in my experience, enormously more effective. I will sit with tea again next January, and I suspect the change I choose then will surprise me in the way these changes usually do — by being smaller and more consequential than I would have predicted.
