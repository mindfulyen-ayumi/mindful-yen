The Okinawan Origins of Ikigai: What's Embellished, What's Real

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The most-quoted English-language source on ikigai is, by some distance, Dan Buettner's reporting on Okinawa as one of the world's "Blue Zones" — regions said to produce unusually high concentrations of centenarians. The reporting has been syndicated in National Geographic, expanded into books, adapted into a Netflix series, and translated into a small commercial industry of branded retreats, cookbooks, and supplements. By 2024, the word ikigai had been firmly attached, in the English-speaking imagination, to the proposition that Okinawan elders live longer because they have one.

I want to write carefully here, because the Buettner story is partly true, partly embellished, and recently subject to serious challenge. I do not want to be one of the writers who pretends, for a clean argument, that a complicated thing is simple. The Okinawan piece of the ikigai conversation deserves a closer look than it has usually received, and the look is more interesting than either the credulous version or the cynical one.

What the Blue Zones thesis actually claims

The Blue Zones thesis, in its strongest form, claims that five regions of the world — Sardinia, Okinawa, Nicoya in Costa Rica, Ikaria in Greece, and Loma Linda in California — produce centenarians at rates substantially above global averages, and that this longevity can be attributed to a small number of shared lifestyle factors. In Buettner's framing, those factors include plant-leaning diets, regular low-intensity movement, strong social networks, moderate alcohol use, and a sense of purpose. The Okinawan version of the purpose factor is, in Buettner's books, identified as ikigai.

Two specific Okinawan practices have become particularly famous. The first is moai (模合) — informal mutual-support groups, usually formed in childhood and maintained for decades, in which members pool small amounts of money and meet regularly for company and aid. The second is hara hachi bu (腹八分目) — the Confucian-derived eating maxim of stopping when one is roughly eighty percent full, often attributed to Okinawan elders specifically. Both are real. Both predate Buettner. Both have been somewhat selectively reported.

Where the embellishment begins

The first thing worth saying is that the Okinawan longevity statistics themselves have come under serious recent scrutiny. The Australian researcher Saul Newman published a working paper in 2024 — widely reported, including in The Conversation and Nature — arguing that Blue Zone longevity claims, including Okinawa's, are substantially explained by data quality problems: missing birth records from the early twentieth century, pension fraud, age exaggeration, and the chaos that war and post-war reconstruction left in vital registration systems. Newman's analysis suggests that several Blue Zones may not be unusually long-lived at all when the underlying records are corrected.

I am not in a position to adjudicate the dispute. The point, for the purposes of this essay, is that the foundational claim — that Okinawans live unusually long because they have ikigai — rests on a longevity statistic that is, at the moment, less secure than the popular literature suggests. A reader encountering Buettner's framing for the first time is rarely told that the underlying numbers are contested. They should be told.

The second thing worth saying is that even granting the longevity claims, the causal arrow Buettner draws between ikigai and lifespan is much thinner than the popular retelling implies. Okinawan elders, when interviewed, do use the word ikigai. They also live in a particular climate, eat a particular diet, exist within a particular social fabric, descend from a particular gene pool, and have, at least historically, had a particular relationship to physical labour through their seventies and eighties. Singling out one cultural element from this dense matrix and presenting it as the operative factor is the kind of move that makes for a good magazine article and a poor inference. The honest conclusion from the Okinawan data is that something is going on; the specific role of ikigai, separated from the dozen other variables it is woven through, is not actually known.

What is real, even if the numbers are contested

It is tempting, having raised the methodological problems, to throw the whole thing out. I do not think that is the right move either.

The moai, for instance, are unambiguously real, and they do something that decades of social-isolation research suggests is genuinely protective: they keep elderly people in regular, low-stakes, durable contact with a small group of others through their entire lives. Whether this adds three years or thirty to a lifespan, it is a structural feature of Okinawan elder life that any serious account of human ageing would want to take seriously. The moai are not a wellness brand. They are a small, sustained mutual-aid arrangement, often started when the members were children, kept going through decades of marriages and bereavements and economic shifts. Most contemporary societies do not produce anything like them, and the absence is felt even where the longevity statistics are contested.

Hara hachi bu is similarly real and similarly worth taking seriously, though the framing in the Western literature has, in my reading, romanticised it. The actual practice is closer to the Confucian discipline of moderate eating than to the Instagram-friendly idea of mindful eating it has been recoded as. It is a habit of restraint built into a culture, not a hack one adopts for a month.

The use of the word ikigai by Okinawan elders is also real. What I would gently note — and what I have written about elsewhere in more detail — is that the Okinawan use of the word is, in actual interviews, very small. The elders Buettner spoke with described their ikigai as things like teaching karate to local children, tending a vegetable garden, going to the community centre on Tuesday mornings. These are not the four-circle Marc Winn answers. They are the same kind of small daily reasons my mother in Tokyo describes when she talks about her balcony herbs and her weekly reading group. The Okinawan data, read carefully, points away from the Western diagram, not toward it.

The thing the Western retelling consistently flattens

The most important distortion in the Anglophone Okinawan story is, I think, not the longevity statistics. It is the way the Okinawan ikigai has been lifted out of its social fabric and sold as an individual practice.

The elders Buettner described did not have ikigai in isolation. They had ikigai inside a moai, inside a multi-generational household, inside a community where elders had visible roles, inside a slow-food culture, inside a religious life that included regular ancestral ritual, inside a climate that permitted year-round outdoor walking. The ikigai was one thread in a thick weave. Pulling it out of the weave and offering it to a Brooklyn reader as a self-actualisation tool, with no moai, no multi-generational household, no village role, no ritual practice, and no walking weather, is something close to a category error. The Brooklyn reader is being sold a thread and told it is the cloth.

This is not, to be clear, the Okinawan elders' fault. They were answering questions in their own context. The flattening happens further down the supply chain — in the books, the talks, the branded retreats, and the social-media graphics that strip the context away because the context does not fit on a poster.

What an honest take-away looks like

If you are an English-speaking reader who has been holding the Okinawan story as evidence that ikigai will help you live to a hundred, I would gently suggest a smaller and more durable take-away.

The Okinawan elders did not live well, if they lived well, because of one word. They lived well because of a particular configuration of small daily practices and dense social structures, of which their use of ikigai was one part. The replicable lesson is not find your ikigai. It is closer to build the configuration. A few small daily reasons. A few low-stakes durable friendships you intend to keep for the rest of your life. A diet that is mostly plants, mostly the same things, mostly cooked at home. Walking. A role in some small community where you are visibly useful. The willingness to be ordinary at all of this for forty years.

None of this is sexy. It is, however, what the Okinawan data — read with caution about its limits — actually appears to recommend. The English-language wellness industry has spent twenty years selling readers the bumper sticker and not the configuration, because the bumper sticker is what fits in a book chapter and the configuration takes a lifetime to build.

How this connects to the Japanese mainland tradition

One last thing worth saying. The Okinawan use of ikigai is not the only Japanese use, and it is not the source of the word. Mainland Japanese psychiatry, particularly Mieko Kamiya's Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966), provides the more careful intellectual treatment, drawn not from longevity statistics but from clinical observation of patients in extreme circumstances. Ken Mogi's later popular book Awakening Your Ikigai (2017) writes about the everyday mainland use, with no longevity claims attached. Both treatments arrive at something like the small-daily-reasons account that the Okinawan elders also describe. The convergence, across mainland psychiatry, mainland popular writing, and Okinawan ethnography, is the actual signal. The Buettner longevity story is one strand of evidence for it; it is not the load-bearing one.

Final thought

The Okinawan story is real, partly. The longevity numbers are contested. The moai are wonderful. Hara hachi bu is a serious cultural practice, not a hack. The use of the word ikigai by Okinawan elders is genuine, and it sounds, on close reading, very much like the small-daily-reasons account the rest of the Japanese tradition has been describing for sixty years. What is not real is the equation find your ikigai = live to one hundred. That equation was built in the West, sold in the West, and is, on the available evidence, not what Okinawa actually shows. The Okinawan elders, if any of them are reading, are probably tending their gardens, going to the community centre on Tuesday, and not thinking about it at all.
