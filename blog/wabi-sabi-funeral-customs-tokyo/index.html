A Tokyo Funeral and the Practice of Mujō

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The funeral hall was on the second floor of a building I had walked past perhaps a hundred times without noticing, on a narrow street in the residential quiet south of Nippori. I arrived ten minutes early, in the black wool suit I keep in a cotton bag at the back of the wardrobe for this purpose, carrying a small envelope of kōden prepared the evening before. An usher in a white gloves bowed at the door and pointed, without speaking, to a table where I signed my name with a slim brush pen and placed the envelope on a lacquered tray. The air smelled faintly of sandalwood. It was 10:48 in the morning. The service was for a former colleague's mother, whom I had met twice.

I want to write about this carefully, because the subject is not mine and the grief belonged to a family who were not me. What I can offer, in the spirit of the slow-life writing this site is about, is what a Japanese Buddhist funeral looks like from the perspective of a quiet attendee, and what the philosophical tradition behind it — particularly the Sōtō Zen idea of mujō, impermanence — looks like when it is enacted rather than spoken. This is not a how-to. It is not morbid. It is, if anything, an essay about the small dignities a very old culture has arranged around the hardest hour of a family's year.

The shape of a sōgi

A Japanese Buddhist funeral — sōgi (葬儀) — has a remarkably stable shape across most sects, with small variations. The service I attended was Sōtō Zen, which is the tradition I know best and the one I will describe. The structure, for readers unfamiliar with it, is approximately this. A wake the night before (otsuya), which I did not attend. The funeral service itself on the following morning, roughly forty to sixty minutes. A committal at a nearby crematorium, usually only close family. A small gathering afterward, sometimes with a meal, often not.

The service proper is built around the chanting of sutras by a priest, the offering of incense by each attendee in turn, and — for a Sōtō service — specific liturgical elements including the Shushōgi, the "Meaning of Practice and Verification," a text drawn from Dōgen's thirteenth-century writings. The priest's voice is low and unhurried. There is no music. There is no speech in the Western sense. The priest does not eulogise the deceased; the service speaks through its own form, and the form is thought to be enough.

This was, for me, the most striking thing on the morning of the service. I have been to funerals in other traditions where the gravity of the hour is carried almost entirely by what the living say about the dead — their memories, their love, their specific remembered virtues. A sōgi does almost none of this. The form carries the weight. The attendees carry it by being present, by standing and sitting when the service asks them to, by offering incense in the correct small sequence. The family is spared the labour of performing their grief. They are simply there, inside a shape older than any of their feelings.

The incense offering

The central act an attendee performs is shōkō — the offering of incense. When your row is called, you stand, walk quietly to the altar at the front of the room, bow first to the family, then to the altar, and then offer incense. The specific gesture varies by sect. In Sōtō Zen, you take a small pinch of granular incense from a wooden box with the thumb and first two fingers of the right hand, raise it briefly to forehead height, and place it on the small bed of glowing ash in a smaller vessel in front of the altar. You do this once. (Some sects repeat the gesture two or three times; this is one of the visible differences between traditions, and it is considered gracious to follow the local custom if you know it, or simply to do it once respectfully if you do not.)

The moment is perhaps fifteen seconds long. It is the only action most attendees will perform during the service. It is, I think, one of the most carefully designed ritual moments in Japanese life.

Fifteen seconds is long enough for attention to land but too short to perform. You cannot stage grief inside fifteen seconds. You cannot turn the gesture into a speech. You can only do it. The ash is warm. The incense is coarse between the fingers. The smoke rises slowly and the smell is in the room for the rest of the service and into the cool hallway outside afterwards. The Zen tradition has understood for a long time that a physical gesture, carefully bounded in time, does work on the mind that words cannot.

What mujō looks like in practice

Mujō (無常) is often translated as "impermanence," and in English-language discussions of Japanese aesthetics it is frequently invoked — in wabi-sabi writing, in cherry-blossom essays, in accounts of Japanese attitudes toward time. What I saw at the funeral was how differently the concept behaves when it is lived rather than described.

Written about, mujō becomes a theme. Enacted, it becomes a quiet relaxation of an impossible demand. The Western funeral I have attended tends to ask its participants to somehow hold both the permanence of the person who has gone (their soul, their memory, their meaning) and the finality of the loss. This is a great deal to ask of anyone. The Zen funeral does not ask it. The form acknowledges that everything, including this person, is passing — and that this is the condition of everything, not a tragedy particular to this family — and what the service offers instead of permanence is continuity. The sutras chanted today have been chanted in roughly this form for eight centuries. The person who has gone is being released into a very long pattern. The family is being held inside the same pattern.

The philosopher Crispin Sartwell, writing about Japanese aesthetics in Six Names of Beauty, notes that mujō is not resignation. It is a recognition that the beauty of any particular thing — a cherry blossom, a face, a life — is not diminished by its passing but is, in some difficult sense, the same as its passing. You cannot have the one without the other. A flower that did not fall would not be a flower. The service I attended said this without saying anything at all.

Small practical details, offered carefully

Because readers of this site sometimes ask about the concrete texture of Japanese ritual life, I will note a few small practical details from the morning — offered not as etiquette instruction (proper guidance on attending a Japanese funeral requires more care than a blog can give) but as the kind of particulars that the phrase "Japanese Buddhist funeral" does not, by itself, convey.

The kōden envelope — a money gift to the family, always in used bills, placed in a special black-and-silver envelope bought at any convenience store — is handed over at the reception desk. The amount varies by closeness to the deceased; for a colleague's parent it is typically between five and ten thousand yen. The envelope is tied, not licked. The usher's bow is deeper than yours. You do not speak at the desk.

The clothing is not showy black. It is matte, restrained, with no jewellery heavier than small pearl studs. Men wear black suits and black ties. Women wear plain black dresses or suits, dark stockings, low-heeled shoes. The uniformity is deliberate. At a sōgi, the community sets its appearance down so that the family's loss is the only particular thing in the room.

On leaving, attendees are often given a small envelope of salt for purification and sometimes a small gift — a towel, a packet of tea, a small pouch of rice — as a thank-you for attending. You bow on the way out. You do not linger.

What I took home

I walked to a small coffee shop six streets from the hall after the service ended. I ordered a hōjicha and sat by the window. My hands still smelled faintly of the incense, and would until I washed them that evening. The small packet of salt was in my bag. I did not think very much. I drank the tea slowly.

What I took home from the morning was not a lesson about death. The tradition is not trying to teach a lesson. What I took home was something closer to a practical orientation: a sense that a very old form had just carried a very hard hour for a family I did not know well, and had asked almost nothing of me except presence, quiet, and fifteen seconds at the altar. That asymmetry — an enormous ritual labour carried by the form, a small attentional labour asked of the attendee — felt like one of the gentler arrangements a culture could make around grief.

It is also, and I say this tentatively, related to the other slow practices this site is built around. A household ledger asks attention, not willpower, and the form does most of the work. A nimono asks time, not skill, and the dashi does most of the work. A wabi-sabi room asks one beautiful imperfect object, not a renovation, and the object does most of the work. A Sōtō sōgi asks presence, not performance, and the liturgy does most of the work. In each case, a long tradition has built a form that reduces the person's burden by moving most of the labour into the practice itself. This is, I think, the deeper resource Japanese household culture has to offer.

A careful note on writing about this

I am aware that writing about a funeral on a blog about slow living risks a kind of appropriation — turning someone else's grief into material. I have tried to avoid that by keeping the person I am writing about essentially absent. This essay is about the form of the service and the philosophical tradition behind it, not about the life of the woman whose service I attended. The specifics I have included are generic to Sōtō funerals; the personal details, I have deliberately left out.

If you have found your way to this essay because you are preparing to attend a Japanese funeral yourself — as a foreign friend, a partner's relative, a long-time resident — I would encourage you to ask someone in the family or a local colleague about the specific sect and the specific customs. The outlines above are correct for Sōtō Zen, but the differences between sects are real and the courtesy of getting them close to right is part of what you owe the family. A fifteen-minute conversation beforehand will serve you better than any guide.

After the service

The colleague's mother's ashes would be interred in a family grave north of the city the following week. I was not involved in that part. I sent a short handwritten note a few days later — a single folded card, two sentences, nothing ambitious — and the colleague replied some weeks after that, also briefly. The exchange was small and correct.

The small pouch of salt sat on my kitchen counter for three days. On the third evening I took it outside and sprinkled a pinch at the threshold, which is the old custom, and put the rest away. The incense smell had faded from my suit by then. The suit went back into its cotton bag at the back of the wardrobe. The next morning I made a pot of genmaicha and sat at the desk and began writing this.

The service had ended. The form had done its work. The family continued on. Mujō, in the quiet practical sense the tradition means, is this — the recognition that everything, including the hardest hour, moves through and is held by something larger and older, and that the living are allowed, after a while, to return to their mornings.
