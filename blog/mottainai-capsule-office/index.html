The Mottainai Capsule Office: Three Pens, One Notebook

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

On my desk this morning, at the second-floor window of our apartment that looks onto a small street in Nishi-Ogikubo, there are three pens in a ceramic cup, one A5 notebook, one mug of hojicha, a small kitchen timer, and a laptop. That is the whole desk. There is no pen organiser. There is no second monitor. There is no wireless charging pad, no standing-desk riser, no "ergonomic" accessory of any kind. The cup holding the pens was given to me by a potter friend in 2017. The mug, heavier than it looks, is my grandmother's. I have been working at this desk, in roughly this configuration, for four years.

I am writing this not as a productivity essay. The productivity industry has its own appetite for "minimal desk" content, photographed against a pale oak grain and sold with a list of affiliate links to the exact items in the photograph. This is not that. What follows is a quieter account of how the desk got to this point — mostly by subtraction, over several years, under the slow influence of the word mottainai — and, more usefully, a list of the things I have stopped buying. The stopping is the more interesting half of the story.

Three pens, and why three

The three pens in the ceramic cup are: a black Uni-ball Signo 0.5 for ordinary writing, a fountain pen (Pilot Custom 74, bought second-hand in 2019) for longer reflective work, and a red Uni Jetstream for editing printouts. Each of the three has a specific job. Each of them has been in the cup, with occasional replacements of the black one, for at least three years.

I used to have many more pens. At one point, perhaps in 2019, I counted fourteen pens across various desks, bags, and coat pockets in the apartment. The fourteen were, roughly, the fossil record of half a decade of hopeful purchases — the pen I had hoped would make me write more, the pen I had hoped would make me write better, the novelty pen from a conference, the pen bought at a stationery shop in Kyoto on a weekend trip because the shop was charming and I wanted to carry something home from it.

The reduction to three did not happen on a single Sunday. It happened as each pen's individual story resolved. Some ran out of ink and I did not replace them. Some I gave away. Some I honestly lost. A few I sent to Mercari. What survived were the three that were genuinely useful, plus the fountain pen that was genuinely loved, and the cup became stable on its own, without any edit or decision.

One notebook

The notebook on my desk is a Midori MD A5, plain paper, soft cover. I am on my eleventh of them in six years. Each one lasts me roughly six to seven months. I have tried, over the years, several more elaborate notebook systems — a bullet-journal phase that lasted three months, a Leuchtturm period, an attempt at a disc-bound system from Kokuyo, a six-week flirtation with a Hobonichi Techo. Each of these produced beautiful first pages and then, usually by month two, a kind of low-grade shame at the gap between the aesthetic ambition of the notebook and the ordinariness of my actual thinking.

The Midori is the notebook that let that shame dissolve. It is plain enough that a messy page does not feel like a betrayal of the book. It is good enough paper that the fountain pen does not bleed through. It costs around 1,400 yen and I can buy a replacement at any Maruzen or Tokyu Hands in the city. I am no longer shopping for notebook systems. The question has been retired.

One mug, one timer, one laptop

The mug is the mug I use every morning. It has a small chip on the rim, near the handle, from a fall in 2022. I have never considered replacing it. The chip is in the place where my lower lip does not meet the rim. The mug continues to function exactly as it always has.

The timer is a small white kitchen timer, analogue, bought at a Muji for 950 yen in 2018. I use it for the Pomodoro-style work intervals that are most of my writing day — twenty-five minutes of focused work, five minutes of pause, repeat. I have tried more sophisticated timers on the laptop. They all lose to the tiny satisfying click of the dial.

The laptop is a laptop. I have had two across the last eight years. The current one is going on four. I do not plan to replace it until it genuinely stops working, which, given Japanese domestic electronics' reputation for mulishness, could be another four.

What I have stopped buying

This is the real list, and the one I think readers find most useful. In no particular order, here are the categories of "office" purchase I have, slowly, stopped making.

Notebook systems. As above. One Midori A5 is my whole system. If you find yourself buying a second notebook before you have finished the first, the second one is not a tool; it is a mild avoidance of whatever the first one is asking you to write.

Desk organisers. I had a small wooden one for two years. It gave the pens a tidier-looking home and produced, in practice, one additional surface on which to accumulate paper clips, receipts, and guilt. The ceramic cup does the job without the infrastructure.

Planner apps. I have tried perhaps six of them. None of them has survived a year. The Midori notebook plus a shared paper family calendar on the refrigerator handles every scheduling need that the apps were supposed to handle, and does not follow me into my evenings the way an app does.

Productivity books. This is a hard one, because I read a great deal. I used to buy perhaps six productivity books a year, hopefully, in the same spirit I bought hopeful pens. I have not bought one in three years. The few principles from them I actually use — short focused sessions with a timer, a small daily reflective practice, clear weekly planning — I had already internalised by my late twenties. The books were not teaching me. They were performing the idea of teaching me.

Second monitors and accessories. I worked with a second monitor for three years. It produced, honestly, no measurable improvement in my work. It produced one measurable decline, which was that the desk became cluttered enough that it no longer wanted to be sat at. The monitor went to Mercari in 2022. The desk has felt spacious since.

"Work clothes." I write from home. I wear what is in my eighteen-item wardrobe. The category of "loungewear I can also work in" does not exist in my apartment, because a grey sweater is a grey sweater, and it can perform both roles without ceremony.

New technology on release. I wait, usually, two to three years after a new laptop model or phone model is released before I think about it. The first year of any new device's life is for other people; the third year is when the price is honest and the known problems are documented. This is not a productivity principle. It is simply how I have arrived at most of my electronics.

The thing no one talks about

The real benefit of a capsule office is not productivity. I do not produce more words per day than I did five years ago. I may produce slightly fewer. The benefit is that the desk has stopped being an arena.

There is a particular mental cost to working at a surface that contains many small, unresolved decisions. Which pen. Which notebook. Which organiser system. Which app. Each of these decisions, individually trivial, produces a small residual attentional drag that most people do not notice because it is invisible and constant. Remove enough of them and the desk quiets down. The pen you pick up is just the black pen. The notebook you open is just the notebook. Attention goes to the work, because attention is no longer being nibbled at by the tools.

This is, I think, what a mottainai capsule office actually produces. Not efficiency. Not aesthetic virtue. A quieter surface to work from. The desk has stopped asking for anything. What I bring to it, I bring from elsewhere.

Final thought

If you would like to move in this direction — and I do not think everyone should; some people genuinely flourish in a richly tooled working environment, and I do not want to prescribe my temperament onto theirs — the method is almost embarrassingly simple. Do not buy anything new for your desk for one full year. Replace only what genuinely runs out or breaks. At the end of the year, look at what is still on the desk. That, very likely, is the real shape of your work. The rest was hopeful furniture.

My desk, after four years of this, is three pens, one notebook, one mug, one timer, one laptop. I do not think that is the right answer for everyone. It happens to be the right answer for me. I would not have arrived at it by designing it. I arrived at it by, month after month, not buying the next thing.
