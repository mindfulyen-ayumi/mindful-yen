Ikigai After 40: A Conversation With My Mother

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

My mother is sixty-three. She lives in a quiet apartment near a temple in the western edge of the city, with a small balcony she has slowly converted into a herb garden. On a Sunday in late spring I went over for lunch and stayed into the afternoon, and at some point — I cannot remember which of us raised it — the conversation turned to ikigai. I had been working on a series of essays. She had been thinking, on her own, about the same questions, with a slightly longer view than mine.

What follows is, more or less, what we talked about. I have tidied the sentences for English readers and compressed two hours of conversation into something closer to thirty minutes. The substance is hers. I have learned more about ikigai from this one afternoon than I did from any of the books on the subject.

What she said her ikigai used to be

Through her thirties and most of her forties, she said, her ikigai had been mostly two things. The first was me — a small daughter who needed her, and then a teenage daughter who somewhat less obviously needed her, and then a young adult daughter who was at university and pretended not to. The second was her work, a position at a publishing house in Jimbocho where she edited a children's literature imprint for twenty-two years.

She described both of these, looking back, as ikigai of a particular kind: ikigai with an obvious shape. They were external, identifiable, narratable. If a stranger had asked her what she lived for, she could have answered the question in a single sentence. She did not have to think about it. The shape of her life and the shape of her ikigai were the same shape. This, she said, was both a gift and a kind of unawareness. She had not had to ask the question. The answer had been doing the asking for her.

What changed in her early fifties

The change came, she said, in her early fifties, and it came in two stages.

The first stage was that I left the country for a stretch and stopped, in any practical sense, requiring her. She did not put it that bluntly, but I understood her to mean it. The shape of motherhood had been quietly carrying part of her ikigai for thirty years, and when the shape became less load-bearing, what was left underneath turned out to be less than she had expected. She did not say this with self-pity. She said it as an observation. There was, she said, a kind of structural lightness in the year after I left, which she had at first mistaken for freedom and which she eventually realised was closer to vertigo.

The second stage was retirement, at fifty-six, earlier than she had planned but encouraged by her firm during a restructuring. The publishing house had been her ikigai for two decades. The press of daily work, the editing of manuscripts, the relationships with authors and illustrators, the slow accumulation of a small body of books she was proud of — all of this had been doing quiet work for her psyche that she had not noticed because it was always there. When the work ended, she said, the silence in the apartment in the first three months was almost deafening. She would sit at the table at ten in the morning with a cup of tea and not know what to do with herself.

The Kamiya distinction, lived

I asked her, at this point, whether she had read Mieko Kamiya. She had not, but when I described Kamiya's distinction between ikigai as object and ikigai-kan as the felt sense of meaningfulness, she nodded slowly and said that this was almost exactly what she had been working through, in her own words, for the past eight years.

Her version was this. Through her thirties and forties, her ikigai had been almost entirely object-shaped — a daughter, a job. The objects had been so reliable that she had never had to develop the underlying capacity. When the objects loosened, in her fifties, she had been forced to discover whether the felt sense could be cultivated independently, with no obvious external object to hang it on. The first year, she said, she had failed at this. She had tried to find a replacement object — a new project, a community role, a return to a craft she had left behind in her twenties — and none of the candidates had taken. The replacement strategy had not worked.

What had eventually worked, she said, was the opposite of replacement. It had been the slow accumulation of many small things, none of which were trying to be a single ikigai. The herb garden on the balcony. A weekly reading group with three other women in her neighbourhood, in which they read a chapter of a long novel together and talked about it over tea. Long walks. A short Tuesday-morning class on Japanese calligraphy at a community centre. A correspondence with two friends from university she had not been close to in thirty years and had recently rediscovered. None of these were her ikigai. All of them, together, were the felt sense.

What she would say to her thirty-five-year-old self

I asked her what she would say to her thirty-five-year-old self, if she could. The question was partly cheap and partly serious; I am thirty-five.

She thought about it for a while. What she eventually said was that she would not tell her younger self to do anything differently. The years of motherhood had been the best years of her life. The publishing house had been a real vocation. She did not regret organising her life around two large objects.

What she would tell her younger self, she said, was that the structure was temporary, and that it would have been wise to begin, in parallel, the slower practice of distributing meaning across many small anchors. Not because the large objects would fail — they would not fail; they would simply, in time, become less load-bearing. But because the underlying capacity for the felt sense takes years to build, and starting at fifty-six is harder than starting at thirty-five. If she had been quietly tending small anchors all along — a few walks, a few friendships outside work, a weekly hour with a craft, a small garden — the transition in her fifties would have been a continuation rather than a rupture.

This, she said, was the only piece of practical advice she had. Start the small anchors now. Do not wait until you need them. By the time you need them, they will be ten years overdue.

What this means for the diagram

I had brought up, earlier in the lunch, the four-circle diagram I had been writing about. She had looked at it on my phone with the particular polite expression she reserves for things she is not going to say what she thinks of. What she did say, later in the afternoon, was that the diagram seemed to her to be a young person's tool. It assumed, she said, that one had a single life-shaped vocation to find, and that finding it was the work of one's twenties and thirties. This might or might not be true for the people who made the diagram. It was, she said, almost certainly the wrong frame for anyone past forty.

The post-forty version of ikigai, she said, was much closer to the Kamiya version: a felt sense, distributed across many small anchors, with no single object carrying the weight. The diagram could not really describe this. There was no single point in the centre. There was a constellation. The constellation was the answer.

I have written elsewhere about why the diagram fails as a description of the Japanese tradition. My mother's account, drawn from her own life rather than from any book, says the same thing in different words. The convergence is, I think, not a coincidence.

A small practice, started late

For readers approaching forty, or already past it, the practical implication of all of this is, I think, modest and useful.

Start the small anchors now. Pick three. They do not have to be impressive. A weekly walk in a particular park. A monthly letter to a particular friend. A Saturday-morning hour with a craft you used to love. Do them with no expectation that they will become your ikigai. Do them precisely because they are too small to be anyone's ikigai. Then add a fourth in six months. And a fifth a year after that. Build the constellation while you still have the energy to do it lightly.

If you would like a quiet weekly practice for noticing what is already accumulating, the three-question Sunday journal I have written about separately is a reasonable place to start. Subscribers to the Mindful Yen Substack sometimes write to me with their own constellations after a year of the practice. The shapes vary considerably. The thing they have in common is that none of them resemble a Venn diagram with a single bright centre.

Final thought

I left my mother's apartment around five, with a small bag of her herbs and a longer note in my journal than I had expected. Walking back to the station in the late afternoon light, I thought about how much of the English-language conversation about ikigai is conducted by people in their thirties writing for other people in their thirties, and how little of it is informed by the view from sixty. My mother's view is, I think, the more useful one. The shape of a life worth living is not a single bright point at the centre of four circles. It is a constellation of small anchors, tended quietly over decades, that holds you up when the large objects loosen. Start tending. The constellation is patient. It will be there when you need it, if you have done the slow work of putting it there.
