A Reading Log in Notion, Built With Tsundoku in Mind

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

There is a stack of books on the floor beside my desk, three deep against the wall, that I have not read. Most of them I bought in the last eighteen months. A handful are inheritances — books my father pressed on me at the new year and that I accepted out of affection rather than appetite. The Japanese word for this stack is tsundoku (積ん読), and any honest reading log built in Notion has to begin by acknowledging that the stack is not a problem to be solved. It is a feature of the practice.

This essay is about the reading log I keep in Notion, which I rebuilt last winter after the third version of it had quietly become a guilt machine. The version I use now is shorter, plainer, and openly affectionate toward the unread pile. If you would prefer to skip ahead, you can try the Notion workspace I use here →, but the structure matters more than the template, and the structure is below.

Why most reading logs make readers stop reading

The first reading log I ever built was a spreadsheet with thirteen columns. Title, author, year of publication, genre, sub-genre, page count, date started, date finished, rating out of five, mood when reading, where I bought it, whether it was a gift, and a long-text field for notes. I filled it in faithfully for about four months. By the fifth month I had stopped reading nearly as much. I assumed I was busy. I was not busy. I was avoiding the spreadsheet, and the spreadsheet had become entangled with the books, and the books had become a chore.

I do not think this is unusual. The most common letter I receive from readers about reading is some variation of I built a beautiful Notion reading tracker and stopped reading. A reading log that asks more of you than the reading itself is, mathematically, a reason to read less. The fix is not better fields. The fix is fewer fields, and an honest field for the books you bought and never opened.

What tsundoku is, and what it is not

Tsundoku is sometimes translated into English as "book hoarding," which is unkind and slightly wrong. The literal sense of the word is "buying-and-piling reading" — books bought with the genuine intention of reading them, then piled up, then not yet read. The piling is intentional. The not-yet is also intentional. Tsundoku is not about acquisition for its own sake. It is about keeping a near-future self stocked with possibilities.

I write this from a city where a single trip to the Jimbocho second-hand bookshops can yield ten volumes for the price of two new hardbacks elsewhere, and where the cultural attitude toward an unread shelf is not embarrassment but quiet anticipation. The shelf is a kind of promise. The reading log, if it is to be useful, has to honour that promise without converting it into a debt.

The four properties I actually use

The current reading log has one database and four properties. That is all.

  • Title — the book.
  • State — a select with three options: on the shelf, reading, finished. There is no "did not finish." Books that I started and put down go back to on the shelf.
  • Date finished — only filled in when I finish. Empty otherwise.
  • One sentence — a single long-text field. One sentence after I finish the book. That is the only writing the log asks of me.

That is the entire schema. There is no rating. There is no genre. There is no page count. The reasons are uniform: every additional field made the log feel like an inventory rather than a companion, and an inventory is the wrong relationship to have with books.

Why no rating

Ratings are the field I miss least. A book I gave four stars three years ago has often turned out, in the slow accounting of memory, to have been a three. A book I gave two stars at the time has occasionally become the book I quote most. The rating, in the moment, is almost never the truth. The one-sentence note, written after, is closer.

Why no "did not finish"

The "did not finish" tag is the field that quietly does the most damage. It converts a paused book into a failed book. Most of the books I have eventually loved I started and abandoned at least once. A reading log that records the abandonment as a verdict makes a second attempt feel like denial. The state on the shelf is more honest. The book is on the shelf. That is the whole truth.

The tsundoku page

Inside the database there is one filtered view that I open more often than any other. It is called the shelf, and it shows every book in the state on the shelf — both books I have not yet started and books I have started and paused. The view is sorted not by date added but by an extremely simple rule: alphabetical by title. The reason for the alphabetical sort is that any other sort imposes a hierarchy on the shelf, and a real shelf does not have a hierarchy. It is a place where books wait.

The shelf currently holds about eighty books in Notion and roughly the same on the wall behind me. (The Notion view includes books I own digitally; the physical pile is its own analogue archive.) Eighty is a number that, once upon a time, would have made me anxious. Now it makes me feel slightly wealthy. There is no week in which I cannot reach behind me and find a book that suits the weather.

What the log is for, when it is not for tracking

If the log is not a productivity tool — and it is not — then the question is what it is for. The honest answer is that it is for the one-sentence field. Once a book is finished, I sit at the kitchen table with the book closed in front of me and write one sentence about it in Notion. Not a review. Not a summary. A sentence that captures whatever the book did to me that I would not want to forget.

Reading those sentences back, six or twelve months later, has turned out to be the single most pleasant thing in the workspace. The sentences are short enough to read in sequence. They form, slowly, a kind of intellectual diary. The book itself recedes; the sentence remains. This is the same logic I have written about in the kakeibo Notion template — the numbers are the raw material, the sentence is the practice.

How the log behaves around purchases

When I buy a book — which I do roughly twice a month, sometimes more after a Jimbocho afternoon — I add it to the database in the state on the shelf, with no other fields filled. I do not log where I bought it, what I paid, or why. The shelf does not need a provenance file. It needs a name and a place.

I keep a separate, parallel record of book spending in the kakeibo ledger, under the culture category. That is where the financial accountability lives, and it lives there gently. Books are one of the very few categories of spending I have decided, after years of monthly reflections, to be permissive about. The decision is not financial. The decision is that a household with too many unread books is, in the long run, a richer household than one with too few. Hani might have disagreed. My grandmother, who kept three shelves of unread novels her whole life, would not have.

What I do once a season

Four times a year, on the first weekend of the new season, I open the shelf view and do a slow re-shelving. I read every title. I move a small handful — usually three or four — into a temporary view called this season, which is the only place the database lets me make a soft commitment. The season view has a maximum of five rows. If a fifth book wants to go in, an existing one has to come out. That ceiling is the only constraint in the entire system, and it is the reason I now finish more books than I did when I had no reading log at all.

The seasonal re-shelving takes about twenty minutes and has the strange effect of feeling like a small holiday. I make tea. I open the balcony door if it is warm enough. I read titles I had forgotten I owned. Sometimes a book I had been carrying around in my head for months turns out to have been on the shelf the entire time.

What changed when I stopped over-tracking

I read more. That is the short answer, and it is the one I find most surprising. The longer answer is that I read differently. With thirteen fields to fill in, every book I started carried a small administrative tail. With four fields and one of them optional, books arrive lighter in my hands. I finish more of them because there is less to do when I do.

I also buy slightly fewer books, although I did not set out to. The seasonal re-shelving exposes books I had forgotten about, and an exposed forgotten book is a strong reason not to buy a new one. The kakeibo ledger has, over the past year, shown a small but steady decline in the culture category, almost entirely from this effect. I did not budget for it. The structure produced it.

Letters from readers about reading logs

A few questions appear in reader letters often enough to belong here.

"Should I import my Goodreads history?" Probably not. I tried. The historical data turned the log into an archive, and an archive is a different kind of object than a log. If you want the historical books somewhere, put them in a separate Notion page called books I have read before this log, and leave them there. The log is for the present.

"What about books I read for work?" I keep them in the same database with no special tag. The distinction between work reading and other reading is, in my experience, less stable than people assume. A book I bought for an editorial project last March is now one I quote in personal essays. The log does not need to know which is which.

"How do you handle re-reads?" I add a new row, with a new finished date and a new sentence. The old row stays. Reading the same book at thirty-three is not the same act as reading it at twenty-six, and the log respects that.

What runs alongside the log

The reading log lives next to the rest of the workspace I have written about in the solo creators essay — the editorial calendar, the reader inbox, the asset library, the kakeibo ledger. The reading log is the only database in the workspace that is purely personal. Nothing in it is for publication. That is, I think, why it has lasted longer than any other database I have built.

If you want a paper companion, the Mindful Yen Etsy shop sells a small reading journal built around the same one-sentence-per-book structure. Some readers keep both — physical for the sentence, Notion for the shelf. That is also how I do it for the books I want to remember most clearly.

Final thought

A reading log built with tsundoku in mind is one that does not punish you for the unread pile. The pile is part of the practice. So is the slow finishing, the occasional pausing, the seasonal re-shelving, the one quiet sentence at the end. The log is small enough to open. The shelf is large enough to wait on you. That is the whole arrangement, and after a year of using it, it is the first reading system that has not slowly made me read less.

If you want the workspace, it is here. If you want longer letters about the reading life, the Mindful Yen Substack is where I write them. The shelf will still be there tomorrow. There is no hurry.
