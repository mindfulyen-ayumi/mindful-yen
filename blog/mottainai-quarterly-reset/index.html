A Quarterly Reset Ritual From Tokyo: What I Revisit Each Season

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Four times a year, on a Saturday afternoon near the start of each season, I carry a paper notebook and a pot of tea to the low table in my apartment's south-facing window, sit on the floor, and spend roughly three hours running what I privately call the quarterly reset. The seasons I use are the traditional Japanese quarters — early April, early July, early October, early January — not the Western calendar ones. The notebook is a plain A5 with a cloth cover. The tea, in July, is cold barley; in January, hot hōjicha. The ritual takes nothing else. I have been doing it for roughly eight years.

This is the essay I have been asked for more often than any other by Substack readers. It is a hard essay to write, because the ritual is both smaller and slower than the question usually expects. I will describe it carefully and in its actual size. If it is useful, it is useful. If it is too small to be useful, that is fine too — not every practice needs to scale to everyone.

Why quarterly, specifically

The quarterly rhythm is not arbitrary. The annual review is too slow — by the time you sit down in December to reckon with the year, most of what went wrong is already six months in the past and the lesson has hardened. The monthly review is too fast — a single month rarely contains enough data to distinguish a pattern from noise, and the monthly reviewer often ends up re-visiting the same small irritations over and over.

Three months is, for me and for most of the readers who have written back, the correct grain. A quarter is long enough that a pattern has had time to form. It is short enough that a pattern you notice in July can still be redirected before October. And the Japanese seasonal calendar, which hinges on small observable changes in the physical world — the first cicadas, the chill on a night in late September, the plum blossoms in early February — gives the quarters a sensory anchor that a pure calendar date does not.

The structure

The reset has three parts. Each takes roughly an hour. I do not set timers; the parts settle into the time they need, and the order is fixed.

The first part is reading backward. I open the notebook to the previous season's reset and read it slowly. I do not edit or annotate. I just read. This often takes longer than I expect — not because the entries are long, but because re-reading what I was thinking in April, from the other side of the summer, produces small private reactions that I want to let land before moving on.

The second part is the three questions. On a fresh page I write, by hand, three questions and answer them in full sentences rather than lists. The questions are always the same. I will list them in a moment.

The third part is the small shelf. I get up, stretch, walk through the apartment, and physically visit three locations — the kitchen shelf, the bedroom drawer, the genkan. At each one I look for a single object that has been there, unused, for the whole previous season. I do not clean aggressively. I do not Marie-Kondo the apartment. I simply name, to myself, one object per location that has earned a decision this quarter. Sometimes the decision is to keep it. Sometimes the decision is to release it. Either is the ritual working.

The three questions

The questions have not changed in six years. I resisted the urge to refine them, because refinement usually makes questions narrower, and these work by being general enough to catch whatever the season was actually about.

What did this season deserve that I did not give it? This is the mottainai question. The direction of the regret is outward — what did the last three months ask of me that I did not meet. The answer is almost always specific and almost always small. A friend I meant to write to. A book I meant to finish. A morning I meant to spend quiet and did not. I write these out in sentences, not bullets, because sentences force me to say the full shape of what happened rather than compress it into a label.

What did I give this season that it did not ask of me? This is the inverse, and the harder one. What did I over-invest in. What did I treat as urgent that was not. What did I push myself on, to no one's benefit, out of anxiety rather than judgment. This question takes me the longest. The answers here are usually the seeds of whatever I will try to adjust in the next quarter.

What one small shape do I want the next season to take? Not a goal. Not a list of goals. A shape — a single sentence that describes the quality of the next three months, not its outputs. "I want the summer to be quieter than the spring was." "I want the autumn to contain at least one long walk every week." "I want the winter to have more tea and less screen." I write only one sentence. Sometimes I rewrite it three or four times before it lands right.

What the small shelf does

The third part, the walk through the apartment, is the piece that surprised me when I added it three years in. I had been doing only the reading and the questions. The walk felt almost silly — a middle-aged woman pacing her own apartment looking for a single object per room — but it turned out to be the part that made the reset stick.

The reason, I think, is that the questions can drift into abstraction. An answer to "what did I give this season that it did not ask of me" can stay comfortably theoretical. The small shelf grounds the reset in an actual object. The jar I have not opened. The shirt I have not worn. The book I started and abandoned. Each object is a tiny piece of evidence about how the last three months actually went, independent of what my notebook self has decided to say about them.

I am careful not to turn this into a purge. A quarterly reset is not a decluttering session. On most visits I keep the object, having simply named it. The naming is what matters. Mottainai is served by attention; it is not necessarily served by removal. A book that has earned a decision and been consciously kept is a different object, afterward, than a book that simply sat there.

What the reset is not

I want to be clear about what the ritual does not contain, because the productivity version of a "quarterly review" — OKRs, scorecards, KPI dashboards — has colonised so much of the English-language internet that the bare version can be hard to see.

There is no metric. I do not grade the previous season against a target. I do not count completed items. I do not score my behaviour. The reset is qualitative end to end, and deliberately so. A quantitative reset produces a tyrant; a qualitative one produces a soft bow.

There is no list of goals for the next quarter. The one-sentence shape replaces the goal list entirely. I have tried goal lists. They fragment. The sentence holds. A reader wrote to me last year that her year changed when she replaced her Q3 goal list with a single sentence — "this quarter I want to stop performing competence for people who have not asked me to" — and I believed her immediately.

There is no tool beyond the notebook. No app, no template, no spreadsheet. I have no objection to digital tools; the money ledger I keep lives in Notion, and I use other digital tools daily. The reset, specifically, is on paper, because handwriting slows me down to the speed of actual thought. I have tried digital versions. They finish faster and change less.

The seasonal specifics

Each quarterly reset has a slightly different texture. I do not plan this; it emerges.

The spring reset, in early April, tends to be about opening. The questions surface what I have been carrying through winter that wants to be set down. The one-sentence shape is usually about motion — walking, seeing, meeting.

The summer reset, in early July, tends to be about noticing. The city is hot. Movement is costly. The reset turns inward and asks what the slower months want from me. The one-sentence shape is often about quiet.

The autumn reset, in early October, is the longest and the most honest. The year's arc is visible by then. What I intended in January is either becoming real or has not. The reset is where I face that without softening it, and where the one-sentence shape often names a reduction rather than an expansion.

The winter reset, in early January, is the one that most closely resembles a Western new-year review, but in a smaller key. I do not write resolutions. I write the one-sentence shape for the first quarter of the year and a slightly longer note about what the year has begun to feel like.

A few practical notes

The notebook lives with the ritual. I do not use it for anything else. An A5 lasts me roughly four years — sixteen resets, each twelve to sixteen handwritten pages. When a notebook fills I archive it on a shelf and start a fresh one. I have read back through the full archive once, two years ago, and it was one of the more useful afternoons of my adult life.

The tea matters more than I first thought. A hot drink paces the ritual; I finish the pot roughly when the reset finishes. A glass of water, or nothing, moves too fast.

The window matters. A room you spend the ritual facing a wall in will produce a different reset than one you spend facing light and a tree. This is not aesthetic preciousness; it is, as far as I can tell, how attention actually works.

If the ritual appeals to you but you would rather work from a slightly more guided version, the Mindful Yen Substack runs a quarterly reset letter four times a year with prompts and a slower walk-through. It is free. I also keep a short handwritten journal of the three questions in the Mindful Yen Etsy shop if you want a ready-made paper version, though a plain notebook works just as well.

The closing posture

I finish each reset the same way. I close the notebook, put the cap on the pen, pour the last of the tea, and sit with the empty page for a minute or two before I get up. The minute is part of the ritual. It is the moment where the reset is allowed to have been what it was rather than immediately converted into a task list.

Then I put the notebook on the shelf, go for a walk, and do not think about it again until three months later. The ritual is designed to be forgettable between resets. Its power is in the return, not in the retention. You close the notebook. The season proceeds. You come back.
