A Notion Template for Writing a Substack

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I write a Substack called Mindful Yen. It goes out on Friday mornings, Tokyo time. Each issue is between 1,200 and 2,200 words, with the occasional photograph taken on a walk and the very occasional voiceover in the audio version. A single reader once asked me, gently, whether I had a system. I told her the truth, which is that I have a Notion setup with three databases and a page, and that the whole thing took about an hour to build and three years to refine. This essay is the refined version.

It is addressed to writers starting a Substack or to writers with a Substack whose setup has, like most setups, grown into something they are faintly embarrassed by. The goal is not a template that is impressive. The goal is a template that gets out of the way on Thursday evening, when the draft is due and the kettle is on.

If you want the workspace in front of you, you can try the Notion template I use here →. The rest of the essay explains why it is built the way it is.

The three databases and the page

One drafts database. One reader-letter inbox. One essay atlas. One editorial-calendar page that is mostly a linked view. That is the whole system.

I will describe each in order of how often I open it during a typical week, because the shape of a workspace is best understood by how much of the week it touches, not by how prominently it sits on the home page.

1. The drafts database

Every piece of writing that might become a Substack post, Mindful Yen essay, or printable journal entry begins here. The fields are minimal: title, state, destination, and a long-text body. The states are the same three I described in the project management essay — Today, Waiting, Done — because one state vocabulary across the workspace is worth more than a tailored one per database.

The destination field has four values: Substack, Mindful Yen, Etsy product, and undecided. Most drafts begin as undecided. Some pieces are clearly one or the other when I start — a reader letter always becomes a Substack reply; a longer researched essay always becomes a Mindful Yen post — but many pieces move between destinations during drafting. A Substack post that grows past 2,500 words is probably a Mindful Yen essay with a short Substack excerpt. A Mindful Yen essay that feels too conversational belongs on Substack instead. The destination field allows this movement without ceremony.

The drafts body itself lives in the long-text field for pieces under about 2,000 words. Longer pieces I draft in iA Writer and paste in at the editing stage, for the small technical reason I mentioned in the solo-creator setup essay: Notion's editor becomes slightly sluggish past a few thousand words. This is the only piece of tool-splitting in the whole workflow. Everything else is Notion.

2. The reader-letter inbox

This database is the single most valuable thing I have, and I am convinced most Substack writers are underusing the equivalent in their own practice.

Every reader reply — on Substack, by email, on Etsy in the rare case someone asks a real question about a journal — gets one row. Date, initials, the question paraphrased, and a checkbox for whether I have replied. That is the entire schema. I have described it before, in the context of Mindful Yen generally, but it deserves its own note here because its relationship to the Substack is particularly direct.

The reader inbox is where Substack issues come from. Not every issue. But a reliable majority. When three readers ask versions of the same question in the same month, that question is the next issue. The essay practically writes itself, because three specific human beings have already asked it, and the drafting consists of answering them at once. Substack issues written this way have, in my experience, about twice the open rate of issues written from the air, and the open rate is less the point than the fact that the issues are simply better. They are replies, not broadcasts. Readers feel the difference even when they cannot name it.

If you are starting a Substack now, I would build the reader inbox on day one, before the first issue goes out. It takes thirty seconds per letter. By the third month of publishing, it will be the database you open most often, and the one that tells you what to write.

3. The essay atlas

The atlas is the bridge between the personal wiki I described in the zuihitsu essay and the Substack itself. Each row is a recurrent theme — kakeibo, attention, slow living, the Tokyo domestic — and each row has a linked view that pulls in every fragment tagged with that theme from the wiki.

When I sit down on a Thursday morning to write Friday's Substack, I usually know which theme it will touch. I open the atlas, open the theme's page, and read the fragments as a kind of warm-up. Two-thirds of the drafting is already done, distributed across months of earlier thinking. The Substack becomes a matter of arrangement rather than generation, and Thursday mornings, which used to be the most stressful part of my week, became the calmest.

The atlas does not exist for the Substack; it exists for the thinking. But the Substack is its most visible output, and the value of the atlas becomes legible in the Substack more than anywhere else.

4. The editorial-calendar page

The editorial calendar is a single page, not a database. At the top, the next four Fridays. Under each Friday, the provisional title of that week's Substack issue. That is the whole thing.

I used to have a proper calendar view with a month of scheduled pieces and carefully colour-coded destinations. I retired it a year ago. The simpler page — four Fridays, four provisional titles — is better, because it is honest about how far ahead I actually plan, which is approximately four weeks. Anything beyond four weeks is, frankly, fictional, and a calendar that pretends otherwise is a calendar that produces unnecessary anxiety every time I look at it. The page-with-four-Fridays produces no anxiety at all, because it shows exactly what exists.

The Thursday-evening rhythm

The Substack goes out Friday at 7 a.m. Tokyo time. The rhythm that gets it there is stable enough to describe.

Monday through Wednesday, I do not think about the Substack directly. I write fragments, reply to reader letters, do the other work of the publication. If a strong idea arrives, it goes into the drafts database in Waiting. If a reader question is clearly Friday's issue, the drafts row gets moved to Today on Monday evening and the title gets added to the editorial calendar.

Thursday morning, I open the theme's atlas page and read the fragments. Thursday afternoon, I write the first draft in iA Writer — paper next to me, tea, no internet. Thursday evening, I paste the draft into the drafts database, edit slowly, and schedule the issue in Substack itself. Friday morning, I do not open the laptop until after the Substack has gone out. This is a small superstition and I keep it.

This rhythm works for a once-a-week Substack. For a twice-a-week or daily Substack, the shape would have to be different, and I would be wary of scaling a slow rhythm to a fast frequency without real thought. Speed tends to flatten voice, and the whole point of a Substack — for me, at least — is the voice.

What I do not do in Notion for the Substack

For honesty, a short list of things that stay outside Notion.

Analytics. Substack's own analytics are fine, and pulling them into Notion only gives the numbers more weight than they deserve. I look at them on the first Sunday of each month, during the monthly reflection, and not at any other time.

The subscriber list. It lives on Substack, where it belongs. I do not mirror it.

Email drafts. The Substack editor itself is where I do final formatting — the long-text field in Notion is for the prose, not for the presentation. Moving between them is the small friction that makes me reread the piece one more time, which is always useful.

Payment records. Substack handles this. The monthly totals get copied into the kakeibo ledger I described in the kakeibo template essay, because the ledger is where financial attention lives.

A brief note on course creators

A question I get often from writers who have been on Substack for a year or two is whether Substack can eventually handle a full creator business — newsletters, courses, products, email sequences. The honest answer is: up to a point, yes, and past that point, probably not. Substack is excellent at writing and at paid subscriptions. It is not a course platform. It is not a funnel.

For writers whose Substack has grown into something more than a newsletter — typically around the point where they are selling a second thing, or running a small course — the usual next tool is a dedicated platform that handles courses, funnels, and automations in one place. Systeme.io is the one I have seen used most often by Substack writers I respect, because its free tier is unusually generous and its learning curve is gentler than the alternatives. I do not use it myself — my setup is Substack, Notion, and Etsy, and that is enough — but I mention it because the question comes up often enough that the omission would be dishonest. For a Substack that is staying a Substack, you do not need it. For a Substack that is becoming a small creator business, it is worth looking at.

How long it took to arrive at this

The first Substack setup I had, in 2023, was a spreadsheet and a lot of anxiety. The second, later that year, was a Notion workspace with eleven databases and a dashboard that I spent more time arranging than writing. The current setup — three databases and a page — is the result of removing, not adding. Each database I removed made the workspace more useful. I am wary of adding anything back.

If you are starting from scratch, you can skip the subtraction years. Begin with the drafts database and the reader inbox. Add the atlas when your wiki has enough fragments to populate it. Add the editorial calendar page only when you find yourself genuinely unsure what is coming up on Friday. That is the whole build sequence, and it will get you to a working Substack workflow in a week.

A closing thought

A Substack is a letter, sent weekly, to people who agreed to receive it. A Notion setup for a Substack should support that letter and otherwise stay out of the way. The test I apply to any new feature or database is whether it makes Thursday evening easier or harder. Easier, in; harder, out. Most features, on this test, fail.
