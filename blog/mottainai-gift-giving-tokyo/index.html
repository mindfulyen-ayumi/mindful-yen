Mottainai and Gift-Giving in Tokyo: Ochūgen, Oseibo, and the Obligation Problem

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

In the first week of July my mother receives, every year, a wooden box of Yubari melons from a family friend in Hokkaido. The box is flat, pale, and sealed with a strip of washi paper printed with the friend's family name. Inside are two melons, each sitting in a cradle of thin shredded paper, each individually wrapped in a soft green mesh. There is also a card. The gift costs, conservatively, twelve thousand yen. My mother has never asked for melons. The friend does not know if my mother even likes melons. The melons arrive anyway, every July, and every July my mother sends, within three weeks, a carefully chosen counter-gift of roughly equivalent value.

This is ochūgen — the Japanese midsummer gift-giving season. Its winter counterpart, oseibo, runs through December. Together they are the twin pillars of Japanese adult gift obligation, and they are, for a word like mottainai, a deeply awkward place to land.

The structure of the ritual

Ochūgen and oseibo are not celebratory gifts. They are gratitude gifts — a structured, twice-yearly acknowledgment of people who have helped you in the preceding six months. The recipients are usually a subset of: your spouse's parents, your own teacher or mentor, your workplace superior, a doctor who has treated a family member, a family friend who has done something substantial for you. The sender is expected to choose a gift appropriate to the relationship's intensity, priced roughly within a known band — three thousand to ten thousand yen for most relationships, more for hierarchically weightier ones.

Department stores run an entire industrial season around these two months. Isetan, Takashimaya, Mitsukoshi — each operates a dedicated ochūgen counter from mid-June, and an oseibo counter from early November. The catalogues are thick. The delivery infrastructure is enormous. The most popular categories — beer sets, fruit boxes, wagyū, dried seafood, specialty oils — move in quantities that genuinely move the country's logistics grid. This is not a boutique tradition. It is a circulatory system.

The obligation problem

The obvious difficulty, from a mottainai perspective, is that a ritual built on obligation produces gifts the recipient did not ask for. Ochūgen and oseibo do not ask the giver to know the recipient's preferences. They ask the giver to signal gratitude within a culturally legible format. The recipient may have no use for a box of beer. They may be diabetic and unable to eat a fruit set. They may already have, in the cupboard from last July, an unopened bottle of expensive oil they will not open this July either.

My mother's melons are, most years, fine. She likes melon. She eats one of the two over the course of a week and gives the second to a neighbour. The system works. But I have seen it fail. I have seen hams sit in refrigerators until they were binned. I have watched a friend's father open his fourth bottle of identical premium whisky in a single oseibo season, three of them still sealed, and shrug. Mottainai was the word in the room. Mottainai was also not actionable. He was not going to refuse the gifts; the refusal would itself be a larger failure of the relationship the gifts encoded.

This is the tension the essay's title names. Mottainai and gift-giving live, in Japan, in mutual irritation. The ethic asks us not to waste. The ritual produces, by design, a structural quantity of waste. Both have survived, side by side, for generations.

How the system tries to manage the tension

The Japanese response to this tension has been pragmatic rather than philosophical. Over the past twenty years, several adaptations have quietly spread.

The first is the catalogue gift. Instead of sending a specific item, the giver sends a catalogue from which the recipient can choose. The price band is encoded; the selection is the recipient's. This preserves the obligation structure while collapsing the preference problem. Catalogue gifts now represent, by some department-store estimates, close to a third of ochūgen and oseibo volume.

The second is the consumable preference. Mid-century oseibo often included household durables — tea sets, linens, serving dishes — which piled up in cupboards. Contemporary oseibo has drifted heavily toward consumables: food, drink, cosmetics that will be used up. The shift reduces the long tail of unused objects. A bottle of oil that gets opened in March is not mottainai. A tea set that sits in a box for thirty years is.

The third, more recent adaptation is the honest refusal letter. Among closer friends and some younger professional relationships, a formal letter declining the gift obligation — please do not send me anything this year; let us instead simplify our relationship to its emotional content — has become quietly acceptable. It remains rare in hierarchical contexts (a junior cannot send this letter to a senior without damage), but in horizontal friendships it is now usable. My mother and her Hokkaido friend have not chosen this path. Some of my cousins have, with theirs.

What gets given well

When ochūgen and oseibo work — when the gift lands, gets used, and does not become a mottainai problem — there are usually a few shared features. The gift is a consumable, to be finished within the season. The quantity is modest enough to be used without strain. The packaging is substantial but recyclable; glass, wood, and washi paper, rather than excess plastic. The giver has made a small effort, visible to the recipient, to match something specific about them — a kind of tea they are known to drink, a region they have connections to, a preference they have mentioned years ago.

The principle underneath all of this is not complicated. A good gift reduces the mottainai friction by matching more closely what the recipient would have bought for themselves. The closer the match, the smaller the waste-risk. The ritual does not reward perfection — perfection would border on presumption — but it rewards effort, and effort tends to produce matches that are good enough for the gift to be used.

The department store's role

I want to be honest about the commercial layer. Japanese department stores have, for a century, benefited enormously from these two seasons. Their ochūgen and oseibo counters are the anchor of their summer and winter commercial calendars. They are not neutral parties in the ritual; they shape it, through catalogue selection, through price banding, through the way staff advise uncertain customers.

The shaping is not entirely cynical. Department store buyers are, in my experience, thoughtful people who have spent their careers inside the ritual and understand its mottainai problem. Category shifts toward consumables and catalogues have come partly from their internal buying decisions. But the stores are also, unavoidably, selling. A store that advised every customer to not send a gift this year would not last a season. The ritual is propped up, in part, by the commercial infrastructure that needs it to continue.

This matters because the ritual's inertia is not only social. It is also economic. You can feel the difference when you try to opt out. The social friction is modest; the commercial friction is what actually keeps most people dialling the department store delivery hotline in late June.

A personal adjustment

My own adjustment — not a prescription, just my own — has been to narrow the list and deepen the attention. I send three ochūgen gifts and four oseibo gifts per year. Each recipient gets something I have actually chosen, not catalogue-delegated, to match something specific about them. The melons, when I send them, are the ones I have tasted myself and know to be good. The tea, when I send tea, is from a shop in Kagurazaka whose owner I have known for twelve years and whose house blend I have watched change across three seasons.

This adjustment does not reduce my outlay much; the per-gift price is similar to the department store band. What it does is reduce, as close to zero as I can manage, the mottainai risk on my end of the ritual. The recipient eats the melons. The tea is drunk. The relationship is honoured without the cupboard tax.

For small printed items I send alongside some of these gifts — a handwritten card, occasionally a small paper print of a seasonal plant — I use the Mindful Yen Etsy shop. If you are looking for quiet, washi-based card stock for this kind of gift-writing, it is there; if you are looking for a mass-market greeting card, it is not, and I would steer you elsewhere.

What a foreign reader should take from this

If you are reading this from outside Japan and wondering whether ochūgen and oseibo are a practice worth importing, my honest answer is: not as a ritual. A ritual without its social fabric is a costume. What is worth importing is the underlying idea — that twice a year, you stop and mark, materially, the people whose help has shaped your life. You do not need catalogues. You do not need department stores. You need a list of three to seven people and a willingness to choose, carefully, a small thing each of them will actually use.

Done that way, the practice is mottainai-compatible almost by definition. The gift matches the recipient. The waste tail is short. The obligation does not calcify. This is, I think, what ochūgen and oseibo were trying to be before they became industries. It is what they still are, in the hands of people who remember.

A final scene

My mother, this July, opened the year's melon on a Thursday evening with my father and me at the kitchen table. She cut it into six careful slices. We ate three each, slowly, with a small spoon. She called the Hokkaido friend the next morning and told her the melon had been perfect. The friend, I could hear from across the room, sounded pleased. The ritual held. The fruit did what fruit is for. The box and the green mesh were folded and kept for next year's counter-gift.

That is what ochūgen can look like on a good day. It is not always that. But it can be, and the difference between its good days and its wasteful ones is almost entirely the attention of the two people on either end.
