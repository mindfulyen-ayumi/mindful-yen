The Kakeibo Categories I Actually Use (Eight, Not Four)

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The canonical kakeibo structure, as Hani published it and as most English-language explainers faithfully reproduce it, uses four categories: survival (needs), optional (wants), culture, and extra. I have written earlier essays — on the Japanese budget method, on the four questions, on the history of Hani Motoko — that treat the four categories as given. In my own ledger I do not use the four categories. I use eight. This essay is an honest account of why, and a quiet pushback against the canon.

I want to be careful here. I am not suggesting that the four-category structure is wrong, or that newcomers to kakeibo should start with anything other than it. The four categories have survived 120 years because they are a remarkably good teaching structure for beginners — broad enough to catch everything, narrow enough to force honest sorting. My argument is not against them. My argument is that for practitioners who have kept kakeibo for several years, the four categories begin to hide patterns that a slightly more granular structure reveals. Eight is not the only right answer. It is the number my own practice has settled on, after trying both five and six, and after reading a fair amount of the more sophisticated Japanese kakeibo literature that does not always make it into English translation.

The problem with four

The four categories have two quiet failures, which become visible after about two years of careful logging.

The first failure is that the survival category collapses very different kinds of essential spending into one column. Groceries, rent, utilities, transit, and basic household goods all live in the same place, but they behave differently. Rent is fixed and non-responsive to your behaviour. Groceries are variable and highly responsive. Treating them identically — as many canonical templates do — means the survival column becomes a mixture of signal and noise, and the signal gets lost.

The second failure is that the optional category is too broad to produce useful reflection. A takeaway coffee and a new winter coat and a dinner with a friend all live in the same column. The reflection that produces real behavioural change requires more separation than this. A month in which you spent heavily on clothes is a very different month from a month in which you spent heavily on social meals, and the fourth kakeibo question — how can I improve next month? — cannot be answered well if the underlying data has collapsed the two.

Japanese practitioners have known this for decades. The Fujin no Tomo editorial guidance I mentioned in the Hani essay has, since at least the 1970s, suggested that experienced practitioners subdivide the canonical four into finer categories matched to their own households. The four-category version is the introductory form. The richer categorisation is the mature form.

The eight I actually use

After several years of iteration, my own ledger has settled on the following eight. I list them in the order they appear on my monthly intake page, which is roughly the order of fixedness — from the least responsive to the most responsive to behaviour.

One: Shelter. Rent, and the small cluster of rent-adjacent fixed costs — building fees, mandatory renter's insurance, a parking fee if I had a car, which I do not. This is the most fixed part of the ledger, and it does not really need to be tracked monthly; it needs to be tracked annually. But I keep it in the monthly intake because it is the first thing to subtract from income, and seeing it every month reinforces how much of the envelope is already claimed before I have made any decisions at all.

Two: Utilities and transit. Electricity, water, gas, internet, mobile, the monthly transit pass. These are technically variable but move slowly and rarely respond to in-month decisions. I keep them separate from shelter because they are negotiable on longer timescales — a decision to change provider, to downgrade a plan — and I want the category to stay visible for those decisions. On a month-to-month basis, I barely look at this column.

Three: Groceries and daily food. The first column that actually moves in response to behaviour. Home cooking ingredients, daily grocery runs, the occasional onigiri at the station when I am short on time. This column is large enough to matter and responsive enough to be worth watching. I split it from restaurants and social meals, which belong in a different column with a different behavioural character.

Four: Social meals. Dinners with friends, lunches out that are social rather than purely functional, occasional drinks at a local bar. This column behaves nothing like groceries. It clusters around weeks when I have more social obligation, drops sharply when I do not, and is probably the single column most responsive to how much energy I have for company in a given month. Separating it from groceries made the pattern visible. It was not visible when both lived in the canonical survival or optional bucket.

Five: Books and culture. Books, magazines, the occasional cinema, museums, concerts, a subscription to a literary journal. This is the canonical culture category from Hani's original structure, largely unmodified. It is the column I watch most carefully, because it is the most reliable predictor of the quality of my year. A year with an underfed culture column is a year that feels narrower when I read the reflections back.

Six: Home and personal care. Clothes, small household goods, toiletries, occasional furniture, haircuts. This is the column that absorbs most of what the canonical optional category was meant to hold, minus food. It is seasonal — the autumn and spring spikes I described in the year of kakeibo essay live here — and it rewards close watching around those transitions.

Seven: Gifts and obligations. The category that does not appear in most canonical kakeibo templates, and that deserves its own column in any Japanese household. Wedding gifts, funeral gifts, New Year gifts, the small obligatory purchases that mark the Japanese social calendar, the omiyage bought for colleagues after a trip. These expenses are real, recurring, and non-discretionary in a cultural sense, but they are not shelter and they are not survival. They need their own column, and the canonical four does not give them one.

Eight: Extra. Hani's original extra column, preserved. The catch-all for genuinely one-off spending that belongs nowhere else — a medical expense, an unexpected repair, a travel cost outside the ordinary pattern. This column should stay small most months and be allowed to spike occasionally without alarm. It is the safety valve of the ledger.

What the eight-category structure reveals

The shift from four to eight produced, in my own practice, three visible improvements that did not appear when I tried six or seven-category variants.

First, the separation of groceries from social meals made visible the behavioural pattern I described above — that social-meal spending tracks energy levels, not income, and that a lean social month is not a sign of virtuous frugality but often a sign of depletion. Seeing this in its own column changed how I interpret low-spending months. Sometimes the right response to a lean social column is not satisfaction but a gentle re-engagement with the people I care about.

Second, the separation of shelter from utilities made visible how fixed my life actually is on long timescales. Roughly sixty percent of my monthly envelope is claimed before I make any in-month decisions. This is a useful number to confront periodically. It disciplines what I expect the month's decisions to be able to change. Most of the envelope is already decided; the work of the ledger is on the remaining forty percent.

Third, the inclusion of gifts and obligations as its own column stopped the silent drift this category had been causing inside the extra column. In the four-category structure, gifts and obligations would land in extra, which made extra look much noisier than it actually was. Pulling gifts into their own column let me see that extra was, in fact, quite stable, and that what I had been reading as extra-column volatility was actually gift-column seasonality. The seasonal cluster of weddings in autumn and condolences in early spring — the ordinary shape of Japanese adult life — has its own rhythm that deserves visibility.

When to ignore the canon

The honest answer to when you should use eight categories instead of four is: after you have used four for at least two years. The canonical four is the right introductory structure for the same reason Hani settled on it in 1904 — it is broad enough to catch everything and narrow enough to force honest sorting, which is what a beginner needs. Jumping to eight too early produces a ledger that is technically more granular but behaviourally less disciplined, because the practitioner has not yet built the habit of sorting honestly at the coarser level.

After two years, the four-category structure starts to feel cramped. Patterns that you know are there become invisible because they have been averaged out across overly broad columns. That is the moment to refine. Eight is one good destination; some practitioners go to six, some to ten. The number matters less than the principle, which is: add a column when a pattern in your own life is not being revealed by the current structure, and not before.

Mechanical notes

I keep the eight categories in both my paper journal and my Notion workspace, with the same column order in both. The paper journal uses a small grid at the top of each daily page, with one tick per category; the Notion database uses a single-select property with the same eight values. Keeping the categories identical across media avoids a re-keying burden during the Saturday review I described in the Saturday reset essay.

The monthly reflection still uses the four canonical questions, unchanged. Adding categories does not require changing the questions; the questions operate over whatever category structure the practitioner has chosen. Hani's architecture is deliberately separable at this layer. The questions are the invariant; the categories are the local variable.

For readers who want a printed journal that supports a richer category structure without locking you into a specific set, the Mindful Yen kakeibo journal provides blank category labels you can fill in yourself for each month, which I think is the honest way to handle this question in a printed product.

A closing caution

There is a failure mode worth naming. Some practitioners, encountering the idea that eight categories might reveal more than four, will immediately jump to twelve or sixteen, on the theory that more granularity must mean more insight. This is incorrect. Beyond about eight or nine categories the marginal value of each additional column drops sharply, and beyond about twelve the structure begins to produce the opposite of its intent — a ledger so finely sliced that no single category carries enough signal to be worth reflecting on. The sweet spot, for most households, is somewhere between six and nine. My eight is not canonical; it is my own local optimum. Yours will be different, but it will probably sit in the same range.

The four-category structure will not steer you wrong in your first two years. After that, let your ledger tell you which columns are hiding something, and split those. The eight I use are simply what that process produced in my own household. The process — not the destination — is what the practice asks for.

For occasional notes on category experiments readers have written in about, and on my own ongoing tinkering with the structure, I keep an irregular thread on the Mindful Yen Substack. There is no orthodoxy here. There is only what each household's honest attention reveals, over time, about the shape of its own spending.
