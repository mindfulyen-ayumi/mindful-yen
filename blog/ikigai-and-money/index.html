Ikigai and Money Are Less Connected Than You Think

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

A reader wrote to me last month from Austin, Texas. She had recently quit a job she described as "soul-eroding" and was halfway through a self-designed sabbatical, with about nine months of runway in the bank. Her question, more or less, was this: how do I find an ikigai that pays. She had read four books on the subject. They had all, in different registers, promised her that the right answer would close the gap between her bank balance and her purpose. None of them had told her how to do it. She was, understandably, beginning to feel that the failure was hers.

I want to write back to her, and to anyone who has carried a version of the same question, with what I think is the more honest answer. Ikigai and money are less connected than the English-language conversation about ikigai has led you to believe. They are not unrelated. They are simply not the same conversation, and treating them as one is what has made the question feel unanswerable.

What Mieko Kamiya actually wrote about

The serious Japanese book on ikigai is Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966), by the psychiatrist Mieko Kamiya. I have written elsewhere about the difference between Kamiya's account and the four-circle Venn diagram that has come to stand in for it in English. What is worth saying again here is what Kamiya did not write about. She did not write about salary. She did not write about career trajectory. She did not write about the alignment of one's vocation with one's compensation. The book is roughly 250 pages long in the original Japanese, and money — in any sense the modern career-design literature would recognise — appears in almost none of them.

This is not because Kamiya was naïve about economics. She was a practising psychiatrist for decades, and the patients she drew her thinking from at the leprosarium on Nagashima had had their economic lives almost entirely removed from them. What she observed, watching people live with very little, was that the felt sense of a life worth living — what she called ikigai-kan — was strikingly independent of the external circumstances she had been trained to expect would govern it. People with almost nothing were finding their way to it. People with a great deal were sometimes not. The connection she was tracing between meaning and material life was not the one her readers, sixty years later, would assume.

The Marc Winn diagram quietly inserted money into the centre

The Venn diagram most English speakers now associate with ikigai was created in 2014 by the British entrepreneur Marc Winn, building on an earlier purpose diagram by the Spanish writer Andrés Zuzunaga (2011). Winn swapped the centre word from propósito to ikigai. The diagram has four circles: what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and — crucially — what you can be paid for.

That fourth circle is where the trouble enters. Kamiya's ikigai does not require it. The Okinawan elders interviewed by Dan Buettner, when they used the word, did not require it either; their answers were about teaching karate, tending gardens, going to community gatherings. The fourth circle is a Western career-design assumption that was bolted onto a Japanese word in 2014, and has been quietly distorting the conversation ever since. The Japanese psychologist Ken Mogi, in his 2017 book Awakening Your Ikigai, makes the same observation in different words: ikigai, in Japan, simply is not a category that includes "what the market will pay for."

Two questions, separated

The reason the Austin reader's question feels unanswerable is that it is actually two questions stacked on top of each other, pretending to be one.

The first question is: what makes my life feel worth living. This is the ikigai question. It is answered, in the Japanese tradition, by the small daily noticing — the cup of tea drunk slowly, the conversation with a colleague, the patient one helped, the sentence that came out the way one wanted. The answers tend to cluster, over months, around a few quiet themes that are recognisably one's own. Almost none of them require an external market.

The second question is: how will I pay rent. This is the livelihood question. It is answered by labour markets, skills, location, luck, household structure, prior savings. It is a serious question and it deserves a serious answer, but the Japanese word for it is not ikigai. It is closer to shokugyō (occupation) or seikei (livelihood). They are different words for different reasons.

What the Western Venn diagram has done is collapse these two questions into one and then ask its readers to feel like failures when the collapsed question turns out to be very hard. Most lives, examined honestly, do not produce a single activity that satisfies all four circles. The ones that do are often products of luck, inheritance, or specific historical timing rather than personal virtue. To tell a thirty-five-year-old in a perfectly decent job that they have failed because their work is not also their passion and the world's pressing need is, I think, a quiet form of cruelty.

What the separation actually permits

If you let the two questions come apart, several useful things become possible.

You can have a job that is, frankly, a job. Many of the most contented people I know in Tokyo work in roles that no career-coach would describe as a calling — administration in a small office in Yotsuya, a desk at a paper company in Jimbocho, a part-time post in a bookshop. Their ikigai lives elsewhere: a granddaughter, a reading practice, a long walk on Sunday morning, a small garden on a balcony. The job pays the rent. The ikigai keeps them upright. The two are allowed to be separate, and the separation produces less friction than the attempted merger does.

You can also, conversely, have a vocation that you love and that does not pay well, without it being a moral failure. The retired tailor I have written about before — his hands are not what they were, his shop is gone, he never made very much money in any of the decades he ran it — describes his old craft as one of his ikigai with no embarrassment about its modest economics. The economics were one thing. The work was another. He is at peace with both.

And you can, if you are someone whose work and ikigai do happen to overlap — some artists, some teachers, some carers, some doctors — receive that overlap as the lucky accident it is rather than the natural endpoint of a properly conducted life. Most people do not get this overlap. The ones who do should not be using their good fortune as evidence that everyone else has done their planning wrong.

The household economics of a separated life

If the two questions are genuinely separate, the practical implication is that the livelihood question deserves its own quiet, competent practice — the same careful attention one gives to ikigai, applied to a different domain. In Japan this practice has, for the last 120 years, often taken the form of kakeibo, the household ledger introduced by Motoko Hani in 1904. Kakeibo does not promise to align your income with your soul. It simply asks you, every month, to look at what came in, what went out, and what you would like to change. It is the livelihood question, asked seriously and slowly.

What I have noticed, in my own household and in the households of friends, is that when the livelihood question is held competently — when the budget is honest, the savings are real, the runway is known — the ikigai question becomes much easier to ask without panic. You are not trying to extract a cheque from your meaning-making. You are letting your meaning-making be itself, while a separate, slower practice handles the rent. The two streams run in parallel and they support each other precisely by not being the same stream.

What I would say to the Austin reader

If she were in Kagurazaka with me over a pot of tea, I think I would say something like this. The question you are trying to answer — how do I find an ikigai that pays — is two questions wearing one coat. Take the coat off. Ask the ikigai question on its own terms, with no economic pressure attached: what, today, made the day worth its weight. Let the answer be small. Let it have nothing to do with your career. Let it accumulate over a month, then three months, into a small set of quiet themes that are recognisably yours.

Then, on a different evening, with a different notebook, ask the livelihood question. What skills can I sell. What does my market pay. What do I need per month to keep the lights on and the savings growing. What is my runway. What is my next step. This is not the same question, and it does not deserve to be answered with the same word.

Some of you, in time, will find that the livelihood question and the ikigai question begin to cross-fertilise. A theme from the ikigai journal will turn out to be saleable. A skill from the livelihood column will turn out to be quietly meaningful. The crossings, when they happen, are gifts. They are not the goal. The goal is to live both questions honestly. If the crossings come, accept them. If they do not, you have lost nothing — you still have a life worth living and a household that pays its rent, which is, by any sane measure, enough.

Final thought

The English-language ikigai industry has spent twelve years selling a diagram that promises money and meaning will eventually meet in a small almond-shaped centre, if only you keep arranging the circles. The Japanese tradition has spent sixty years quietly observing that they sometimes do and often do not, and that this is fine. The honest move, I think, is to stop asking ikigai to be your financial plan. It was never trying to be. Let it be the smaller, quieter thing it actually is, and give the money question the separate, patient practice it deserves. Both will go better.
