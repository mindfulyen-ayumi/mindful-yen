The Slow Email Practice: For People Who Reply Too Fast

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The laptop on my kitchen table this morning is open to a single email I drafted last night and did not send. The draft is six lines long, addressed to a magazine editor in London who had asked me a slightly difficult question about a piece I am writing for her. My first reply, written at half past nine in the evening, ran to nineteen lines and contained two apologies that did not need to be made and one defensive paragraph I would have regretted within an hour. I closed the laptop, went to bed, and rewrote the message this morning while the kettle was boiling. The new version is six lines, has no apologies, and answers the question. I will send it after my second cup of tea.

This essay is about that overnight gap — the small practice of not sending the first version of anything that matters — and about a slow email habit that has, over the past four years, made my working life calmer by an amount I would not have predicted.

The problem is not the email; it is the speed

I worked in magazine publishing for most of my twenties, in an office where the cultural expectation was that emails were answered within the hour during business hours and within four hours outside them. I was, by any office metric, an excellent emailer. I replied fast. I replied thoroughly. I cleared my inbox several times a day. I was also, by my late twenties, deeply tired in a way that did not respond to weekends, and a substantial fraction of that tiredness was attributable to the constant low-grade vigilance of being available.

It took me longer than it should have to notice that the speed of my replies was producing a separate problem: the quality of my replies was lower than it would have been if I had waited. Fast replies were reactive replies. They mirrored the emotional temperature of the incoming message. An anxious email produced an anxious reply; an irritated email produced a defensive one; an urgent email produced agreements I would later wish I had thought about for an extra hour. The speed was not professionalism. The speed was a kind of compulsive responsiveness that the office had taught me to perform.

The slow email practice began as a personal experiment. It has become something close to a discipline.

The four small rules

The practice has, over time, settled into four rules. They are not original. They are simply the four that have stuck.

1. Two windows a day, at fixed times

I open email twice a day. Once at half past nine in the morning, after I have done about an hour of writing. Once at half past four in the afternoon, after the day's main work is done. Each window lasts about forty-five minutes. Outside those windows the email application is closed. Notifications are off. The phone, if it must be on the desk, faces down.

The first month of this was uncomfortable. I noticed how often, throughout the day, I had been reaching to check the inbox. The reach was not driven by any specific expected message; it was a habit of small relief, the way some people open the fridge without being hungry. By the third month the reach had quieted on its own. By the sixth month I no longer thought about the closed inbox during the hours it was closed. The world had not ended.

2. Drafts overnight for anything difficult

Any reply that involves a delicate negotiation, a refusal, a correction of a misunderstanding, or any sentence that begins with the words "to be honest" gets written, then closed, then sent the next morning. The overnight gap is the single highest-value habit I have built around email. The morning version is almost always shorter, calmer, and more accurate than the evening version would have been. The few times I have skipped the gap because the message felt urgent, I have, on average, regretted the sent version within a day.

For correspondents who are not used to this — particularly correspondents in faster cultures — the small lag is sometimes confusing at first. It almost never causes a real problem. What it causes, more often, is a small upward shift in the quality of the conversation, because a thoughtful reply received the next morning is a different artefact than an instant reply received the evening before, and most working adults can tell the difference even if they could not articulate it.

3. Short bodies, no apologies for slowness

The slow email practice produces short emails. This is a side effect, not a goal. When you reply once a day rather than five times, you stop using emails to think out loud. You think first; you write second. The result is messages that average four to seven lines instead of fifteen to twenty.

The other small change is the disappearance of apology language. I used to begin slow replies with "so sorry for the delay" or "apologies for the slow turnaround." I have stopped. The apologies were a tax I was paying on a behaviour that did not require apologising for. A reply within twenty-four hours during business days is not late by any reasonable working definition; calling it late was a small daily concession to a culture I had decided to opt out of. The apology language has not been replaced with an explanation of my email schedule. It has simply gone, and no correspondent has ever pointed out its absence.

4. The end-of-day inbox glance, but no replies

At the end of the working day, around half past five, I glance at the inbox for two minutes. I do not reply. The glance is purely to know whether anything genuinely time-sensitive has arrived — the rare case (a sick family member, a real emergency at a publication) where waiting until tomorrow morning would be wrong. In four years I have triggered an out-of-window reply perhaps a dozen times. The glance reassures me that nothing is on fire; it does not pull me back into the inbox; and it ends the working day cleanly.

What the practice is not

The slow email practice is not an inbox-zero method. I have a substantial backlog of older messages I have decided not to reply to, and the backlog does not bother me. It is not a productivity hack; it does not produce more output. If anything it produces marginally less output, distributed more evenly. It is not a refusal of email; I send and receive plenty. It is not a stunt. I do not announce the schedule in autoresponders or signatures. I simply work this way, quietly, and most of my correspondents have never noticed.

It is also not, importantly, a moral position. I do not think people who reply quickly are doing something wrong. The fast email culture I came out of was producing real value for real people, and the speed was part of that value for some of them. The slow practice is a personal answer to a personal problem — the steady drain of compulsive availability — not a recommendation that everyone should hold their replies until morning.

The wabi-sabi reading of slow email

The wabi-sabi sensibility shows up here in a slightly indirect way. The tradition has always preferred the considered to the spontaneous, the made-by-hand to the produced-by-machine, the object that bears the marks of attention to the object that has been through a frictionless process. An email written quickly and sent immediately is a frictionless object. An email written, set aside, and rewritten the next morning is a small handmade object — not handmade in the literal sense, but in the sense of bearing the marks of a human pause.

Sōtō Zen has a concept it calls aji — flavour, presence, depth — that I have referenced in other essays in connection with old objects. The same concept, applied to written communication, is roughly: a thing that has been allowed to sit. The overnight email has aji. The instant reply, almost by definition, does not. This is not just an aesthetic claim. The recipient can feel the difference, even when they could not name it.

There is also, sitting underneath all of this, the older Japanese epistolary tradition — the long correspondence between Heian-era courtiers, the careful Edo-era letters between merchants and scholars — in which a reply written and sent the same day would have been considered an embarrassment. The cultural baseline for a written reply, for most of Japanese history, was several days. The instant reply is a recent invention, made possible by a technology that arrived faster than the etiquette around it. The slow email practice is, partly, an attempt to restore a small piece of the older etiquette to the new medium.

Three months in: what to expect

Readers who have tried the practice have shared a fairly consistent arc with me, and my own experience matched it.

The first two weeks are uncomfortable. The closed inbox produces a low background anxiety that is mostly proprioceptive — the hand keeps reaching for a phone that is not there. A few correspondents will notice the lag and one or two may ask if everything is all right.

By the second month, the discomfort has receded. The two daily windows feel sufficient. You discover that you have been doing something else with the hours you used to spend in the inbox, even if you cannot quite say what.

By the third month, the practice has become invisible. You stop thinking about it. You also begin to notice — and this is the part most readers report with some surprise — that the quality of your sent emails has noticeably improved. They are shorter, clearer, less defensive, more accurate. People you correspond with regularly have started to write back in slightly more considered ways themselves. The conversation, on both sides, has slowed by a small margin and improved by a larger one.

By the sixth month, slow email is just how you do email. The earlier mode looks, in retrospect, slightly frantic, the way an old photograph of yourself sometimes looks slightly more tired than you remember being.

A practical starting kit, if you would like one

If you would like to try the practice, the simplest version is two changes. Set two times in the day at which you will open email. Write a one-sentence note to yourself, on paper, taped near your desk, that says nothing here is on fire. That is the whole starting kit. The rest accumulates on its own.

For correspondence that genuinely benefits from a longer-form medium — readers who want a weekly letter rather than an inbox exchange — I keep a quiet weekly Substack from Tokyo, which sometimes turns into a small running record of practices like this one. The newsletter goes out once a week, never more, and never with notifications.

I will send the London editor's reply now. The kettle has been off for ten minutes. The sentence I rewrote this morning is a sentence I will not regret tomorrow. That is the modest, compounding reward of the practice — a small increase in the proportion of sent messages that you would still send a week later.
