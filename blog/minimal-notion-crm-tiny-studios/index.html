A Minimal Notion CRM for Tiny Studios

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The word "CRM" is, for a one-person studio, almost too large for the thing it should describe. A small editorial practice, a freelance illustrator, a translator working with a handful of returning publishers — none of these need a customer relationship management system in the corporate sense. What they need is a small, durable memory of the people they work with. Names. A sentence about how the relationship started. The last time anyone heard from anyone. That is most of it.

This essay is about the four-property Notion database I use as the studio's CRM, and which, after eighteen months of running it, I would now suggest to almost any tiny studio without hesitation. If you would like to start from a working version, you can try the Notion workspace I use here →. The CRM page lives inside the wider workspace described in the solo creators essay.

Why most CRM templates do not fit a tiny studio

The CRM templates I bought and tried — one of which I wrote about in the paid templates review — were designed around a sales funnel. Lead, qualified, proposal, won, lost. The pipeline was the point. For a small studio without a sales process — which is to say, for most one-person studios — the pipeline is a structure imposed on a thing that is not a pipeline. Most of my client relationships did not begin with a lead. They began with an email from a stranger, an introduction at a Jimbocho bookshop, a reader letter that gradually became a working friendship.

Pipeline-shaped CRMs flatten that texture. Every relationship has to be funnelled into a stage, and the stages do not describe what is actually happening. After a month of trying, I found I was writing fictional stage values into a database in order to make the database work, which is the moment any database has stopped earning its keep.

What a tiny-studio CRM is genuinely for

The honest job of a small studio's CRM is not sales tracking. It is memory. Specifically, it is the memory of two questions: who are the people I am in some kind of working relationship with, and when did I last meaningfully speak with each of them. Almost every useful thing a tiny studio's CRM does is downstream of those two answers. Everything else can be discarded.

Once that is the premise, the database almost designs itself. A row per person. A field for who they are, in a sentence. A field for when we were last in touch. A field for whatever the next gentle thing is, if there is one. That is the CRM.

The four properties

The current database has these four properties and no others.

  • Name — the person, or occasionally a small organisation if the relationship is institutional rather than personal.
  • Who they are — one sentence. Plain text. Written when the row is first created, edited rarely. The sentence is for me, three months from now, when I have forgotten the context.
  • Last contact — a date. Updated whenever there is a real exchange. I do not log every email; I log conversations.
  • Next gentle thing — a short note, usually empty, occasionally containing one sentence about a small thing I would like to do — send a book, write a thank-you, follow up on a half-finished idea.

That is the entire schema. There are no statuses. There are no tags. There are no relations to other databases. The CRM is, deliberately, an island. The reason will become clearer below.

Why no pipeline

The pipeline is the first thing I removed from every CRM template I tried, and the first thing I would advise any tiny studio to refuse to add. A pipeline has the structural property of treating a relationship as a thing that should be moving toward an outcome. For a sales team this is true. For a one-person studio's working relationships, it is mostly not true. A reader who occasionally writes me a thoughtful letter is not "in" any stage. A returning translation client is not "qualified." A friend-of-a-friend who occasionally sends an editorial referral is not anywhere on a funnel.

A pipeline forces every row to fit a shape. The shape is wrong for most of the rows. The cost of wearing the wrong shape, every time you open the database, is small but real, and it accumulates. After a month of pipeline-flavoured CRMs I had stopped opening the CRM. After replacing the pipeline with a single sentence per person, I open the CRM most weeks.

Why no relations

The CRM is not connected to the editorial calendar, the reader inbox, the kakeibo ledger, or any other database in the workspace. This is intentional. Relations are powerful and they are also expensive — every relation is a small claim that two databases need to know about each other, and most pairs of databases do not. The CRM and the editorial calendar, in particular, want different cadences and different mental modes. Linking them encourages me to think of relationships as inputs to projects, which is the wrong frame for most of the people in the CRM.

The reader inbox, which I described in the solo creators essay, is the closest neighbour to the CRM and the one I most often considered linking. I decided not to. Most reader letters are from people I will hear from once and never again, and those people do not belong in the CRM. The handful of readers who become correspondents over time get added to the CRM by hand, with their permission, when the relationship has obviously crossed that threshold.

How I use the CRM in practice

The rhythm is gentle. Once a week, on Saturday morning after the weekly review described in the weekly review essay, I open the CRM and do two things. First I sort by last contact, ascending. The names at the top of the list are the people I have not heard from in the longest time. I read those names. Most weeks I do nothing about them, which is correct. Some weeks one of them prompts a short note — a hello, a passing-along of an article, a thank-you that I did not get around to at the time. The note takes ten minutes and does, sometimes, real work.

Second, I look at the next gentle thing field. Most rows are empty. The few that are not become small Saturday-morning tasks. None of them are urgent. None of them are billable. They are the kind of small relational labour that a corporate CRM would not have words for and that a tiny studio depends on entirely.

What changes after a year

The CRM had perhaps thirty rows after the first three months. It now has slightly over a hundred. The growth has been slow and untroubled, because nothing about the database creates pressure to add rows. A row joins the CRM when a relationship has done at least two things — usually two exchanges separated by enough time that they were not part of the same conversation. Below that threshold, the person belongs in the reader inbox or in regular email, not in the CRM.

Most of the rows are quiet. A handful are active. A few have not been touched in a year and probably will not be again, and that is also fine. The CRM is not a list of obligations. It is a small map of the people the studio is in some kind of relationship with, and most relationships, like most weather, are not currently doing anything.

What the CRM has changed about the studio's working life

Two things, both small and both real. The first is that I am noticeably better at not losing touch with people I had genuinely intended to stay in touch with. The Saturday sort surfaces names I would otherwise have allowed to drift, and a ten-minute note, sent on a quiet morning, prevents the months-becoming-years effect that I used to find embarrassing in my own correspondence.

The second is that I am noticeably less anxious about networking, which I have always found slightly draining as a concept. The CRM makes networking, as a separate activity, more or less unnecessary. The relationships I have are visible in one place; the relationships I do not have are not haunting me from outside the database. I do not need to "build a network." I need to occasionally remember the people I already know.

Why a separate tool would be wrong

There are dedicated CRM tools designed for freelancers — small, well-made, often quite affordable. I have looked at several. For a tiny studio, the cost is not the subscription. The cost is the second tool in the workflow. Every tool I add to the studio's daily life is a tool I have to remember, open, and maintain. A four-property database in the same workspace as the editorial calendar and the kakeibo ledger has the structural advantage of being where I already am. The dedicated CRM, however well-designed, would be somewhere I would have to go.

This is the same logic that runs through the rest of the workspace, and which I have written about in the Notion vs Obsidian essay. The right tool is the one whose structural fit is closest to the daily reality of the studio. For a tiny studio's CRM, four properties in Notion is closer than any dedicated tool I have seen.

What I would do differently if I were starting today

Almost nothing. The only adjustment I would make, looking back, is starting the CRM about six months earlier than I did. The first half-year of a small studio is when the most relationships are formed and the fewest are recorded, because everything is moving too quickly to feel like it needs a system. By the time I built the CRM, I had to reconstruct the first half-year from email, and the reconstruction was slightly inaccurate. If you are starting a tiny studio now, build the four-property CRM in the first month. There is nothing else to add. The whole setup takes about ten minutes.

Letters from readers about CRMs

"Don't you need a way to track invoices and proposals?" Yes, but not in the CRM. Invoices live in a separate invoicing service, with totals copied monthly into the kakeibo ledger. Proposals are usually a single document I save in the asset library. Combining these with the CRM is the move that converts a memory tool into a project tool, which is the wrong job for it.

"What about birthdays, anniversaries, follow-up reminders?" I do not track them in Notion. The few I want to remember live in the regular calendar, with a notification a day in advance. The CRM is for the persistent memory of who someone is. The calendar is for the time-bound prompts. They are different jobs.

"Could this work for a two-person studio?" Yes, with the addition of one property: whose contact. Two people in a studio almost always have asymmetric relationships with each contact, and the asymmetry is worth recording. Otherwise the structure transfers cleanly.

Final thought

A tiny studio does not need a CRM in the corporate sense. It needs a small, durable memory of the people it works with, and a quiet weekly hour to read that memory and notice who it has not heard from in a while. Four properties is enough. A pipeline is too many. The relationships do the rest of the work, as they always have, in their own time.

If you want the workspace, it is here. If you want longer letters about running a small studio without imitating a large one, the Mindful Yen Substack is where I write them, and the paper companions in the Etsy shop are where I keep the analogue half of the practice. The CRM, in either case, will be small enough to open on a Saturday morning, and that is most of what it needs to be.
