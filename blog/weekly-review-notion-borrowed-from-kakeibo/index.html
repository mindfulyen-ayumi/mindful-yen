A Weekly Review in Notion, Borrowed From Kakeibo

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Every Saturday morning, before I open email, I sit at the kitchen table with a pot of hojicha and answer four short questions about the week that has just ended. The questions are not, in any direct sense, mine. They are adapted from the four questions Motoko Hani asked at the close of every kakeibo month in 1904, and which I have written about at length in the kakeibo Notion template essay. What I did, eighteen months ago, was lift the structure out of the financial ledger and apply it to the working week. The result is the closest thing to a weekly review I have ever managed to keep up.

This essay is about that template — what the four questions became, why I run them in Notion rather than on paper, and what changed after a year of doing it. If you would like to start from a working version, you can try the Notion workspace I use here →. The template page itself is part of the wider workspace described in the solo creators essay.

Why most weekly reviews fail

I have, over the years, tried perhaps a dozen weekly review formats. Most were borrowed from productivity books and most lasted between three and seven weeks before I stopped opening them. The reason was almost always the same: the review asked me to inventory my week, and an inventory of an ordinary week of work is, frankly, dispiriting. Most weeks contain a small number of meaningful moments and a large amount of administration. Listing the administration in detail at the end of every Saturday is a remarkable way to make the week look smaller than it actually was.

The kakeibo monthly reflection has the opposite property. It does not ask you to inventory the month. It asks you four short questions that require you to think about the month rather than transcribe it. The numbers in the ledger are the inventory; the questions are the review. The separation matters. Once I noticed that, the structure transferred almost directly to the working week.

The four questions

The weekly version of Hani's four questions, after a year of small adjustments, is:

  • What did I make this week?
  • What did I let go of?
  • What surprised me?
  • What do I want less of next week?

Each question gets two or three sentences, written in plain text, in a single Notion database row whose title is the date of the Saturday. There is no rating. There is no scoring. There are no tags. The row has the date, the four answers, and nothing else. The whole template fits on a single screen and takes between forty and sixty minutes to complete, which is roughly the length of a pot of tea.

"What did I make this week?"

The first question is the one I expected to find easy and which has turned out to be the most useful. The answer is almost never what I would have guessed at the start of the week. A week I remember as quiet has, on inspection, produced a draft, two reader replies, a season-edit of the reading log, and a small revision to the kakeibo ledger structure. A week I remember as productive has, on inspection, produced an empty draft and several handsome calendar squares. Writing the answer down, weekly, is a slow correction to the systematic misjudgement most working people make about their own output.

"What did I let go of?"

The second question is the one I was most reluctant to keep, because it sounded faintly self-helpy. I kept it for a month as an experiment and have never removed it. It turns out that most weeks involve a small, deliberate giving-up — an essay idea I decided not to pursue, a reader request I decided not to act on, an administrative project I declined to start. Naming those gives them dignity. They stop being failures and start being decisions.

"What surprised me?"

The third question is the one that, more than any other, generates the next week's writing. Surprise is the single most reliable signal of an essay worth writing. If a thing surprised me on a Tuesday, it will surprise a reader some Saturday two months from now. The surprises that recur — the same kind of thing surprising me four weeks in a row — almost always become essays. The reading log essay, the dashboard essay, and the present essay all began as recurring entries in this field.

"What do I want less of next week?"

The fourth question is the one that felt most awkward to write at first. The default cultural script for a weekly review is to plan more — more goals, more commitments, more ambitious arcs. The question of what you want less of is structurally different. It assumes the week is already full and that the useful intervention is subtraction rather than addition. A year of asking it has, slowly, made my weeks smaller and the work inside them better.

Why Saturday morning, and why with tea

The timing matters. I tried Sunday evening for a few weeks and stopped. Sunday evening is too close to Monday; the review becomes a planning meeting in a thin disguise. I tried Friday afternoon and stopped. Friday afternoon is too close to the week's residue; the review becomes a complaint in a thin disguise. Saturday morning, with the week genuinely closed and the next one not yet visible, turns out to be the right place. The mood is reflective rather than transactional, which is what the four questions need.

The tea is not optional. It is, more accurately, a forty-minute timer that does not look like a timer. The pot lasts roughly the length of the review. When the pot is empty, the review is done. The constraint prevents the review from expanding to fill an entire morning, which it would otherwise reliably do.

How the template sits in the workspace

The weekly review database lives next to the monthly reflection database in the same Notion workspace. The two are linked: the monthly reflection's first long-text field — what I made this month — is informed by reading back the four most recent weekly reviews. The reading takes about ten minutes. It is, by a clear margin, the most useful ten minutes of the monthly reflection.

In practice this means the weekly review is doing two jobs. It is a Saturday-morning ritual on its own, and it is the source material for the monthly reflection. Without the weekly reviews, the monthly reflection becomes a memory test. With them, the monthly reflection becomes an act of reading.

Why Notion rather than a paper journal

For the kakeibo monthly reflection, I write the four answers by hand first and transcribe them. For the weekly review, I write directly into Notion. The reason is that the weekly review's value compounds through searchability and proximity. I want to be able to read fifty-two reviews back-to-back at the end of the year, in chronological order, without flipping through a notebook. I want to be able to filter by a phrase — "tired," "Jimbocho," "the inbox" — and find the weeks in which a pattern lived. Notion does this in two seconds. A paper journal cannot.

This is the inverse of the choice I make for the monthly reflection, where the slower, more deliberate act of handwriting is part of the practice. The two cadences want different tools. I have written about the broader question of which tool fits which kind of writing in the Notion vs Obsidian essay.

What changed after a year of weekly reviews

The most measurable change is that I make slightly less work and slightly more of it gets finished. The unfinished essays in the editorial calendar have decreased by roughly a third. The number of new essays started has decreased by roughly the same amount. The net is more finished work and a calmer calendar, which was not what I expected and is not what most productivity literature would predict.

The less measurable change is that I have stopped feeling, on most Sundays, that the week was somehow shapeless. The shape was always there. The review was the place I could see it. Without the review, the week ends in a kind of haze that the next week's beginnings have to push through. With the review, the week ends in writing, and the next week begins on a clean surface.

What the weekly review deliberately does not do

It does not plan the next week. There is no "priorities for next week" field, no goal-setting block, no commitment list. The forward-looking aspect of the review is contained entirely in the fourth question, which is about subtraction. Planning the next week is a different activity, and combining it with the review tends to convert the review into a planning meeting that begins on Saturday morning.

It also does not score the week. There is no numeric rating, no traffic-light status, no "how did the week go" select. Ratings invite comparison across weeks, and comparison across weeks is the single most reliable way to make a weekly practice feel like a performance review. The review should feel like a private conversation with the week, not an audit of it.

Letters from readers about the weekly review

"Forty to sixty minutes feels long. Can it be shorter?" Yes, but I would resist shortening it below thirty. The questions need a little space to be answered honestly, and a fifteen-minute review tends to produce performative answers rather than truthful ones. The pot of tea is the right length, and the pot of tea is about an hour.

"What if I miss a week?" Skip it. The next Saturday, do that one. Trying to backfill missed weeks turns the review into a chore. The ledger of weekly reviews can have gaps for the same reason the kakeibo ledger can have gaps — the practice is a years-long thing, not a streak.

"Do you read old weekly reviews often?" Yes. Once a month I read the four most recent ones, as part of the monthly reflection. Once a quarter I skim the previous quarter. Once a year I read all fifty-two in sequence on the first morning of the new year, with tea, which has become my favourite hour of the year.

If you want a paper companion

The four-question structure adapts cleanly to paper. The Mindful Yen Etsy shop sells a small weekly journal built around the same four questions, intended for readers who prefer to write the weekly review by hand and keep the Notion side for the monthly aggregation. Several readers run both — paper for the writing, Notion for the archive. That is also a fine arrangement.

Final thought

The kakeibo monthly reflection has been doing its quiet work in Japanese households for 120 years because Hani designed it around questions rather than inventories. Lifting the same idea up one cadence — from monthly to weekly — turns out to produce something with the same property. Four questions. One pot of tea. A row in a database. The week becomes legible to itself, and the work of the next week begins on a clean surface.

If you want the workspace, it is here. If you want longer letters about weekly practice, the Mindful Yen Substack is where I write them. The hour, in either case, will be on Saturday morning, and it will be enough.
