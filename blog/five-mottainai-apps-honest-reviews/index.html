Five Mottainai Apps: Honest Reviews from Tokyo

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

On a Tuesday evening last month I wrapped a second-hand Iwanami paperback in brown paper, walked it to the convenience store on the corner, paid 215 yen for tracked postage, and sent it to a woman in Fukuoka who had bought it from me for 480 yen. By the time I walked home, Mercari had already deposited the proceeds, minus its fee, into my app wallet. The whole transaction had taken, end to end, perhaps eleven minutes of my life. Somewhere in Fukuoka, the book was on its way to someone who actually wanted it. This is the best case of what the so-called "mottainai apps" can do. It is also, I should say up front, not representative.

Most writing about Japan's reuse economy is either breathless — a country of perfect second-hand grace — or cynical — a country drowning in plastic. The reality is a handful of apps that vary wildly in quality, each of which solves one part of the problem and ignores others. I have used all five of the ones I am about to describe over several years, sometimes as a seller, sometimes as a buyer, sometimes as a give-away poster. This essay is my honest review of each. Honest means some of them are bad.

Mercari

Mercari is the dominant Japanese resale app. If you buy or sell one second-hand thing in Japan in 2026, it is almost certainly going to be on Mercari. The app is fast, the listing flow is well-designed, the QR-code postage system at convenience stores and Yamato lockers is genuinely frictionless, and the user base is large enough that almost anything listed at a reasonable price sells within a week.

This is also where the honesty has to begin. Mercari has, over the last decade, become a mixed environment. The original pitch — a gentle flea-market in your phone — has been partially overtaken by semi-professional resellers who buy in bulk at second-hand shops and list at markup, buyers who haggle aggressively in the comments, and a small but persistent contingent of users who game the review system. The platform's own efficiency has selected for a slightly harder culture than it began with.

I still use it. It is the only app I use, reliably, when I need something to leave my apartment within a week. The friction is low and the match rate is high. What I have learned to do is price slightly above what I would accept, accept the first civil offer, and decline the rest without negotiating. The time saved is worth more than the two hundred yen of margin lost. Rating: the best tool for the job, with caveats.

Jimoty

Jimoty (ジモティー) is the closest Japan has to a local classified-ads board. You list an item, you list your neighbourhood, the buyer comes to you — or, often, you meet them at the station. Most listings on Jimoty are free or nearly free. It is the app you use when you do not want to pay postage to get rid of something, and when the item in question is too bulky to reasonably ship.

I have given away, on Jimoty: a bookshelf that did not fit the new apartment; a set of four dining chairs my husband inherited from his university days; a microwave oven whose timer had broken but whose basic function still worked; a rice cooker my grandmother used until 2018. Each of these left my apartment within three days of listing. The people who came to collect them were polite, on time, and in most cases carried a small thank-you — a box of biscuits, a tangerine, once a hand-written note.

The downside is search. Jimoty's interface feels, as of 2026, perhaps four years behind Mercari. Search results are uneven. Location filtering works but is imprecise. If you are looking for something specific, you will usually give up and go to Mercari. Jimoty's strength is on the selling side, for large or heavy items that would not be worth the postage. In that niche, nothing else in Japan matches it.

PayPay Flea Market

PayPay Flea Market (PayPayフリマ, now integrated into Yahoo!Auctions since the late-2024 restructuring) is Mercari's second-largest competitor. It is well-made, it integrates with the PayPay wallet most of Japan already has, and its fee structure is marginally better for sellers.

It is also, at least in my usage, noticeably slower to match. Items I would have sold on Mercari in three days sat on PayPay for ten. The buyer base is smaller and the search discoverability is weaker. I have mostly stopped listing there, not because the app is bad, but because the two platforms mean double the listing labour for what, in practice, is a sale on Mercari anyway.

The one case where PayPay wins is when I am buying. It tends to be slightly cheaper, on average, because fewer resellers have bothered to arbitrage it. If you are hunting for a specific book or a specific piece of kitchenware, a search on PayPay in addition to Mercari will occasionally surface a listing fifteen percent cheaper than the same thing on the bigger platform. Rating: worth knowing about as a buyer, not worth the time as a seller.

Suttoko

Suttoko (スットコ) is a smaller app in the local-giveaway category, positioned as a gentler Jimoty. The pitch is good: no money changes hands, no haggling, just a neighbourhood board of things going free.

I used it for four months. I have to be honest: it did not work for me. The user base in my ward was small enough that items either sat for weeks or received a flurry of interest from users who then did not show up to collect. The friction of the "just meet me at the station" exchange — which works on Jimoty because there is a softer social contract around small payment — does not always work when the item is free, because the buyer has no skin in the game. I ended up re-listing everything I had posted on Suttoko on Jimoty and then meeting people who actually came.

The app itself is well-designed. The gap is in the network effect. If Suttoko reaches critical mass in your neighbourhood, it may be genuinely better than Jimoty. In mine, it was not. Rating: check it, use it if it works in your area, do not be surprised if it does not.

Tabete

Tabete is the Japanese equivalent of Too Good To Go — an app that lets restaurants and bakeries sell their unsold end-of-day food at a discount, rather than throwing it out. Onigiri at half-price, the last three croissants of the day, a bento that did not sell at the station kiosk.

Tabete, of the five apps in this essay, is the one I most wish worked better than it does. The pitch is close to a perfect mottainai product: food that has been made, and would be wasted, goes instead to someone who will eat it. The reality, at least as of early 2026, is that the app's coverage is patchy. My ward has perhaps a dozen participating vendors, most of them within a fifteen-minute walk, but the pickup windows are narrow — usually a single hour in the evening — and the reservation system is aggressive. A slot that appears at 5pm is often gone by 5:03.

When it works, it is lovely. I have picked up three rice balls and a piece of salmon bento from a small shop near Yoyogi-Uehara for 350 yen, food that would otherwise have gone into the waste stream. When it does not work — which is most evenings — it is an app of empty slots and a vague sense of guilt. I keep it installed. I use it perhaps twice a month. Rating: genuinely virtuous, logistically uneven, worth having.

What this tells me about the Japanese reuse economy

Taken together, the five apps describe an economy that is much more functional than the one most Western visitors imagine, and much less utopian than the export image of Japanese reuse. Mercari is large and mostly works. Jimoty is underrated. PayPay is a secondary buyer's tool. Suttoko is a good idea that has not reached critical mass. Tabete is a morally clean product undermined by logistics.

The deeper point, I think, is that mottainai as a national word does not automatically produce good infrastructure. The apps that work, work for the same reasons apps work anywhere: large user base, low friction, good enough matching. The apps that fail, fail for ordinary reasons too. Culture helps, but culture does not build the pipes.

If you are visiting Japan, or if you live here and are quietly curious what the locals actually use, my honest answer is: install Mercari first, install Jimoty second, check PayPay if you are hunting something specific, and try Tabete if you live near participating shops and are willing to accept that half your evenings it will not work. The rest you can skip.

And — a small closing confession — even with all five apps installed, I still buy most of my second-hand books at the Book Off in Kichijoji, on foot, because a physical shop lets me touch the spine before I commit, and because the apps, for all their virtue, cannot replicate the small mottainai pleasure of walking home with a paper bag of paperbacks under your arm.
