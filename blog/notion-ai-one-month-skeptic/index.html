One Month with Notion AI: A Skeptic's Review

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I was not the ideal reviewer for Notion AI. I do not enjoy generated prose. I find the phrase "let me summarise that for you" quietly insulting when said by software. I have, for four years, run my publication out of a Notion workspace organised around the principle that the slow act of writing is the point, not the friction. So when I finally paid for a month of Notion AI in May — the decision prompted by a reader who kept asking, politely, whether I had tried it — I did so with a stack of suspicions and a notebook to record them in.

The month is now over. What follows is what I found. It is neither the enthusiastic embrace some Notion content suggests nor the blanket dismissal my priors pointed me toward. Notion AI is a real tool with real uses, and it is also a tool that quietly reshapes how one thinks if one is not careful with it. Both things are true. The question is where the line falls, and the answer, after a month, is more specific than I expected.

For context on my workspace, the solo-creator setup essay describes what Notion AI was added to. If you are curious about Notion itself, you can try Notion here →; the AI is a paid add-on on top.

Where it genuinely helped

I will start with the good, because the good is real, and I do not want to bury it under the caveats it deserves.

The clearest win, by some distance, was transcription cleanup. I take audio notes on walks — a habit I described in passing in the personal wiki essay — and I had, for years, transcribed them by hand. The hand transcription was slow and often incomplete, which meant that roughly a third of my audio notes never actually became fragments. I tested Notion AI on a week's worth of raw transcripts: paste the messy auto-generated text in, ask it to clean up punctuation and paragraph breaks while preserving every word. It did this well. It did not invent. It did not summarise. It did exactly the narrow task I asked for, and the time saved was substantial.

The second clear win was meeting-note tidying — in my case, the occasional collaborative call with an illustrator or a bookshop owner. I type quickly and messily during a call. Asking the AI afterwards to produce a clean list of decisions and actions, with original phrasing preserved wherever possible, saved me about twenty minutes per call and produced output I actually used.

The third, smaller, win was property-fill in databases. When I imported old receipts into the kakeibo ledger, the AI was capable of extracting dates, amounts, and merchant names from pasted email confirmations with reasonable accuracy. I still checked every row. But the base layer was faster than typing from scratch.

What these three uses have in common is worth naming: they are all tasks where the AI is operating on a text I have already produced or received, performing a narrow transformation, and where the ground truth remains the original. The AI is not generating. It is tidying. Tidying is, to my quiet surprise, something it does well.

Where it quietly harmed

The harms were subtler and took longer to notice, which is why the month of use mattered more than the first week.

The first harm was to my first drafts. I did not ask Notion AI to write anything for Mindful Yen. I did, once, ask it to "help me get unstuck" on a Substack post I had been avoiding. It produced three paragraphs that were competent, readable, and not mine. I rewrote them — every sentence — and the post took longer than if I had written it from scratch, because I was now wrestling with the phantom of a draft that should not have existed. The problem was not that the AI's prose was bad; the problem was that it was plausible. A bad draft is easy to discard. A plausible draft is a gravitational field.

I watched this same effect in other writers I know. An American essayist friend described the same sensation: the AI's drafts are not wrong exactly, but they are generic in a way that is difficult to escape once read. The only reliable defence, in my experience, is not to ask for drafts at all. Use the AI on text that already exists. Never let it produce text that will become the seed of your own writing.

The second harm, slower to appear, was to my rereading. I have described the monthly rereading of fragments as the central mechanism of my personal wiki. For two weeks, I experimented with asking Notion AI to "find patterns" across the past month's fragments. It was eager. It produced neat summaries. It identified themes. And the summaries were consistently, subtly wrong, in the way that someone who had read the table of contents of a book would be wrong about the book. The AI's version of my month was plausible and flat. My own version, produced by slow rereading, was messier and more accurate and, crucially, mine. I stopped asking.

The third harm was the hardest to notice and the one I want to flag most clearly. Having the AI available, as a kind of always-open second mind, began to change which questions I asked. Small questions that I would previously have sat with — the right verb in a sentence, the correct structure of a paragraph — became questions I typed into a prompt box. The typing was faster. The sitting-with, which is most of what writing is, shrank by a small amount each day, and the small amounts accumulated. By the end of the month I was, measurably, a slightly worse writer, in the particular sense of being less willing to tolerate the discomfort of not knowing what sentence to write next. The AI had not replaced my writing. It had replaced the pause before the writing, and the pause was where the writing actually happened.

The verdict, such as it is

I kept the subscription at the end of the month, but only after setting three rules for myself, which I will pass on in case they are useful.

The first rule: never use it on a first draft. Not even for unsticking. First drafts are the one place where the AI's fluency actively damages the writer's voice, because a first draft is a voice-finding exercise and an imported voice short-circuits it. I now write first drafts in a plain text editor, not in Notion, specifically to remove the temptation.

The second rule: use it only on material that exists. Transcripts. Meeting notes. Email confirmations. Pasted text. Anywhere the ground truth is already written, the AI can tidy without inventing. This is the reliable productive zone.

The third rule: do not ask it to reflect. The monthly review, the weekly habit review, the kakeibo reflection — these are not tasks to be delegated. The labour of reflection is the reflection. Outsourcing it produces the appearance of self-knowledge without the substance, which is a worse outcome than not reflecting at all, because the outputs look real and quietly aren't.

A note on what Notion AI is actually good at, by design

Reading Notion's own documentation and the several blog posts the company has published about the AI's underlying design, it is fairly clear that the tool is optimised for information-worker tasks: summarising, drafting, translating, extracting. These are the tasks that make meetings in offices shorter. They are also, not coincidentally, the tasks where the generic middle-quality output of a large language model is most economically valuable, because in those contexts, middle quality is better than the alternative, which is the same text read slowly by a human who does not want to read it.

For a writer, the calculation is different. The alternative to generic middle-quality prose is not "nothing." It is one's own voice, at whatever cost in time. The trade that makes Notion AI valuable in a corporate context makes it dangerous in a writer's context. Recognising this asymmetry is, I think, the most useful framing for anyone deciding whether to use it.

What I use, and what I don't

Four weeks in, the AI features I still use are: transcript cleanup, meeting-note tidying, and occasional property extraction from pasted emails. The features I have turned off or ignored are: writing assistance, summarisation of my own material, and the "ask AI anything" box that Notion has helpfully placed in approximately every view. I would remove that box if I could.

For comparison, the workspace I described in the solo-creator setup essay is exactly as it was before the AI arrived. The three-state task system from the project management essay is exactly as it was. The kakeibo template has not changed. None of these benefited from the AI, and none of them suffered. The AI occupies a small, specific corner. That corner is useful. The rest of the workspace is better off not touching it.

A closing thought

A friend asked me, at the end of the month, whether I would recommend Notion AI. The honest answer is: only if you know precisely what you want it to do, and only if that task is tidying, not thinking. If you are a solo creator running a publication in Notion, it is worth the subscription if, and only if, you have a lot of transcripts. If you do not, the money is better spent elsewhere — a good pen, a month of silence, a book you have been meaning to read slowly.

I write more about the texture of working this way in the Mindful Yen Substack, and the Etsy shop has the paper tools I use to keep the AI from encroaching on the parts of the work where it does not belong. If you are curious about Notion itself — with or without the AI — you can try it here →. The base tool is excellent. The AI is a narrow, honest addition to it, nothing more and nothing less, and treating it that way is the whole of the method.
