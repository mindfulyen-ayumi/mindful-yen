An Ikigai Journal Practice: Three Sunday-Morning Questions

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I keep a small notebook on the shelf above the kettle. It is an A5, soft-cover, nothing remarkable. On Sunday mornings, after the first cup of tea but before the second, I open it and answer three questions in long-hand. The whole exercise takes between fifteen and forty minutes, depending on the week. I have done it, with various interruptions, for the better part of seven years.

This is, I think, the smallest useful ikigai practice I have found. It is not the only one. It does not require any of the Western frameworks I have written about elsewhere. It does not ask you to find the intersection of four circles. It does not promise a vocation or a salary. It is simply three questions, asked once a week, with a pen, and a willingness to write the honest answer rather than the impressive one.

Why three, and why Sunday morning

The number three is not sacred. I arrived at it after experimenting with longer protocols — a five-question version inspired by Mieko Kamiya's distinction between ikigai and ikigai-kan, a seven-question version a friend in Nakameguro lent me, even, briefly, a daily one-question version that I abandoned after about three weeks because it began to feel like an obligation rather than a noticing. Three is what I came back to. It is short enough that I will actually do it. It is long enough that the third answer is sometimes the one that matters.

Sunday morning is similarly practical rather than mystical. The week is over. The next one has not begun. The mind has, briefly, no obligations. In Tokyo this also tends to be the quietest part of the week — the streets in my neighbourhood are still half-asleep until about ten — and the quiet does some of the work for you. If your Sunday mornings are not free, almost any other reliably quiet hour will do. What matters is the rhythm, not the hour.

The three questions

The questions, exactly as I have them written on the inside cover of the notebook, are:

  1. What, this past week, was worth it?
  2. What did I do that I would not have wanted to write down?
  3. What small thing, this coming week, do I want to make room for?

I will say something about each.

The first question: what was worth it

This is the Kamiya question, made weekly. Worth it is doing a lot of work in the sentence — it is closer to the Japanese kachi ga atta (it had value) than to any English phrase about productivity or accomplishment. The answer is allowed to be very small. A meal cooked properly. A walk through Yanaka in the early evening, when the light is at its best. A patient I helped, if you are a clinician. A sentence I wrote that came out the way I wanted, if you write. A long phone call with a friend. Ten minutes spent watching a particular kind of bird in the park near my flat that I have never been able to identify.

I write three to seven of these, in short sentences, without trying to impose a theme on them. The themes, if they exist, will emerge over months. Trying to extract them in the moment tends to flatten them into something less useful than what they would have become on their own.

What I have noticed, year over year, is that the answers cluster. Not into a vocation, exactly — though for some of my readers they have done so. More often into something like a private aesthetic. A list of textures and rhythms and small acts that one keeps quietly returning to. Most of mine have nothing to do with my work. Some of them have nothing to do with anyone but me. They are the closest thing I have to a life's worth of ikigai, and they have been there the whole time. The journal is just a way of seeing them.

The second question: what did I do that I would not have wanted to write down

This is the harder question. I borrowed it loosely from a passage in Marcus Aurelius — though Marcus phrased it more sternly — and adapted it to fit inside the ikigai frame rather than the Stoic one. The point is not self-flagellation. The point is the noticing of a particular kind of asymmetry: there are certain things one does in the course of a week that one does not, on reflection, want to record. The fact of not wanting to record them is the data.

For me the answers tend to be small. An hour lost to the phone in a way that left no trace. A short irritation expressed at someone who did not deserve it. A second piece of cake eaten standing at the kitchen counter, not because I wanted it but because it was there. None of these are scandalous. None of them require dramatic correction. The point is to look at them clearly, write the short sentence, and let the looking do the work. Most of them, I have found, change quietly over the following weeks without my needing to launch a campaign against them. The light is the correction.

The third question: what small thing do I want to make room for

This is the forward-looking question, and I keep it deliberately small. Not what enormous life change will I undertake. Not what new system will I install. Just: what small thing, this coming week, do I want to make room for.

The answers tend to be modest. A trip to the bookshop in Jimbocho I have been meaning to revisit. A long bath on Wednesday night. A handwritten letter to a friend who lives too far away to call. An evening with no screen. The reading of one particular essay I have been carrying in a tab for weeks. None of these are projects. They are micro-permissions. The act of writing them down, however, makes them roughly twice as likely to actually happen, which is enough.

Tools, briefly

I am asked about tools more often than I think the question deserves. The honest answer is that the tool barely matters. A notebook. A pen. A quiet half-hour. That is the whole apparatus.

For readers who would prefer a digital version — and there are good reasons to prefer one, especially if you travel, or if you would like to search past entries — I keep a parallel Notion page that mirrors the paper notebook. It has the three questions at the top, a date stamp, and a simple weekly archive. Nothing elaborate. If you would like a ready-made version of it, the Notion template I use is free on Notion's free plan; it does not require any subscription, and it will do everything most readers need.

Either format works. What matters is the consistency, not the medium. I have done a stretch of the practice on hotel notepads and another stretch entirely in Notion and the answers came out roughly the same shape.

What three months of this looks like

If you do this practice for a month, you will mostly notice the friction of building a new habit. The first Sunday will feel slightly self-conscious. The second will be easier. The third will start to feel like part of the week.

By month two, the answers will begin to surprise you. You will write down something on the first question — a small evening with a friend, a particular walk — and realise, looking at it, that you had been undervaluing it for years. You will write down something on the second question that you had not quite been admitting to yourself. The journal will start, gently, to do the work it was designed for.

By month three, the practice quiets down. The questions are answered more quickly. The answers are more honest because the act of writing them is less self-conscious. And the small set of recurring themes from the first question — three, four, five things that keep returning — will have begun to look like a description of the life you are actually living, rather than the one you keep meaning to live. That description is the closest thing this practice produces to a result. It is also, I think, the most useful one.

A note on what this practice is not

This is not a productivity system. It will not make you more efficient. It will not, in any direct sense, increase your income. It will not deliver the fourth circle of the Marc Winn Venn diagram. If you are looking for an instrument that promises those things, this is not the instrument.

What it will do, slowly, is increase the resolution at which you see your own life. The Kamiya tradition would call that ikigai-kan — the felt sense of meaningfulness, the inner weather. It is not a destination you arrive at. It is a quality of attention that the practice cultivates, week by week, until one day you notice that the question of whether your life is worth living has stopped feeling like a question that needs answering. The practice is the answer.

Subscribers to the Mindful Yen Substack often write to me about how their own version of this journal has evolved — different questions, different rhythms, different notebooks. The variations are the point. Take what is here, change what does not fit, and let your version be yours. The frame is small enough to bend.

Final thought

I started this practice because a longer one had collapsed under its own weight. Three questions felt almost embarrassingly modest at the time. Seven years later, the modesty is what has kept it alive. The grand journaling systems I tried in my twenties are all in a drawer somewhere. The small notebook is still on the shelf above the kettle. Sunday morning will come around again. The questions will be there. The honest answers, when I write them, will continue to do their quiet work. That is, I think, the whole shape of an ikigai practice that lasts.
