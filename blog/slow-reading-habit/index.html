The Slow Reading Habit: Why I Stopped Speed-Reading

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The book on the small table beside my reading chair tonight is a hardback novel by Yōko Ogawa that I have been reading for nine weeks. The bookmark is a thin strip of dark green washi paper a friend made me, and it sits about a third of the way through. At the speed I read in my late twenties — six or seven novels a month, a paperback on every train — I would have finished it in three evenings. Tonight, in the same chair, with a small glass of barley tea on the table, I will read for forty minutes and move the bookmark perhaps twelve pages. Then I will close the book. The pleasure is in the twelve pages, not in the finishing.

This essay is about why I stopped speed-reading, what a small bookshop in Jimbocho had to do with it, and what slow reading has produced — and not produced — in the years since.

The decade of speed

I worked as a magazine editor in my late twenties, and the job required reading at a particular pace. Manuscripts came in on Friday and were due back marked up by Monday. Books for review had to be read in a sitting. I learned all the standard speed-reading tricks — chunking phrases instead of words, suppressing the inner voice, scanning for the shape of an argument before reading the sentences themselves. My reading rate roughly doubled. My retention, I assumed at the time, held steady.

It did not. About three years into the practice, I noticed a particular kind of forgetting. I could remember whether I had read a book. I could often remember its argument or its plot. What I could no longer remember was the texture of any individual sentence — the specific image, the surprising verb, the small phrase that ten years earlier would have stayed with me for weeks. The books had been processed rather than read. The speed had stripped them of the thing I had originally come to reading for.

I read a study around then — I believe it was the same Mueller and Oppenheimer line of work that demonstrated handwriting's depth advantage over typing, though there are several similar studies — finding that comprehension at high reading speeds drops sharply once the text has any literary or argumentative density. Speed-reading works for surface information. It does not work for prose that wants to be heard.

Jimbocho, and a man who would not be hurried

Jimbocho is the old bookshop district of Tokyo — several streets in Chiyoda lined with second-hand bookshops, some of which have been in continuous operation since before the war. I went there one Saturday in my early thirties looking for an out-of-print book on the tea ceremony, and ended up in a narrow shop run by a man in his seventies whose name I never asked.

I told him what I was looking for. He nodded, did not move toward any shelf, and asked me what I was reading at the moment. I told him — something I had been racing through, a long novel I was determined to finish that month. He looked at me for a moment and said, in the slow polite Japanese of his generation, that he had stopped reading quickly forty years ago, and that the books he loved most were the ones he had taken five or six months to finish. He said it without judgement. It was simply information he was offering me, the way he might have mentioned the weather.

Then he turned and pulled the tea-ceremony book from a shelf I had not noticed and handed it to me. The whole exchange took perhaps four minutes. I have thought about it for ten years.

What changed when I slowed down

I did not become a slow reader overnight. The shift took about eighteen months and was uneven. The first thing I did was stop counting books. I had kept a list, as many readers do, of titles finished per year. I stopped. The number had been quietly distorting my reading — turning every book into a small race I was running against myself.

The second thing was harder. I began reading some books aloud, in the morning, at the kitchen table, before anyone else was up. Not entire books — usually a single page, sometimes two. The reading-aloud reset the pace. You cannot speed-read aloud. The voice forces you back to the rate the sentences were written for.

The third thing was the bookmark. I started to notice that I had been treating the bookmark as a wound — a sign of incompletion, something to be erased by reaching the end. I began to think of it instead as the place I had stopped on purpose. The book was being read at the pace I was reading it, and that pace was the right pace.

By the end of the first year of this, my book count was a fraction of what it had been. My memory of what I had read was sharper than it had been in a decade. Specific sentences from books I had finished six months earlier returned to me unprompted — the way phrases from poems do. I had got back the thing speed had taken.

What slow reading is not

Slow reading is not a productivity hack disguised as a philosophy. It is not a system for retaining more information per unit time. It does not produce a higher words-per-hour rate of any useful kind. If you measure it against a speed-reading course on its own terms — books finished, facts retained, summaries produced — slow reading loses every contest.

It is also not a vow of poverty. I still read a fair amount. The Ogawa novel beside the chair is one of perhaps fifteen books I will read this year. In my speed-reading years it would have been seventy. Fifteen is not a small number. It is simply the number that fits the pace I now read at.

And it is not, importantly, a refusal of the modern world. I read on a screen for work most days. I read fast when fast is what the text wants — emails, manuscripts, essays I am editing. The slow reading is reserved for the books that ask for it, in the chair, in the evening, with the lamp on. The two pages are protected from the speed of everything else.

The wabi-sabi reading of slow reading

The wabi-sabi sensibility — austere, weathered, attentive to small things — has a quiet relevance here. Leonard Koren writes that wabi-sabi attention is the attention you bring to "things modest and humble," to "the irregular, the intimate." A novel read at twelve pages a night, over nine weeks, is a different object than the same novel consumed in three evenings. You see the irregularities. You notice the chapter that drags and the small image in chapter four that the author plants quietly so it will bloom in chapter eleven. The book becomes intimate in the literal sense — known from the inside, paced with — rather than possessed.

This is why my friends who read at speed cannot tell me which sentences they loved in books they finished last week, and why I can quote, two years later, lines from books I read in 2024. The slowness is not the cost of the practice. It is the practice.

A modest method, if you would like one

Slow reading does not need a system, but a few small changes help if you are coming down from a long speed-reading habit. These are the ones that worked for me.

Read one book at a time. Not three. The practice of having several books on the go was, in my case, a way of ensuring I always had something easy to pick up — and the easy book always crowded out the hard one. One book per period creates the necessary friction. When the book is hard, you read the hard book.

Set a time, not a page count. Forty minutes in the chair, not twenty pages. Some evenings forty minutes produces fifteen pages. Some evenings it produces six. Both are correct. The time is what is yours to control; the pages are the book's.

Keep a thin notebook beside the chair. Not for summaries. For the one or two sentences from each session that you want to remember. Three lines, written by hand. Over a year this becomes its own quiet record — fifty or sixty pages of the sentences that mattered. I write more about the practice of paper beside the screen in another essay, but the principle is the same: handwriting catches what the eye would otherwise let pass.

Re-read. A book worth slow-reading is usually worth re-reading. The second pass is not a duplication; it is the part of reading that finishes the work. I re-read perhaps three books a year. They are almost always the books I remember most vividly.

Allow yourself to abandon a book. Slow reading and stubborn reading are not the same thing. If a book is not asking to be read at this pace, close it without guilt. The bookshop man in Jimbocho, when I asked him, said he abandoned about a third of the books he started. The other two-thirds got the full slow attention.

What the chair has done over time

The chair where I do this reading is an old armchair re-upholstered in a coarse linen, with a small side table and a paper-shaded lamp. The chair is unremarkable. The reading practice that happens in it has, over five years, become one of the steadier things in my life — the thing I miss most when travel disrupts it, the thing I return to first when I am home.

It has also slowly altered what I read. The contemporary fiction that depends on plot velocity has receded from my shelves. What has filled its place is older — Tanizaki, Sōseki, the diaries of Sei Shōnagon and Murasaki Shikibu, twentieth-century essays in translation, the slow non-fiction that used to bore me when I was racing. None of these books work at speed. All of them work at twelve pages a night.

I write more about the slow reading practice — and the books currently on the chair — in my Substack, where the weekly letter often turns into a kind of reading diary. The newsletter is free; the slow reading is its own reward.

I am closing the Ogawa now. The bookmark is in the right place. Tomorrow night, twelve more pages.
