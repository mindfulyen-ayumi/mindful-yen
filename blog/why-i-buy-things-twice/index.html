Why I Buy Things Twice: A Wabi-Sabi Consumer Theory

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The wool coat hanging in the small entry of our apartment is the second wool coat I have owned in this size. The first one I bought in the autumn of my twenty-fourth year from a fast-fashion shop in Shibuya for about six thousand yen. It looked, in the shop, identical to the more expensive coats it was imitating. It lasted a winter and a half. The shoulders began to pill within three months; the lining tore at the seam in the second winter; by the spring of my twenty-sixth year it was in a charity bin and I was, technically, coatless again. The second coat — the one in the entry now — I bought eight years ago from a small Japanese maker for about ten times what the first one cost. It has been worn for eight winters. It looks better now than it did the day I bought it. The wool has softened; the cuffs have darkened slightly with use; a small repair on the lining, done by a tailor in our neighbourhood for two thousand yen, has held without comment for four years. I expect to wear this coat for another decade.

I have done the arithmetic. The two coats together cost me roughly seventy thousand yen across twelve winters. A single coat at the same price as the second one would have cost sixty thousand yen across the same twelve winters. The difference is small in money. The difference in everything else — the hour spent buying the replacement, the regret of throwing the first coat away, the slow erosion of the conviction that I knew what I wanted — is much larger. This essay is about that larger difference, and about why the wabi-sabi sensibility argues, quietly and without preaching, against buying things twice.

The cheap-then-good cycle, named

I think of the pattern as the cheap-then-good cycle, and almost everyone I know in their thirties has lived through several iterations of it. You buy something cheap because the good version feels extravagant. The cheap version disappoints, breaks, or wears out. Three years later you buy the good version, having paid for both. The total spend is higher than if you had bought once. The total satisfaction across the years is lower. The waste — environmental, attentional, emotional — is the silent third cost no one tallies.

The pattern is not about willpower. It is about a particular failure of imagination at the moment of purchase. The good version, in the shop or on the screen, looks roughly like the cheap version. The eye does not yet know how to see what makes the good version durable. The hands have not held both. The price difference looks like a markup rather than a property of the object.

Once you have lived through the cycle a few times, the eye learns. By my early thirties I could tell, holding two wool garments side by side, which one would survive a decade. The information was in the weight, the density of the weave, the way the seam felt under the thumb, the smell of the lining. None of this is mysterious. All of it is invisible from a photograph, which is why online shopping defaults you toward the cheap-then-good cycle even when you are trying to escape it.

The wabi-sabi argument, slowly

The wabi-sabi tradition has a particular reading of objects that is relevant here. In the lineage that runs from Sen no Rikyū's tea practice through to contemporary craft writers like Sōetsu Yanagi (whose The Beauty of Everyday Things is the standard mid-century text), an object is understood not as a fixed product but as something that develops over the years it is used. A new tea bowl is unfinished. A tea bowl that has been used daily for thirty years has acquired what Japanese craftspeople call aji — a flavour, a presence, a depth that the new bowl could not yet have.

The cheap-then-good cycle is, from a wabi-sabi standpoint, a refusal to let any object reach aji. You buy the cheap version, and it cannot acquire depth because it is engineered to fall apart before depth could form. You replace it, and the replacement does not yet have depth either. You spend your life surrounded by objects that are perpetually at year zero. The room never becomes itself.

The wabi-sabi alternative is not the consumerist "buy expensive things" — it is the patient "buy fewer things, slowly, and keep them long enough to see them age." The first instinct produces a wardrobe of luxury labels at year zero. The second produces a wardrobe of perhaps half as many garments at year ten, all of which look more interesting than they did when they arrived.

The economics, in three minutes

Let me put the economics on the page, because money is sometimes the language that finally convinces.

The first coat: 6,000 yen, lasted 1.5 winters. Cost per winter: 4,000 yen.

The second coat: 60,000 yen, on track for at least 18 winters. Cost per winter: 3,333 yen.

The good coat is cheaper per winter. This is true even if I count only the winters already used, not the projected ones. It would be true even if the good coat lasted only ten winters; the cost per winter would be 6,000 yen, only 50 percent more than the cheap coat, in exchange for a garment that does not embarrass me, does not need replacing in the middle of a cold December, and that I have come to look forward to wearing.

The same arithmetic, in my experience, applies to most categories that have a "good version available." Wool coats. Leather shoes. Cookware. Knives. A wooden cutting board. A winter blanket. A reading chair. Anything with a fifteen- or twenty-year version available is, on the long arithmetic, almost always cheaper to buy once. The only categories where the arithmetic flips are technology (which becomes obsolete on a five-year cycle regardless of build quality) and trend-driven clothing (which you will not want to wear in eight years anyway). Everything else rewards patience.

I have written elsewhere about the kakeibo budget practice, and one of the small effects of keeping a kakeibo for several years has been that the cost-per-use logic becomes second nature. You stop seeing the price tag. You start seeing the rate.

Why we buy things twice anyway

If the arithmetic is so clear, why do we buy things twice? Several reasons, none of them stupid.

The first is liquidity. The cheap version is buyable now; the good version requires either savings or a willingness to wait. For people whose budgets are tight, the cheap version is not an error of taste; it is the only available option. I lived this pattern for the whole of my early twenties. The wabi-sabi answer for that period of life is not to pretend you can afford the good version. It is to wait longer, to live without the object until you can buy the good one — which sometimes means a winter in a thinner coat than is ideal, with extra layers underneath, and the small acceptance that some material discomforts are the price of not buying badly.

The second is uncertainty. We do not know yet which objects we will keep using long enough to justify the good version. A 25-year-old does not know whether they will still want a wool coat in their thirties or will have moved to a warmer city. The cheap-then-good cycle is, in part, a hedge against the uncertainty of one's own future tastes.

The third is the speed of contemporary buying. The internet has made the cheap version available in two clicks; the good version often requires an actual visit to a shop, a conversation, a wait. The cheap version is therefore winning by default. Returning the friction to the buying decision — by deliberately requiring yourself to visit a shop, to hold the object, to wait at least a week between deciding and buying — is the simplest practical defence against the cycle.

How to break the cycle, in modest steps

I do not have a programme. What I have is a few small practices that, over years, have shifted my own buying away from the cheap-then-good pattern.

Keep a wishlist with dates. Not items in a shopping cart. A handwritten list, with the date you first wanted the object beside each entry. Most things you want urgently you stop wanting within a month. The dates make this visible. The objects that survive on the list for six months or a year are usually the ones worth buying — and worth buying well.

Buy from sellers who can answer questions. A shop where you can ask the maker how the wool was finished, or how the pan was forged, is a shop where you can buy once. A shop with no human present cannot teach your eye. I have bought a number of long-keeping objects through the small Etsy sellers I follow — the kind whose listings are written by the person who made the object — and the buying experience itself is part of why those objects have become things I keep.

Look at what you have already bought twice. The list of categories where you have already gone through the cheap-then-good cycle is your most valuable consumer document. It tells you exactly which categories are worth investing in once. For me the list includes coats, shoes, cookware, knives, bedding, and notebooks. Anything in those categories now goes through the slow buying process by default.

Allow yourself to be coatless for a winter. The hardest practice. Sometimes the right move is to not own the object yet. The discomfort is finite. The relief of not having bought badly is permanent.

The coat, considered at year eight

I will go back to the coat in the entry. It is the colour of damp slate. The wool, looked at closely, has a slight texture that the better Japanese mills are known for — a low, irregular nap that catches light differently from spun wool. The buttons are horn, slightly mismatched in tone, which I noticed for the first time about three years in. The lining is a pale silk that has frayed at one cuff and been re-stitched. There is a small permanent crease at the back where I have, for eight winters, rested my satchel against the wool while standing on trains.

None of this damages the coat. All of it is the coat becoming itself. I will wear it tomorrow morning to a meeting in Marunouchi. By the time I get home it will look exactly the same as when I left, which is to say it will look like a coat that has been worn into its eighth year by a person who intends to wear it into its eighteenth.

This is, in the end, what wabi-sabi consumer theory comes down to. Not buying less for the sake of buying less. Not buying expensive for the sake of buying expensive. Buying once, slowly, and then living with the object long enough that it stops being a purchase and starts being a part of the room. The arithmetic works out. The room becomes itself. The coats hang where they hang.
