Kakeibo for Freelancers with Irregular Income

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The most common objection I get from freelance readers — translators, editors, illustrators, the small army of independent workers who keep the Japanese publishing industry running — is that kakeibo cannot work for them, because the first question of the practice asks how much money they have, and the truthful answer changes every month by a factor of two or three. A salaried household opens its kakeibo on the first of the month and writes a confident income figure. A freelance household opens its kakeibo on the first of the month and writes a question mark.

This objection is partly correct. Kakeibo as Hani designed it in 1904 assumes a household with a stable monthly income, because in 1904 most urban Japanese households did. The version of the practice my grandmother kept assumed the same. Adapting kakeibo to irregular income is not difficult, but it does require three structural additions that the canonical practice does not include. I have made these additions for my own household — I have been freelancing as an editor for nearly a decade, and my own income varies between roughly half and roughly twice my median month — and I want to describe them as plainly as I can. None of the three are my invention. They are the adaptations Japanese freelance practitioners have made over the past several decades, and they have settled into something close to a standard.

The first addition: a buffer account, sized to two median months

The buffer account is the single most important structural change. It sits between your client invoices and your kakeibo, and its function is to convert lumpy income into smooth income before the ledger ever sees it.

The mechanics are straightforward. You open a separate bank account — in Japan I use a Sony Bank account for this; abroad, any second checking account will do — and you direct all client payments into it. You then pay yourself, on the first of each month, a fixed monthly figure into your main account. Your kakeibo only sees that fixed figure. The variability is absorbed inside the buffer account, where it does not interfere with the practice.

The buffer needs to be large enough to absorb the realistic variability of your income, which means sized to about two median months of your fixed self-payment. A freelance editor whose median is, let us say, three hundred thousand yen per month should be aiming for a buffer of around six hundred thousand yen. Below this threshold, the buffer cannot smooth a normal lean month; above it, the additional buffer is not earning its keep and might as well be in savings. Two months is the durable working figure across most of the freelance practitioners I know.

Building the buffer takes time. If you are starting from zero, the standard approach is to set your monthly self-payment slightly below your median for the first six to twelve months, and let the buffer accumulate from the months when income comes in above the self-payment figure. This is uncomfortable. Setting your self-payment lower than you need to feels, at the start, like a hardship without purpose. After the buffer reaches one month and then two, the discomfort reverses — the smoothness it produces is so valuable that practitioners often keep their self-payment slightly conservative even after the buffer is full, just to keep the buffer growing gently.

The second addition: the three-month rolling average

The buffer account smooths your spending. The three-month rolling average smooths your decision-making. They are different tools.

Once a quarter — I do this on the first weekend of January, April, July, and October — I look at the actual revenue that has flowed into the buffer account over the past three months, divide by three, and adjust my monthly self-payment accordingly. Not by the full amount of any change, but by about half. If the rolling average has risen by, let us say, twenty thousand yen, I might raise my self-payment by ten thousand. If it has fallen by the same amount, I lower the self-payment by ten thousand. The half-step damping is deliberate. It keeps the self-payment moving in the right direction without overreacting to a single quarter's noise.

The rolling average is also where you discover, slowly, whether your freelance practice is genuinely growing or merely fluctuating. A single good quarter can be misread as growth; three consecutive rising rolling averages cannot. I keep the four most recent rolling averages on a single page in my paper ledger, in pencil, so the trend line is visible at a glance. This is the closest thing to a dashboard the kakeibo allows itself.

The third addition: the lean-month envelope

The third structural addition is the lean-month envelope, which is the freelance practitioner's response to the fact that even with a buffer and a rolling average, some months are unmistakably leaner than others. A client pulls a project. A long invoice cycle creates a gap. A holiday quarter produces a temporary income drop. The buffer absorbs these, but only if you are willing to spend less in the lean month than in the median month.

The lean-month envelope is a pre-written, smaller version of your normal monthly envelope. It assumes the same fixed costs, the same savings figure, but a noticeably smaller spending allowance — typically about seventy to seventy-five percent of the normal envelope. It lives in a sealed page at the back of my ledger, written once a year and unsealed only when needed.

Knowing in advance what a lean month looks like is the entire point. The decision to compress spending is hard to make in the moment, when the pressure is real and the temptation is to dip further into the buffer. The decision is much easier to honour if it has already been made — by you, in a calmer moment, with the same information but without the pressure. I unseal the lean-month envelope perhaps twice a year. The discipline of having pre-decided what it would contain is what makes those months survivable without distress.

What about taxes

The other freelance question I am asked is where taxes fit. Japanese freelance taxation, like American or British self-employment taxation, requires you to set aside a meaningful share of each invoice for income tax, residence tax, and pension contributions. None of this should ever pass through your kakeibo.

The standard arrangement, which I use, is a third account — a tax account — that sits alongside the buffer account. Each invoice arrives into the buffer, and a calculated share is immediately moved into the tax account. The tax account is invisible to the kakeibo. It does not appear in any of the four questions. It exists only to be drawn on at tax time, when the figures it has accumulated against are due.

The percentage to set aside varies by jurisdiction and by income level. In my own case, the figure has settled at around twenty-five percent of gross invoiced revenue, and after a decade I have not had a year where the tax account came up short. Your own figure will be different. The principle is the same: the kakeibo is for the money you actually have to live on, not for the money the government has temporarily lent you while it waits for the tax return.

How the four questions read for a freelance household

With the three structural additions in place, Hani's four questions become answerable for a freelance household, with very little modification.

Question one — how much money do I have? — now reads against the fixed monthly self-payment, not against any client invoice. The answer is stable from month to month, and it is honest, because the variability has been absorbed elsewhere.

Question two — how much would I like to save? — works exactly as Hani intended, against the fixed self-payment figure. The savings line in a freelance kakeibo is no different from a salaried one.

Question three — how much am I actually spending? — also works as designed. The daily log is unchanged. The four categories are unchanged. The monthly tally is unchanged.

Question four — how can I improve next month? — gains an additional dimension for a freelance household, which is whether the self-payment figure itself needs adjusting. This is not a monthly question; it is a quarterly one, and it belongs to the rolling-average review rather than to the monthly reflection. But it sits in the same family of forward-looking questions, and the kakeibo accommodates it without strain.

The Notion side of a freelance kakeibo

I run my own freelance kakeibo as a hybrid. The paper journal handles the monthly reflection and the lean-month envelope, which both benefit from the slowness of writing by hand. The digital side — built inside a Notion workspace I have iterated on for years — handles the daily log, the buffer-account tracking, the rolling-average calculation, and the tax-account allocation. Notion is not the only tool that would work for this; a spreadsheet would be sufficient. I prefer Notion because the freelance-kakeibo workspace can also hold my client tracking, my invoice register, and my project notes, all linked to the same financial backbone.

For freelance practitioners who already use heavier business tools — accounting software, invoicing platforms, client CRMs — the kakeibo sits underneath those, not alongside them. The business tools handle the legal and operational side. The kakeibo handles the household. They look at the same money from different ends.

The Tokyo freelance context

One last thing, which is specific to my own context but may translate. Freelance work in Tokyo, particularly in the editorial and translation worlds I move through, sits inside a culture of long invoice cycles. Sixty days is normal. Ninety days is not unusual. A new client, in publishing, may not pay until the project is fully delivered, which can be six months from the first piece of work.

This means the buffer account, for a Tokyo freelancer, often needs to be larger than the two-month rule of thumb suggests. I keep mine sized to about three months of my self-payment, because the lag between work done and money received in my industry is genuinely that long. Freelancers in faster-paying industries — software contracting, for example, where thirty-day terms are common — can usually run with a smaller buffer.

Whatever the size, the buffer is what makes a freelance kakeibo work. Without it, the first question is unanswerable; with it, the practice opens up. The two-month figure is a starting point. The right figure for your own practice is the one that lets you write a stable income figure on the first of every month without either lying to yourself or hedging.

The freelance kakeibo is not a different practice. It is the same practice, with three small structural supports added underneath it, so that the four questions remain honestly answerable when the income that flows past them is anything but smooth.
