Why I Don't Throw Away My Father's Coat

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The coat hangs at the far left of my wardrobe, on a wooden hanger I bought specifically for it, between a grey wool scarf I almost never wear and the cotton bag that holds the black suit I keep for funerals. It is a navy double-breasted wool coat, mid-weight, with horn buttons and a dark inner lining that is beginning to fray at the shoulder seam. There are three small moth holes in the right sleeve — one near the cuff, one halfway up, one just below where the sleeve joins the shoulder. The coat smells, faintly, of cedar from the small block I keep in the wardrobe, and, more faintly, of my father, who died four years ago in November, and who wore this coat for perhaps thirty of the sixty-one years of his life.

I have not worn the coat. It is too big for me by about a size and a half, cut for a man's shoulders, and even if it fit I would not wear it; this is not that kind of keeping. I have also not repaired the moth holes, though I know a tailor three neighbourhoods over who would do it well. This essay is about why I have not done either, and about the particular class of objects — the ones we are not supposed to keep, according to one version of the minimalist literature, and that we keep anyway — and what the wabi-sabi and mottainai traditions have to offer someone trying to think clearly about grief and inheritance and the small physical weight of a person who is no longer here.

The decluttering literature's gap

The dominant English-language writing on decluttering, from Marie Kondo onward, handles the question of inherited objects with a specific move. The objects are to be thanked for their service, released with gratitude, and let go. The gratitude is meant to be sincere. The release is meant to be clean. For many objects — a parent's coffee maker, a stack of unread books, a set of dishes one never liked — this framework works well, and I have used it without apology on a number of the items that came to me after my father's death.

It does not work on the coat. It does not work on a small wooden brush his own father carved, which sits on my desk. It does not work on the handwritten margin notes in his copy of Kokoro, which is on the second shelf of my bookcase. The decluttering framework's gap is that it assumes all objects can be thanked and released, and that the test is whether the object sparks joy. Some objects do not spark joy. Some objects carry weight. The two are not the same. A coat that carries the weight of a person's winter walks for thirty years is not failing the joy test; it is playing a different game.

The philosopher Crispin Sartwell, in his book Six Names of Beauty, writes about the category of objects that have absorbed a life — that have, as he puts it, become sites of ongoing relationship rather than instances of aesthetic preference. A wedding ring. A grandmother's teacup. A father's coat. These objects do not ask to be enjoyed. They ask to be kept.

What the coat actually weighs

I took the coat down once, about eighteen months after my father's death, and put it on over a thin sweater in the kitchen, alone, on a November evening when it had started to rain. The coat is made of a coarse English wool — he had bought it in London in the early 1990s, one of the few genuinely extravagant purchases of his life, and had worn it through Tokyo winters for three decades — and the weight of it, empty, across my own smaller shoulders, was a surprise.

Wet wool is heavier than dry wool. The coat was dry. It still weighed more than any garment I own. I understood, standing in the kitchen with the sleeves too long and the shoulders falling away from my own, what my father had been carrying for thirty Novembers, what he had felt on his back as he walked home from the station through the rain, what it had meant for his body to be inside this particular shape of warmth. I had not known before. You cannot know a coat from watching someone wear it. You can only know it by putting it on.

That was the only time I have worn it. I hung it back up. I have not taken it down since. But the weight is now part of what I know about my father, and the coat, sitting in the wardrobe, is the physical archive of that knowing. If I threw it out, the knowing would not go — I would still remember the weight — but the possibility of returning to it, of re-verifying it on some future evening when memory had thinned, would be gone.

The three moth holes

The moth holes appeared in year two, after I had been keeping the coat without cedar blocks. I noticed them during a seasonal change, at the bottom of the wardrobe, and felt something close to panic — a small specific shame at having failed to protect the one object I had most clearly meant to protect.

I did not have them repaired. This surprised me. I had assumed I would. The tailor three neighbourhoods over does invisible wool mending; he could have made the sleeve look as it did on the day my father last wore it. But when I sat with the idea for a few days, something in me pushed back. The holes were now part of the coat. They were the record of my specific failure, in my specific apartment, during a specific winter of my early grief. Repairing them would not restore the coat. It would overwrite the last two years of its life with me.

This is the wabi-sabi instinct, in the careful sense — not "perfectly imperfect" as a slogan, but the tradition's recognition that objects accumulate marks of time and relationship, and that those marks are the object's history becoming visible. Leonard Koren writes about this; Andrew Juniper writes about it more philosophically. Sen no Rikyū, in the sixteenth century, is said to have preferred cracked tea bowls because the crack was a record of the bowl's life. The three holes in the right sleeve of my father's coat are a record of mine.

This is also close to kintsugi — the practice of repairing broken ceramics with gold-dusted lacquer so that the repair is visible and honoured — though the object's direction is different. I am not adding a gold seam. I am leaving the damage. But the underlying instinct is the same: the object's truthful history is more valuable than its restored appearance. I have written more about kintsugi as a wider practice elsewhere.

What mottainai actually means, in this case

Mottainai, often translated as the ethic against waste, is the usual term reached for when a Japanese household keeps an object longer than a Western household might. The translation is correct as far as it goes, but it misses a register. Mottainai is not only about resources. It is about the small violence of discarding something that still has a relationship to a life. A functional coat that belonged to a dead father is not waste-able because the coat is not only a coat. It is a physical node of a finished relationship, and the relationship, even finished, is still a relationship.

The anthropologist Merry White, writing about Japanese domestic life, has observed that Japanese households frequently keep objects that have been "retired from use but not from meaning." The father's coat sits in this category. It is retired from use — I will not wear it; my mother did not want it; my brother lives in Osaka and keeps his own father-objects there. It is not retired from meaning. As long as I am alive and the coat is in the wardrobe, one thread of my father's physical presence in the world continues.

This is, I think, a category Western minimalist literature does not quite name. It is not sentiment. It is not clutter. It is something like kept weight — the deliberate decision to carry, materially, the evidence of a relationship that can no longer carry itself.

What I do with the coat now

Nothing, mostly. I open the wardrobe most mornings. I see the coat. I do not think about it, most mornings, in any conscious way. Perhaps once a month, during the seasonal wardrobe changes in spring and autumn, I take it down, shake it gently, check the cedar block, look at the three holes, and put it back. In November — the month my father died — I tend to notice it more. I do not perform a ritual. I simply notice it more.

What I have not done, and will not do, is any of the things people sometimes suggest. I have not had the coat altered to fit me; that would change what it is. I have not had the moth holes invisibly repaired; that would erase two years of my life with it. I have not photographed the coat as a "memory archive" and then given it away; the photograph is not the coat, and the weight is not in the photograph. I have not donated it to a charity shop with the thought that some stranger might wear it well; the coat does not belong to the category of garments that circulate. It is, quite specifically, not for anyone else.

I keep a small wooden box in the wardrobe with a folded slip of paper in it — a single line, in my handwriting, describing the coat and where it came from and why it is here. If I die, and my brother has to empty the apartment, the slip will tell him what the coat is. He can decide then. I have no instructions to leave. The object is the instruction.

On the small Etsy practice around kept objects

One practical aside, because I have been asked. For a long time I kept the coat without any particular care. The moth holes appeared in year two, which is how I learned. The small things I now use — cedar blocks, a cotton garment bag, a wooden hanger sized for the coat's actual shoulders — I have come to think of as a quiet practice around kept objects, rather than storage. A few of the hand-finished garment bags and cedar blocks I use and recommend are listed on the Mindful Yen Etsy shop, alongside the kakeibo journals; I mention them here because readers have written asking.

The practice is small. It is to take a kept object seriously enough that it is protected, without turning it into a shrine. A cedar block is not sentimental. It is a promise that the object is being looked after. A cotton bag is not a memorial. It is a physical acknowledgement that this thing has been chosen, specifically, to remain.

A note on grief and permission

I want to end by saying that I do not think every reader should keep their dead parent's coat. Some people's grief travels better light, and the objects associated with a lost person can, for some people, become a weight that does not serve them. Throwing a parent's coat out is not a failure of love, and for some people it is the cleaner, truer move.

What I do think, and what I think the slow-living literature has sometimes missed, is that the minimalist default — that objects without immediate joy should be released — is the wrong default for a small number of objects in most lives. These are the objects that carry weight. The wabi-sabi tradition, and the older mottainai instinct, offer a counterweight. Some things are kept. The keeping is the work. The work is legitimate.

If you are sitting with a similar object — a mother's scarf, a grandfather's watch, a partner's favourite cup from a marriage that ended — and you have been told, subtly or directly, that you are supposed to let it go, I would offer only this. Ask whether the object is carrying weight that would otherwise have to be carried differently. If it is, keep it. Give it a hanger, a shelf, a small defined place. Let it accumulate the marks of its life with you. Do not repair every hole. The object is doing work. It does not need to sparkle.

What the coat does not do

It does not bring my father back. It does not make the November he died easier. It does not substitute for his voice on the phone or for the annual trip he and I used to take to the bookshop in Jimbōchō the week before the New Year. The coat is not a magic. It is a coat, with three moth holes in the right sleeve, hanging on a wooden hanger in a cedar-scented wardrobe in my apartment in Tokyo.

What it does do, and what the decluttering literature underestimates, is hold a physical place in my daily life for a person who no longer has one. Every morning when I open the wardrobe, the coat is there. This is not a daily grief. It is a daily continuity, which is a different and gentler thing. My father is dead. His coat is in the wardrobe. Both of these facts are true, and the second fact makes the first fact slightly more bearable. That is the whole case for keeping it. It is, I think, enough.
