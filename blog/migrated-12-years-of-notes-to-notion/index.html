I Migrated Twelve Years of Notes to Notion

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Twelve years is long enough to have several selves. The note archive I finished migrating into Notion last autumn contained fragments written by a university student in Kyoto, by a junior editor in a Tokyo publishing house, by a woman recovering from an illness in her late twenties, and by the person I am now. Reading it in sequence, across an archive that began in Evernote in 2013 and passed through Obsidian between 2019 and 2023 before arriving in Notion, was the strangest and most worthwhile afternoon of work I had done all year.

This essay is about that migration. What I kept. What I let go of, deliberately, after reading it one final time. What I would do differently if I were beginning again now. And the small method that survived all three tools, which turned out, on inspection, to be the only thing that had ever really mattered.

If you are considering a migration of your own, or simply curious about the trajectory, the current Notion workspace the archive now lives in is described in the solo-creator setup essay, and you can try Notion itself here →. The rest of this piece is the story.

What was in the Evernote archive, and what went in it

Evernote, in 2013, was the software that taught a generation of students to keep notes on computers. I was one of them. My first notebook — titled, touchingly, University — contained lecture notes, PDFs of assigned readings, photographs of whiteboards, and the occasional journal entry written during a long train ride home. It grew steadily for six years. By the time I left Evernote in 2019 it held approximately eleven thousand notes across forty-one notebooks, with a tag system that I had rebuilt three times and still did not entirely understand.

What I saved from that archive, when I migrated out, was probably twenty percent of its volume and ninety percent of its value. The twenty percent consisted of: journal entries, transcribed book passages, a handful of editorial correspondence, the photographs of the whiteboards that still mattered, and the essays I had drafted in the app itself. The eighty percent I let go of was: every PDF of an assigned reading I had not opened since the class ended, every web clipping I had saved in case it became relevant (it had not), every meeting note from a job I no longer held, and a remarkable quantity of recipes I had clipped and never cooked.

Letting go of the eighty percent was harder than it should have been. I had absorbed, from years of productivity writing, the idea that a note archive was an asset, and that deleting from an asset was a kind of damage. This turned out to be a category error. A note archive is not an asset; it is a conversation with past selves. Most of that conversation is not worth preserving, in the same way that most phone calls are not worth recording. The valuable part is small. Admitting this took me two full weekends.

The Obsidian years

In 2019 I moved to Obsidian, which had just been released and which appealed to me for the same reasons it appealed to the writers I respected: plain text, local files, no lock-in. The migration from Evernote was technically straightforward — export as ENEX, convert to Markdown, drop into an Obsidian vault — and the transition felt, at the time, like a purification.

Obsidian was, and remains, a very good tool. I do not want anything I say next to be read as criticism of it. The reason I eventually left is not that Obsidian failed. It is that the way I was using it began, slowly, to resemble the way I had used Evernote at the worst of it — dozens of folders, a baroque tag system, and a graph view I had initially found beautiful but which had become, in practice, an ornament I looked at instead of writing.

The specific thing Obsidian did not help me do, and which I eventually realised I needed, was the combination of plain text with structured databases. My kakeibo ledger — described in the kakeibo template essay — never fit cleanly in Obsidian, because kakeibo is not text, it is attention-shaped numbers. The reader-letter inbox described in the Substack template essay also did not fit. The personal wiki I began to build, described in the zuihitsu essay, fit well in Obsidian but did not connect to the rest of the studio's work, and the disconnection was a small daily friction.

In early 2023 I started a trial of Notion and, after three weeks, began moving the vault. I am aware that this is the opposite direction of the migration many writers have been doing since — Notion to Obsidian, toward plain text and local files — and I want to say, plainly, that both migrations are defensible. The right tool depends on what the tool is being asked to do. For a writer who only writes prose, Obsidian is probably better. For a writer who runs a small publication, with a ledger, a reader inbox, and an editorial calendar alongside the prose, Notion's database features are, in my experience, worth the trade.

The Obsidian-to-Notion migration itself

The mechanical migration was more work than I expected and less work than it might have been. Obsidian stores notes as Markdown files in a folder. Notion imports Markdown files reasonably well, with a few quirks. The two main quirks were internal links — which broke, because Obsidian's [[wikilink]] syntax is not Notion's — and attachments, which needed to be re-uploaded individually because my Obsidian vault had been storing them in a folder structure Notion did not preserve.

I wrote a small Python script to convert the wikilinks into Notion-compatible page references where possible, and to flag, with a consistent marker, the ones that needed to be manually relinked. The script was about eighty lines and the Claude assistant I occasionally use helped me write it in an afternoon. The manual relinking took about two weeks, done in small batches, usually while listening to something. I deliberately did not rush this stage, because the relinking was also the rereading, and the rereading was the part that mattered.

For the attachments, I adopted a policy I wish I had adopted in Evernote: only re-upload attachments that were still relevant to the current note, and leave the rest behind. This cut the attachment volume by roughly half and the workspace felt measurably lighter as a result. The attachments I did not migrate are still, for what it's worth, in a backup folder on an external drive. I have never opened it.

What I kept, in the end

After twelve years and three tools, the material that made it into the current Notion workspace breaks down, approximately, as follows.

Journal entries from 2013 onward. All of them. Even the ones I find embarrassing to read now. These are the single most valuable thing in the archive, because they are the only material nobody else could have produced. An essay I drafted in 2015 might be reproducible; a journal entry from a specific Tuesday in 2015 is not.

Transcribed book passages with my annotations. Roughly nine hundred of them. This is the commonplace-book material I described in the zuihitsu essay, and it is the second most valuable part of the archive. A transcribed passage is a piece of thinking. A mere highlight is not.

Completed essay drafts — every piece I had ever finished, even if unpublished. Roughly two hundred. These serve as a private reference, useful mostly for the rare occasions when I find myself drafting something I have drafted before, which has happened more often than I like to admit.

Editorial correspondence that I re-read and decided was worth keeping. About forty exchanges. These are the conversations that shaped how I think about writing, and they are worth more than any craft book on my shelf.

The kakeibo ledger, which I had been keeping in Evernote, then in a spreadsheet, then in Obsidian, and which finally found its proper home in Notion's databases.

What I let go of

Web clippings. Almost all of them. If the article was worth reading a second time, I will find it again; if I cannot find it again, it was not worth reading a second time. This is harsher than the internet-productivity culture suggests, and I have not regretted it once.

Meeting notes from jobs I no longer hold. These felt, while deleting, like I was erasing a part of my career; they look, from the other side of the deletion, like the right call. The meetings themselves are in me. The notes were summaries of summaries.

Recipe clippings I never cooked. Several hundred. I have a small paper binder of recipes I actually use. It contains, after twelve years of cooking, maybe forty dishes. That is enough.

My earliest tag system. I exported the tags for archival purposes and did not import them. The rereading during migration was itself the new organisation, and it was better than any tag structure I had ever maintained.

Several embarrassing attempts at a "second brain." Readers of a certain era will know what I mean. I read the manifesto, I was persuaded, I built an elaborate PARA-style folder system, and it slowly became the main thing in the archive that I avoided opening. The migration was also the funeral.

The method that survived all three tools

Here is the part of the essay I hesitated to write because it risks sounding precious, but which is honestly the most useful thing I can offer to anyone planning their own migration.

The only practice that survived from Evernote through Obsidian into Notion, essentially unchanged, was the habit of writing short fragments in a dated page. Everything else — the folders, the tags, the dashboards, the graph views, the PARA system, the zettelkasten experiment — was eventually retired. The fragment practice was the constant.

What this suggests, and what I now believe, is that the tool matters much less than the practice does, and the practice that matters most is the smallest one: writing something short, dating it, and leaving it alone for a while before rereading it. Every tool I have used has supported this practice. Every tool I have used has also offered more elaborate alternatives, each of which I eventually abandoned. If I were starting again today, I would skip the elaborate alternatives and keep only the fragments. I would save myself about seven years.

What I would do differently from the beginning

Three things, briefly.

I would start from plain text, regardless of tool, because plain text survives every migration. Markdown is the closest thing to a universal format we have, and anything I have written in Markdown has moved cleanly between tools. Anything I wrote in a proprietary format has been either difficult to migrate or partially lost. This is a small technical choice with large long-term consequences.

I would keep the fragments separate from reference material from the beginning. The collapse of these two categories — a fragment is thinking, a reference is a pointer — is the single most common source of clutter in a note archive. Separating them, as I described in the zuihitsu essay, is worth doing on day one.

I would not try to preserve the archive as an archive. The archive is a living record of a living mind, and living things prune. The health of a note archive is measured not by its size but by how often the keeper returns to it without resentment. A small archive returned to regularly is worth more than a large archive the keeper avoids.

A closing thought

A twelve-year note archive, read in sequence, is a slow autobiography. The work of migration is, among other things, the work of reading that autobiography. Most of what one finds is not worth keeping, and the decision to let it go is itself a form of writing — a quiet editorial pass on the record of a life. What remains, after the editing, is closer to a self-portrait than to an archive. That is what the Notion workspace holds now. It is smaller than the Evernote vault it came from. It is also, for the first time in twelve years, entirely legible to its keeper.
