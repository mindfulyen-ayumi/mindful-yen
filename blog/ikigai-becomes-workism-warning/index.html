When Ikigai Becomes Workism: A Warning from Tokyo

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

In February 2019, the American journalist Derek Thompson published an essay in The Atlantic titled "Workism Is Making Americans Miserable." His argument, condensed, was that the educated professional class in the United States had quietly converted work into a religion. Career was no longer just a livelihood; it was the place one looked for identity, community, transcendence, and meaning. The trouble with this arrangement, Thompson observed, was that work — being work — was structurally unable to provide most of those things. The result was a generation of high-achieving people who were, on paper, doing everything right and were, in their own private accounts, exhausted and bewildered.

I read the essay a few weeks after it was published, in a café in Shinjuku, and I remember closing my laptop with a strange double feeling. The first feeling was recognition: Thompson was describing something I had watched friends in Tokyo go through for most of my adult life, dressed in slightly different clothes. The second feeling was apprehension. The diagram of ikigai that had become popular in English by 2019 — the four-circle Marc Winn version, with its insistence that one's vocation be also one's passion and the world's pressing need — was, I realised, the perfect ideological vehicle for workism. It looked Eastern. It promised meaning. And it pointed, with a small italic word at the centre, directly at one's job.

This essay is a warning. Not against ikigai — the actual Japanese concept is fine. Against the specific way ikigai has been deployed in the Anglophone world to license a workism that the Japanese tradition itself does not endorse, and that Japan, more than most countries, has reason to be cautious about.

What Thompson got right, briefly

Thompson's central observation was that workism — the elevation of work to the status of a quasi-religion — produces predictable misery for two structural reasons. First, work is contingent: companies fail, layoffs happen, careers end. Anchoring identity to a contingent thing is mathematically unstable. Second, work is transactional: even at its most fulfilling, it is paid for, which means it is partly governed by the logic of the buyer rather than the doer. A vocation that must also be a marketable product cannot be entirely free.

The Western ikigai diagram embeds both problems and presents them as solutions. It tells the reader that the right life is one in which work and passion and contribution and compensation all sit on top of one another. It does not warn that this configuration is rare, fragile, and historically contingent. It does not say what happens when one of the circles slips. It simply hands the reader the map and wishes them well.

The Japanese context the diagram quietly removes

Japan, of all countries, is not the place to import a meaning-through-work ideology. We have a word for what happens when work becomes everything: karoshi, death from overwork. The word entered the Japanese language in the late 1970s, was officially recognised as a workplace cause of death in the 1980s, and is now tracked annually by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare. The annual White Paper on Karoshi, published since 2016, documents hundreds of recognised cases each year — strokes, heart attacks, suicides — directly attributed to working hours and conditions. The actual number is widely understood to be substantially higher, because the bar for official recognition is high.

It is into this context that the four-circle diagram has been imported and translated into Japanese self-help books. The cognitive dissonance, watching it happen, has been considerable. A culture that knows, viscerally and with statistics, what work-as-religion costs in human lives is being told, by its own bookshops, that the answer to a flat decade is to find the place where one's job is also one's passion. The Japanese readers I know who have actually engaged with the diagram have, almost without exception, found it slightly alarming on closer reading. They recognise, in a way that English-speaking readers often do not, where the road it points down ends.

The Kamiya distinction the diagram erases

Mieko Kamiya, in Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966), drew a distinction that becomes crucial in this conversation. She separated ikigai as object — the thing that gives life meaning — from ikigai-kan, the felt sense of meaningfulness itself. Her clinical observation was that the felt sense was more durable than any particular object, because objects could be lost: through illness, through death, through the slow erosion of one's circumstances. People who had pinned their entire ikigai to a single object — a child, a vocation, a position — were uniquely vulnerable when that object went away. People who had cultivated the felt sense across many small daily anchors were more resilient.

The Western workism version of ikigai is, structurally, the maximum-vulnerability case. It pins the entire weight of one's meaning-making to a single object — one's job — and asks that the job carry passion, contribution, and compensation all at once. When the job slips, as jobs do, the entire structure collapses. The Japanese tradition Kamiya was describing would have called this an obviously poor design, and would have suggested distributing one's ikigai across many small things, most of which have nothing to do with one's employment.

How the slippage happens, in practice

I have watched this slippage happen in several friends, on both sides of the Pacific, and the pattern is consistent enough that it is worth describing.

It begins, usually, in one's late twenties or early thirties. A reasonably good job. Some real success. The first promotion. A growing sense, encouraged by the surrounding culture, that work is not just paying the rent but is also where one's life is happening. The hours expand. Friendships outside work quietly thin. Hobbies become "things I used to do." A subscription to a meditation app or a productivity newsletter is added, partly as a counterweight, partly as a way of optimising the now-central work life.

By one's mid-thirties, the structure looks, externally, very impressive. Internally, however, the load-bearing capacity of the job has been quietly exceeded. The job was always meant to be a job. It was not built to also be one's social life, one's identity, one's source of transcendence, and one's primary creative outlet. When it fails to provide those things — and it must, because they are not what jobs are for — the conclusion the workism narrative offers is that one has chosen the wrong job. So one quits, finds another, and the cycle restarts. The diagram is consulted again. The circles are rearranged. The actual problem — that no single object can carry the whole weight — is never named.

What the Japanese tradition actually recommends

The corrective, in the tradition Kamiya was describing, is not to find a better job. It is to distribute one's meaning-making across many anchors, most of them small, most of them outside the labour market.

The Okinawan elders interviewed by Dan Buettner described their ikigai as things like teaching karate to local children, tending a garden, attending the community gathering on Tuesdays. Several of them no longer worked at all. The Tokyo neighbours I know who seem to be ageing well describe their ikigai in similar terms: a grandchild, a long walk, a Saturday-morning ceramics class, a small balcony garden. The work question, where it appears at all, appears as one anchor among many. It is rarely the central one.

This is, I would argue, the actually useful lesson of the Japanese tradition for an English-speaking workism culture. It is not find the job that is also your passion. It is closer to cultivate enough small reasons that no single one of them needs to do all the work. A practice for this — a quiet weekly noticing of the small things that made the week worth its weight — is something I have written about separately. It is not a productivity system. It is, more precisely, the opposite.

What this means for how you read the diagram

If you have a copy of the four-circle diagram on a wall or in a notebook, I am not asking you to throw it away. The diagram is, as I have written before, a serviceable career-clarity tool sold under the wrong name. Use it for what it is. Stop calling it ikigai. And, more importantly, stop expecting your job to also be your passion and your contribution and your compensation simultaneously, because that expectation is the workism trap, and it will, in time, take more from you than it gives.

The job is allowed to be a job. The passion is allowed to live elsewhere. The contribution can be made through ten small acts a week rather than through a single career trajectory. The compensation is what pays the rent. These can be different streams. The Japanese tradition has spent sixty years observing that they often are, and that the lives in which they are kept honestly separate tend to age better than the lives in which they are forcibly merged.

Subscribers to the Mindful Yen Substack get longer letters on the structural risks of workism and on the small daily practices that are, in my own observation, the actual antidote. There is no rush, and there is no productivity hack at the end of any of them.

Final thought

The word karoshi exists because Japan learned, the hard way, what happens when work is asked to be everything. The Japanese tradition of ikigai, properly read, is among other things a quiet warning against repeating that mistake. The fact that the same word has been imported into the English-speaking world as a license for exactly the workism it was meant to caution against is one of the more painful translation losses of the last decade. The good news is that translation losses can be undone. Read Kamiya. Notice the small daily reasons. Let the job be a job. The diagram, taken at its word, points down a road we have already walked. There is no need to walk it again.
