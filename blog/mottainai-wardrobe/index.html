The Mottainai Wardrobe: 18 Items, Five Years

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

On a Sunday in early March I pulled everything out of the narrow cedar closet in the hallway of our apartment and laid it on the tatami of the guest room. Not for an Instagram photograph — I do not take those — but because I had begun to suspect, as I did every spring, that I owned more than I was actually wearing, and that the gap between the two numbers was getting wider. I counted eighteen items of clothing on the tatami, not including underwear, socks, or the two yukata in the bottom drawer that belonged to my grandmother. Eighteen items had been the count the previous spring, and the spring before that, and, as far as I could reconstruct, the spring before that as well. I have worn roughly the same eighteen-item wardrobe for five years.

This is not a minimalism essay. I am suspicious of minimalism, which as practiced in English-language lifestyle writing tends to be another form of aspirational consumption — the aesthetic of a Muji-toned life, photographed against white walls, achieved by buying an entirely new set of more tastefully plain things. The wardrobe I am describing is not the result of an edit. It is the residue of five years of mottainai, a word I have written about before, operating at the scale of the closet. Some items survived because they were well made. Some survived because I mended them. Some survived because, when I honestly looked at them, they were still in relationship with me, and I could not release the relationship without a small internal bow.

The inventory, roughly

For transparency: the eighteen items are, approximately, a grey wool coat (Uniqlo, 2019, now mended at the left cuff), a black wool coat (second-hand, unknown maker, 2017), four cotton shirts (two white, one cream, one pale blue — Muji and a small Kichijoji label), two linen shirts (summer), one heavy cotton sweater (oatmeal, Japanese brand, 2018), one cashmere sweater (dark navy, a gift from my mother, 2015), three pairs of trousers (two wool, one heavier cotton), two skirts (one pleated wool, one plain linen), one summer dress (cotton, 2020), and two cardigans. The numbers fluctuate by one or two when a piece is in transit — being mended, or packed away for the season — but the shape has been stable.

I do not wear the same thing every day. The eighteen items rotate in predictable combinations that emerge without effort from the weather and the day's demands. I do not feel under-dressed. I do not feel over-curated. I feel, mostly, that I am wearing clothes.

What survived, and why

The striking thing, when I look at the eighteen, is that they did not survive because they were expensive. Several of them are from Uniqlo or Muji, bought at the prices those shops sell at. A couple were second-hand. Only the cashmere sweater and one of the wool coats would pass the kind of quality test that fashion writers use. The rest are, by any objective metric, ordinary clothes.

What they share is a quieter quality. Each of them, when I put it on, still feels like itself. The grey wool coat has the small weight at the shoulders it had when I bought it. The pleated skirt still holds its pleats when I hang it. The cream cotton shirt, washed perhaps three hundred times, has gone very slightly soft at the collar but has not lost its shape. These are not heirloom garments. They are simply clothes that were made well enough to continue being themselves, given reasonable care.

The care, I think, is what actually produced the survival. I wash in cold water, almost always. I air-dry. I do not put anything in the tumble dryer, except towels. I mend small tears within a week of noticing them, with a small sewing kit I keep in a tin on my desk. I rotate shoes on a two-day cycle so the leather can rest. None of this is extraordinary. Almost all of it is what my mother did, and what her mother did. The clothes survived because they were treated, gently, as objects with their own small lives.

What went, and why

Over the five years a number of things left the closet. I want to be honest about them, because the mottainai wardrobe is not the wardrobe of a woman who buys perfectly every time. I have made several mistakes.

The first category of departures was the well-intentioned failures. A linen jacket I bought for a wedding in 2021 and wore twice. A pair of wide-leg trousers that looked right in the shop and wrong on my body every time I tried to wear them out of the apartment. A silk blouse, fragile beyond my care, that pulled a thread within a month. These left quickly, usually to the second-hand shop near Shimokitazawa, where I took them without much ceremony. I had been wrong, and the best form of respect for the garments was to let them find someone they fit.

The second category was things genuinely finished. A pair of black wool trousers I had worn for seven years. A cotton shirt whose underarm finally thinned past repair. A heavy winter cardigan that had felted where I did not want it to felt. These I mourned, briefly, and then took to the fabric recycling bin at the ward office, because the correct end for a garment that has truly finished is, in a small way, to return to its material rather than to pretend to be a garment it cannot be.

The third category — the one I am least proud of — was the impulse repentances. A cardigan I had bought online in a low moment, which I wore once, photographed nothing, and quietly resold on Mercari at a loss. A dress that was not me. I mention these because a truthful mottainai wardrobe does not pretend the impulse-buying brain does not exist. It is still there. It is just smaller than it used to be, and it produces fewer casualties per year than it used to.

The care rituals, in detail

What actually keeps eighteen items alive for five years is not one big decision but a set of small weekly rituals. I will describe mine.

On Sunday afternoons I do a short inspection. I take the five or six items I wore that week, look at the cuffs and elbows and hems, and note anything that has moved — a thread, a small pull, a button beginning to loosen. I mend within the same hour if I can, or I put the item on a small chair next to my sewing tin if it needs more than ten minutes. The items on that chair usually do not sit there longer than a week.

I wash in small loads, in cold water, with a very mild detergent that smells of almost nothing. I use a mesh bag for anything that might snag. I air-dry on a rack near the window in summer, on a rack near the radiator in winter. This sounds laborious written out. In practice it takes the same ten minutes a load that any wash takes.

Once a month, at the beginning of the month, I do a slower look. I open the closet on a morning when I am not in a rush and I simply look at everything hanging there. I notice what I have not worn. I notice what I have turned to repeatedly. I do not make decisions in this moment. I just register. Most of the structural shifts in the wardrobe have started in those Saturday-morning looks, where an item I had been avoiding announced itself and asked, quietly, to leave.

The financial shape of eighteen items

Readers sometimes ask what this kind of wardrobe costs. The honest answer is that in the five years I am describing I have spent, on average, somewhere between 30,000 and 45,000 yen per year on clothing — roughly the cost of one not-particularly-expensive coat, spread across the year. Most of that goes to replacements, mending, and the occasional second-hand addition. I buy almost nothing at full retail price. I replace one or two items per year, when something has genuinely finished.

This is not a fortune saved. I mention the figure because readers, when they try to imagine a small wardrobe, often imagine it must either be expensive (high-quality "investment pieces") or punishing (a monastic stripping-back). The mottainai wardrobe is neither. It is closer to what my grandmother's generation would have called fudan no fuku — everyday clothes — kept carefully, across time. The expense was never high. The attention was.

What I keep on Etsy and why

I should also admit, since I curate a small Mindful Yen shop on Etsy built around the same sensibility, that my own wardrobe has not been built primarily from what I sell. The shop carries printable journals and a few small domestic objects; the closet is its own slower project, unrelated. I mention the shop here because readers occasionally ask, and because I would rather they know than guess.

What five years has actually taught me

If I had to distil five years of an eighteen-item wardrobe into what I have actually learned, I would say three things.

First, the number of items matters less than the relationship with them. Twenty-four well-attended items would be better than twelve neglected ones. The count is a symptom of the practice, not the practice itself.

Second, the care rituals are cheap. The sewing tin, the mesh bags, the cold-water cycle — these are not a hobby. They are ten minutes a week that quietly extend the lives of clothes by years. Almost no one I know outside Japan does them, and almost everyone I know outside Japan spends more on clothes per year than I do, and I do not think this is a coincidence.

Third, the wardrobe stops being an arena. This is the real gift. Getting dressed, five years in, has become one of the quieter parts of my morning, not one of the louder. The closet is not a daily negotiation. It is eighteen items I know. I put on whichever of them the day asks for. The question what should I wear has mostly disappeared, and the attention it used to consume has gone elsewhere — to work, to reading, to the slow maintenance of the rest of an ordinary life.

This, in the end, is what mottainai does at the scale of the closet. It does not produce a beautiful wardrobe. It produces a wardrobe that has stopped asking for attention, so that attention can return to the things that actually need it.
