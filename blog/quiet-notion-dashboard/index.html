A Quiet Notion Dashboard: Less Color, More Calm

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The Notion dashboard I open every morning has, on its top half, exactly six lines of text and one horizontal divider. There are no banner images. There are no progress bars. There is no widget that tells me the weather, because I have a window. The whole page fits on a single screen of my laptop without scrolling, and when I open it the first thing I notice is, usually, nothing — which is the desired effect.

This essay is about how I arrived at that page, after about two years of building dashboards that were progressively more impressive and progressively less useful. If you would like to start from a quiet template rather than build down from a noisy one, you can use the Notion workspace I keep here →. The reasoning matters more than the template, though, and the reasoning is below.

What a dashboard is for

A dashboard, in the original sense of the word, is the panel between the driver and the engine. It exists to show you only the information you cannot drive without. The accelerator pedal is not on the dashboard. The map is not on the dashboard. Even the destination is not on the dashboard. What is on the dashboard is the speed, the fuel, and a small number of warnings that mean stop driving and look at this.

A Notion dashboard, when it works, has the same property. It shows the very small number of things you cannot proceed without seeing. Everything else lives one click away, on a clearly named page, and is allowed to be invisible until called for. The mistake almost every dashboard tutorial makes is assuming the dashboard is the interesting page in the workspace. It is not. The interesting pages are the ones the dashboard quietly points to.

The case against colour

Notion supports about a dozen colours for tags, callout blocks, and column headers. They are pleasant colours, used in moderation. I do not use them, except in two specific places I will mention, and the absence has been the single largest improvement to my workspace in the past three years.

The reason is simple. Colour, in a workspace, behaves like volume. A page with three colours is louder than a page with one. A page with eight colours is shouting. Once a workspace is shouting, every new piece of information has to compete to be heard, and the natural response is to make it louder still — bolder, redder, larger, with more emojis. The volume creeps up, and after a few months the workspace becomes a place you do not want to open in the morning before tea.

A quiet dashboard uses one accent colour at most, in one place. Mine uses a soft grey for the divider line and the muted text underneath the section headers. Everything else is the default. The result is that when I do, occasionally, want to draw attention to something — a deadline, a flag from a reader letter — a single coloured tag is loud enough to actually mean something. In a noisy workspace, a coloured tag is just one more colour.

The structure of the dashboard I actually use

From top to bottom, the page contains:

  • One sentence at the top, in plain text, naming what the studio is currently focused on. I rewrite it once a quarter.
  • A horizontal divider.
  • A linked view from the editorial calendar showing the next three pieces of writing. No more.
  • A second divider.
  • Three plain links: kakeibo, reader inbox, monthly reflection.

That is the page. There is no second screen. The editorial calendar is the only dynamic element, and it is constrained to three rows because the next three pieces of writing is the only forward-looking number a one-person studio reliably needs. Anything beyond three is, in practice, fiction.

I have written about the wider workspace this dashboard sits inside in the solo creators essay. The dashboard is the surface. The four databases are the rooms. The dashboard's job is to be a hallway with three doors, well-lit, and nothing else on the walls.

What I removed, and what I learned by removing it

The dashboard I use today is the result of a series of removals. Each removal was small. Each of them, when I made it, felt slightly wrong, and each of them turned out to be correct after a week.

The cover image

For about a year I had a soft photograph of my balcony shiso plant as the page cover. It was a nice photograph. I took it myself on a March morning when the light was unusually clear. I removed it the day I noticed I had stopped seeing it. A cover image you have stopped seeing is taking up the most prominent slot on the page for no return. The page is now coverless, and the title sits cleanly at the top of an empty surface, which is the closer match to how I want to feel when I open it.

The progress bars

I had three of them. Words written this month, books read this year, days since the last monthly reflection. They were all calculated automatically through Notion formulas, which I was quietly proud of. They were also, every single one of them, a thin layer of pressure I did not need at the start of the day. The books read counter, in particular, made me suspect every reading session of being too slow. I removed all three. Within a fortnight my reading slowed even further, then sped back up to its natural pace, and I have not missed the bars since.

The embedded widgets

Weather. Spotify. A small calendar from another tool. I tried each of them. None of them survived a month. The weather is in the window. The music is in the room. The calendar is in the calendar app, which I open when I need a calendar. Embedding them in Notion gave me one more reason to keep the dashboard open and one more thing to be distracted by once it was. The dashboard's job is to be closed quickly.

Two places I do use colour, on purpose

For honesty: there are exactly two places in the workspace where I use a coloured element on purpose, and both are restrained.

The first is the kakeibo ledger, where the four traditional categories — survival, optional, culture, extra — each have a coloured tag. The colours are muted, and they are constant. I have not changed them in two years. The point of the colour here is that the eye can find the category in a long list without reading every label, which is the rare case where colour is doing genuine work.

The second is the reader inbox, where unanswered letters get a single soft red tag. This is the only red in the entire workspace. Its rarity is what makes it useful. I have written more about both databases in the kakeibo essay and the solo creators essay.

On typography, and why I leave the defaults alone

Notion gives you two font choices and two width choices. I use the default font and the default width. I have tried serif. I have tried the wider page. I went back to defaults each time, because the defaults are deliberately tuned for long-term legibility, and small typographic decisions in a workspace you open every day quietly accumulate into either ease or fatigue.

The same logic applies to icons and emojis. I use a single small text icon — a hyphen, in fact — for any page that needs an icon at all. Most do not. A workspace with no emojis is not austere. It is just quiet. The first week feels strange. By the second week the strangeness is gone, and what remains is a surface that does not perform anything.

The dashboard test

The test I now use, when deciding whether something belongs on the dashboard, is a simple one. I ask: is this the kind of information that, if I did not see it today, the day would go meaningfully worse? Almost nothing passes that test. The next three pieces of writing pass. The links to the kakeibo ledger and the reader inbox pass, because they are doors I want to be able to find without thinking. The single sentence about what the studio is focused on passes, because forgetting it for a quarter is the kind of drift the dashboard is there to prevent.

Everything else fails the test, including most things I previously had on dashboards: word counts, project statuses, completion percentages, weekly goals, quote of the day, recent activity feeds. None of them, on inspection, made my day measurably better when I saw them. Several of them made it slightly worse.

What this changes, downstream

A quiet dashboard does not, by itself, make a workspace useful. It does, however, change what the workspace is for. With a noisy dashboard the workspace tends to become a place you go to feel productive. With a quiet dashboard the workspace becomes a place you go to do the next piece of work, briefly, and then leave. The leaving is important. A workspace you stay in too long stops being a workspace and becomes a kind of office you have built inside your computer, and the long-term cost of that is real.

This is the same principle that runs through the rest of Mindful Yen — kakeibo, the reading log, the reader inbox. The tool should be quiet enough that the practice is louder than the tool. When the tool starts performing, the practice starts hiding.

Letters from readers about dashboards

"Don't you miss having a beautiful page to look at?" Genuinely, no. The pages I want to look at are the essays, the reader letters, the monthly reflections — the contents of the workspace, not its lobby. A beautiful lobby that you stop noticing has the same value as no lobby at all, and a slightly higher cost.

"Is this just minimalism for its own sake?" I do not think so. The dashboard is small because the information that genuinely needs to be on it is small. If a one-person studio needed twelve dashboard items, twelve would be the right number. Mine needs three.

"What about teams?" A team dashboard is a different object. It has to do shared coordination, which a solo dashboard does not. The principle still holds — colour as volume, widgets as cost — but the threshold for what passes the dashboard test will be higher. I would still suggest fewer items than feels comfortable.

If you want a paper companion

The same principles inform the printable journals in the Mindful Yen Etsy shop. The covers are plain. The interiors use a single ink colour. The page furniture is reduced to the few rules a journal genuinely needs. Several readers keep one of the journals open beside their Notion workspace as a paper version of the dashboard — three lines, written by hand, at the top of each day. That is also a fine arrangement.

Final thought

A quiet Notion dashboard is not a design style. It is a discipline of removal. Each thing the dashboard does not show is a small return — of attention, of calm, of the strange but real sense that the workspace is on your side rather than competing with you. The dashboard becomes a hallway you walk through quickly on your way to work that matters. That is the whole purpose of a hallway, and dashboards have, for some reason, forgotten this.

If you want the workspace, it is here. If you want longer essays about quiet practices, the Mindful Yen Substack is where I write them. The dashboard, in either case, will be doing very little, and that is what it is doing well.
