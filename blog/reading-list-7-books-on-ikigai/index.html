Seven Books on Ikigai: An Honest Reading List

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I have been asked, more times than I can count, for a reading list on ikigai. I have put off writing one for years, partly because I did not want to contribute to the promotional machinery that has grown up around the word in English, and partly because most of the books I would have to include I find, on honest reflection, disappointing. I have decided to write the list anyway, because the question is a reasonable one and the existing English-language reading lists are almost uniformly commercial. What follows is my actual assessment of seven books, in descending order of how much I think they repay the reading.

I am a Tokyo editor turned writer. I read these books some in Japanese, some in English, some in both. I am going to be candid about which ones I think are worth your time and which are not, because politeness is not a service to the reader.

The one you can only read in Japanese, if you read Japanese

Mieko Kamiya, Ikigai-ni-tsuite (1966). This is the serious Japanese book on the subject, and it is, by some distance, the best of the seven. It has never been fully translated into English. Kamiya was a psychiatrist who worked for many years at a leprosarium on Nagashima, and her thinking about ikigai was shaped by years of clinical observation of people whose external circumstances had been catastrophically reduced and who were nonetheless finding reasons to live.

The book's great move is the distinction between ikigai — the object that gives life meaning — and ikigai-kan, the felt sense of meaningfulness itself. Kamiya argues, gently but firmly, that the second is more durable than the first. Her prose is unshowy, clinical where clinical is needed, literary where literary serves. If you read Japanese, this is where to start. If you do not, I am sorry to report that every other book on the subject is a partial reflection of this one, usually without citing it. There are persistent rumours of a forthcoming English translation; until it appears, Kamiya remains a rumour in English.

The best of the English-language books

Ken Mogi, The Little Book of Ikigai (2017). Mogi is a working neuroscientist and a thoughtful Japanese writer, and his book is the one English-language treatment I would recommend without significant reservation. He frames ikigai around what he calls five pillars — starting small, releasing the self, harmony and sustainability, the joy of little things, being in the here and now. The framing is his own, not traditional Japanese pedagogy, but it is honest about being his own, and the examples he draws on — a sushi apprentice, a manga artist, the Kyoto tea ceremony — sit within Japanese life rather than ornamenting it.

The book is not a masterpiece. It is occasionally repetitive. Mogi has a slight tendency to generalise about the Japanese in ways a careful Japanese reader will flinch at. But it is written in good faith, it draws on Kamiya without pretending to replace her, and it does not sell the four-circle Venn diagram. For an English reader without Japanese, this is the best starting point.

The famous one, which I find shallow

Héctor García and Francesc Miralles, Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life (2016). This is the book most English-language readers encounter first, because it has been translated into dozens of languages and has sold in the millions. I am going to be honest: I do not think it is a good book.

The authors visited Ogimi, a village in Okinawa, conducted interviews with elderly residents, and returned with a book that braids their findings together with a thin layer of generalisation about Japanese culture. The Okinawan reporting itself is not bad. The elders they quote are recognisably speaking a Japanese register of ikigai. The problem is everything around the reporting. The four-circle Venn diagram features prominently — a diagram invented, as I have documented elsewhere, by a British entrepreneur in 2014 — and the book presents it as traditional Japanese wisdom. It is not. The book also repeatedly flattens ikigai into a framework for finding your passion and vocation, which is not what the word does in Japanese.

If you have read it and enjoyed it, I am not asking you to undo the pleasure. I am asking you not to mistake it for a reliable account of the Japanese concept. It is an English-language self-help book using Japanese vocabulary. That is a legitimate genre. It is not the same as a book about ikigai.

The longevity book, honest but not really about ikigai

Dan Buettner, The Blue Zones (2008) and The Blue Zones of Happiness (2017). I include Buettner because he is often cited in the same breath as ikigai and because his Okinawa chapters are where many English-language readers first encounter the word.

Buettner is a careful reporter. His Okinawan material is not dishonest. Okinawan elders do use the word ikigai to describe what keeps them going, and they do live, on average, unusually long lives. What I would note is that Buettner's project is not really about ikigai. It is about the statistical clustering of longevity in a handful of communities worldwide, of which Okinawa is one. Ikigai gets a chapter or a chapter's worth of attention, and then the book moves on to Sardinia, Ikaria, Loma Linda.

I would also, gently, note that the Blue Zones methodology has come under serious demographic scrutiny in the last few years — notably in work suggesting that the apparent longevity clusters may be partly artefacts of poor record-keeping. I am not competent to adjudicate that debate. I mention it because Buettner's books are often cited as hard evidence for ikigai's longevity effects, and the evidence is less hard than the citations suggest. Read him for the Okinawan texture, not for the causal argument.

The one on the diagram's history

Marc Winn's 2014 blog post and Andrés Zuzunaga's 2011 diagram. Not a book, but worth including in any honest reading list because these are the actual primary sources for the Venn diagram that dominates English-language ikigai discourse. Zuzunaga, a Spanish astrologer and writer, created the four-circle diagram around 2011, with propósito (purpose) in the centre. Winn, a British entrepreneur, reproduced the diagram on his blog in 2014 with the centre relabelled as ikigai.

Neither is a scholar of Japanese culture. Neither claimed to be. The viral career of the image is largely independent of its creators. I mention them only because any reading list on ikigai that omits the diagram's actual origin is doing the reader a disservice. If you have ever wondered why every English-language ikigai book has the same four-circle image in it, the answer is not ancient Japanese wisdom. The answer is a 2011 Spanish diagram and a 2014 British blog post.

A quieter Japanese book, worth a mention

Shoma Morita, Morita Therapy (various editions). Morita was a Japanese psychiatrist whose early-twentieth-century therapeutic work predates the modern popularity of the word ikigai but sits in a similar intellectual neighbourhood. His emphasis on accepting feelings rather than managing them, and on doing what needs to be done regardless of mood, is a useful counterweight to the Western wellness register that the English ikigai literature tends to adopt. Morita is not for everyone. His prose is dry and clinical. But for readers who find Kamiya out of reach and Mogi slightly light, Morita is a serious adjacent option.

A gentle memoir, loosely related

Naoko Abe, The Sakura Obsession (2019). Not a book about ikigai, strictly speaking, but a book that demonstrates, through one man's lifelong devotion to preserving cherry-tree cultivars, what ikigai looks like in practice. I include it because the best way to understand the Japanese register of the word is often to see it embodied in a single life rather than to read a direct treatment of it. Abe's book is beautifully researched and quietly moving, and the subject — Collingwood Ingram, an English ornithologist who spent decades preserving Japanese cherry varieties — is an unlikely but illuminating case study. A reader who finishes this book has, I think, a better sense of ikigai than a reader who has finished García and Miralles.

What to read, and in what order

If you read Japanese, read Kamiya first, Morita second, and feel free to skip the English-language list. If you do not read Japanese, read Mogi first for the concept, Abe second for the embodiment, and treat the remaining books as cultural artefacts rather than sources. Specifically, if you pick up García and Miralles, read it knowing it is an English-language self-help book that borrowed a Japanese word, not a Japanese book translated into English. That single orientation changes the experience considerably.

Final thought

The English-language literature on ikigai is, with two honest exceptions, commercial. The Japanese literature is small, sober, and mostly untranslated. A reader willing to accept this asymmetry and read accordingly — Mogi gratefully, García and Miralles sceptically, Kamiya longingly — will come out of the reading with something closer to the actual Japanese concept than the industry around the word would suggest. The books are not the practice. They are scaffolding for the practice. Some scaffolding is sturdier than others, and it is worth knowing which.
