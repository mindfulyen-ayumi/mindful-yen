The Kakeibo Question I Never Want to Answer

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

There is a question I have avoided, some months openly and some months by a more sophisticated inattention, for the entire twelve years I have kept a kakeibo. It is not one of Hani's four. It is a fifth question, unofficial, that tends to emerge at the edge of an honest monthly reflection and which I have never found a way to welcome. The question is: did I need this? This essay is a small confession about that question, and a note on what I have learned in the months when I managed to answer it, set against the many more in which I did not.

I should say at the outset that I do not think the fifth question belongs in Hani's original four. Her sequence — have, save, spend, improve — is complete on its own terms, and a household that answers those four honestly each month will produce good enough decisions over time without ever asking whether any individual purchase was necessary. Hani understood something about the psychology of sustained reflection that modern financial writing tends to forget: a practice made too demanding will be abandoned, and an abandoned practice teaches nothing. The four questions are calibrated to be answered every month for a lifetime. A fifth question, asked faithfully, would probably break most practitioners within a year.

But the question appears anyway. Quietly, usually on a Saturday morning with tea, often after I have finished the third question's tally and am sitting with the result. The optional column, say, has come in twelve percent higher than last month. The obvious tactical adjustments are already visible — a cap here, a pause there. And then, just below those, the unwelcome voice: did you need any of it?

Why the question is harder than the four

Hani's four questions are structural. They ask about quantities and categories and improvements — the architecture of a household's money. They do not ask, directly, about the relationship between a purchase and a life. The fifth question is relational, and that is what makes it harder.

To answer did I need this? honestly, you have to hold, in the same mind at the same moment, a specific purchase and a specific life. The navy cardigan in September — did I need it? Not for warmth, strictly; I owned several warm things already. Not for work; nothing professional depended on it. Not even for pleasure in the deep sense, because I remember the cardigan less than I remember what I paid for it. The answer, if I am being honest, is no. I did not need it. But the cardigan is not the point. The point is the cost of sitting with the honest no, and what it asks me to notice about the part of me that bought the cardigan anyway.

This is why the question gets avoided. It does not resolve cleanly. It does not produce an obvious adjustment to next month. It produces, instead, a small acquaintance with the dimmer corners of your own decision-making — the buying that is not about the object, that is about loneliness, or tiredness, or a particular failure to know what to do with a Saturday afternoon. Those are not financial problems. They are human ones that happen to leave a financial trace. And the kakeibo is, by strict design, a financial document.

Hani, I think, knew this. She built a system that would produce most of the benefits of financial self-awareness without requiring the practitioner to look directly at the human material beneath the numbers. That is a kindness. It is also, for a certain kind of practitioner in a certain kind of month, a limit.

The months I have asked it anyway

I have, at a generous count, genuinely answered the fifth question perhaps six or seven times in twelve years. Not six or seven months of theatrical reckoning. Six or seven evenings, in specific months, when something in the monthly tally made avoidance impossible.

The first time was a January — the January after a year in which my optional column had grown quietly every quarter without my noticing the trend. The year-end review laid the four quarters side by side and the pattern was undeniable. I sat with it, and then the fifth question appeared, and instead of turning away I made more tea and let it stay.

What I noticed that evening was not a list of unnecessary purchases. It was a pattern of timing. The optional spending concentrated in two bands — late afternoon, when I was tired and wanted a small reward, and late evening, when I was lonely and wanted something to do. The purchases themselves were inexpensive and individually reasonable. What was not reasonable was the frequency. I was buying, roughly twice a week on average, a small object or experience whose actual function was to manage an emotional state. That is a use of money, and a legitimate one in small doses, but I had drifted into using it as a primary emotional regulator. Seeing that was the value of the fifth question. No category cap would have surfaced it.

The adjustment I made, in the following months, was not financial. It was structural. I added a small walking habit in the late afternoon and a reading practice in the late evening, and the optional column drifted downward on its own, without any cuts imposed. The money moved when the underlying behaviour moved. The ledger had pointed at the behaviour only because I had allowed the fifth question into the room.

The quiet rule I now follow

After that January, I did not become a person who asks the fifth question every month. I am not, I think, psychologically robust enough for that, and I would not recommend the attempt to most practitioners. Instead, I have settled into a soft rule: ask the fifth question only when the monthly tally contains a surprise.

A surprise, in this context, is a category that has moved outside its usual range — upward or downward — without any obvious cause. Ordinary variation does not require the fifth question; most months, the four questions are sufficient. But when a column produces a figure I did not expect, I try, in that specific month, to sit with did I need this? for at least ten minutes before moving to the improvement question. I do not always manage it. Perhaps half the time the avoidance wins and I move on to the tactical adjustment. The other half, the question produces something the ledger alone would not have produced — a small insight about a habit, a relationship, a use of time.

The discipline, if there is one, is to ask the fifth question when it asks to be asked and to refrain from asking it when it does not. A month without a surprise can pass in peace. A month with a surprise is a quiet invitation, which you can accept or decline. The declining is not a moral failure. The Japanese instinct on this is gentler than the Anglophone personal-finance instinct, which tends to treat every month as an opportunity for radical reckoning. It is not. Most months are simply months.

What the question teaches when it is answered

When I have answered the fifth question, the findings have fallen into roughly three categories. I will name them because readers sometimes tell me the categories help, though your own may be different.

The first category is what I call regulation purchases — spending whose real function is to manage an emotional or physical state. The late-afternoon coffee that is really about tiredness. The evening shopping that is really about loneliness. These purchases are not wrong in themselves; they become costly when they calcify into the primary tool for handling a recurring state, crowding out cheaper and often better alternatives. The question surfaces them.

The second category is identity purchases — spending whose real function is to shore up a version of oneself one is anxious about losing, or to construct a version one has not yet earned. The books one buys to feel like a reader. The cookware one buys to feel like a cook. These, too, are not wrong; most of us do them to some extent, and the economy depends on it. The cost becomes visible when the purchase substitutes for the practice rather than supporting it.

The third category is relational purchases that were, on reflection, not quite about the relationship they claimed. The gift that was slightly more about the giver than the recipient. The shared meal that was partly about signalling one's financial ease to a friend whose own finances were quieter. These are the hardest to look at, and I will not pretend I examine them often. When I have, the findings have been humbling.

None of these categories produces a line-item cut in next month's ledger. They produce, instead, a slightly adjusted relationship between me and my own spending — a small addition to the set of things I can notice in the moment. The adjustment is behavioural, not tactical. Over years, it compounds in the same quiet way the rest of the practice does.

A last word

If you are new to kakeibo, please ignore the fifth question entirely. Answer Hani's four, honestly, for at least a year. The four on their own will produce most of the benefit, and the practice needs to be stable before it can absorb anything harder.

If you have been keeping a kakeibo for some time and recognise the small shadow at the edge of certain monthly reflections — that is the fifth question asking to be asked. You can decline it. You can accept it. Both are valid moves. On the rare evening when I have accepted it, the ledger has told me something about my life it could not have told me otherwise, and on the many more evenings when I have declined it, the practice has continued unharmed. The honesty of the four questions is sufficient. The honesty of the fifth, when it comes, is a gift the practice occasionally offers rather than a rule it imposes.
