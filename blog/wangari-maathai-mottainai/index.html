Wangari Maathai and Mottainai: Why a Kenyan Nobel Laureate Chose a Japanese Word

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

In February 2005 a Kenyan woman in her sixties stood at a podium at the United Nations Commission on the Status of Women in New York and said, slowly and clearly, a Japanese word most of the room had never heard. She repeated it. She asked her audience to repeat it with her. A few people tried. Most did not. The word was mottainai, and the woman was Wangari Maathai, who had, four months earlier, become the first African woman to receive the Nobel Peace Prize.

I remember watching a recording of that session a year later, in my parents' living room in Kichijoji, and feeling two things at once. The first was the quiet surprise, familiar to anyone whose small language is briefly picked up by a larger one, of hearing a Japanese word in a foreign mouth. The second was a question I have not entirely answered in the twenty years since. Why that word? Of all the words available to her — in English, in Swahili, in Kikuyu — why did Maathai choose a four-syllable Japanese term to carry an argument she had spent three decades making?

The encounter in Kyoto

Maathai had come to Japan in early 2005 at the invitation of the Kyoto Protocol anniversary events. Between formal engagements she was taken, as most visiting dignitaries are, through the slow choreography of Japanese hospitality — meals presented in sequence, small gifts exchanged, a procession of bows. Somewhere in that week a Japanese colleague, trying to describe why a half-eaten bowl of rice at the end of a meal felt heavier than it should, used the word mottainai.

Maathai asked what it meant. The colleague tried, failed, tried again. The English approximations — "what a waste," "such a pity" — did not seem to satisfy either of them. Eventually, as Maathai later described in interviews, the colleague said something closer to: it is the feeling that the thing deserved better than what we did with it.

That sentence was what she had been looking for. She later said, repeatedly and publicly, that the word gave her something her English had not been able to produce: a single term that contained reduce, reuse, recycle, and a fourth element she had been quietly adding to her Green Belt Movement for years without naming — respect.

Why the fourth R mattered to her

The Green Belt Movement, which Maathai founded in 1977, had planted more than thirty million trees across Kenya by the time she received the Nobel. On paper it was a reforestation project. In practice Maathai had always understood it as something else. The women who planted the trees were also rebuilding a relationship, severed by colonial land use, between a community and the ground it stood on. The trees were material. The repair was not.

Three R's — reduce, reuse, recycle — described the material layer. They said nothing about the repair. They were verbs of management, not verbs of regard. Maathai had tried over the years to fold a fourth verb in. Respect worked in some contexts; reverence was too religious for UN floors; care sounded soft in policy documents. None of them held.

Mottainai held because it was not a verb at all. It was a way of seeing that produced all four R's as consequences. If you felt that a thing had mottai — a dignity of its own — you would, without further instruction, reduce your use of it, reuse what you could, recycle what you could not, and treat the remainder with regard. The Japanese word did in one morpheme what her English sentences had been doing in three.

What happened on the UN floor

When Maathai brought the word to the United Nations that February, she did something unusual. She did not translate it. She spelled it on a card, had the audience repeat it, and then used it untranslated through the rest of her remarks. She explained its pieces — mottai, the inherent dignity, and nai, the absence — but she did not offer an English substitute. She was, I think now, refusing the thing that translation usually does, which is to file a strange word down until it fits the listener's existing categories.

The refusal was strategic. A word that could not be reduced to an existing English concept was a word that could not be absorbed without changing the host language a little. Maathai understood this. She wanted the word intact. She wanted the discomfort of the untranslated term to do the work that a translation would have spared the audience.

This is not how most loanwords travel. Most are worn smooth on the way across. Tsunami means "big wave" in English even though it does not, quite, in Japanese. Karaoke has lost its Japanese syllable break. Maathai was asking for the opposite treatment. She wanted mottainai to arrive at the UN the way it left Kyoto — in its full, untidy, four-part grammar of dignity and absence.

What the word did outside Japan

For a time, it worked. Mottainai was printed on educational materials in Kenya, Tanzania, and Ethiopia. It appeared in European sustainability manifestos. A Mottainai Campaign was founded as a formal organisation, and it continues, in a reduced form, today. The word travelled further than almost any Japanese word that is not the name of a food.

And then, as words do when they travel, it began to lose pieces. By the mid-2010s mottainai was being used as a marketing term for products that violated it: compostable coffee pods sold in cardboard multi-packs wrapped in a second layer of plastic, fast-fashion sustainability lines produced in factories whose labour conditions no careful person would describe as respectful, a line of sponsored Instagram posts hash-tagged #mottainai for an airline loyalty programme. The word's power — that it could not be fully translated — turned out also to be its vulnerability. Because no one outside Japan could quite say what it meant, no one outside Japan could quite say when it was being used wrongly.

Maathai died in 2011, before the decoration era of the word began in earnest. I think about this often. I do not know what she would have made of a conference tote bag that said mottainai in clean sans-serif on one side and contained, when unpacked, four single-use water bottles on the other. I suspect she would have laughed, once, and then continued using the word anyway, because the alternative — letting its misuse kill it — would have been the larger waste.

What it meant that she chose a Japanese word

There is a reading of Maathai's choice that I want to push back against gently. It is tempting, particularly in Japanese press coverage at the time, to treat her use of mottainai as a kind of validation — the Kenyan laureate confirming, from outside, that Japan's traditional virtues are globally significant. That reading flatters Japan and underestimates Maathai.

She did not choose the word because it was Japanese. She chose it because it worked. If she had encountered an equivalent Kikuyu, Yoruba, or Quechua term first, she would almost certainly have carried that one to the UN instead. Mottainai was not, for her, a flag. It was a tool, picked up from the nearest workbench, which happened to be in Kyoto.

This matters because the Japanese retelling of Maathai's relationship to the word can drift toward something faintly proprietary — as if the country of origin has a special claim on its correct use. It does not. Once a word travels, it belongs to whoever is thinking with it. The word my grandmother used over the kitchen sink in 1962 is no more authentically mottainai than the word a Kenyan schoolteacher uses now while showing her students how to fold a reusable bag.

What remains

Twenty years after the UN speech, mottainai has settled into something quieter outside Japan. The campaign exists. The word appears in environmental curricula from Nairobi to Helsinki. It is no longer a flash of novelty; it has been absorbed, unevenly, into a handful of international vocabularies. Most English speakers still cannot quite define it. A smaller number — the ones who have spent time with it — can use it accurately without defining it, which is probably the condition Maathai wanted it to reach.

What I take from her use of the word, sitting at my desk in Tokyo in 2026, is a reminder that languages are workshops, not museums. The words that matter most are the ones that travel, get bent slightly by use, and still do the work they were made for. Mottainai bent. It did the work. It is still, imperfectly, doing it.

A closing scene

In a small park in Nairobi there is, I am told by a reader who sent me a photograph, a Green Belt Movement plaque on which the word mottainai is carved beside the Swahili phrase heshima kwa dunia — respect for the earth. The two are not a translation of each other. They are two ways of arriving at the same intuition from opposite sides of the Indian Ocean. Maathai would have liked, I think, that they are printed together without a bridge between them. The reader is expected to do the bridging. That is what she had been asking her audiences to do all along.
