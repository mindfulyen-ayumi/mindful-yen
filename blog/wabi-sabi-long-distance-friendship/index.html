Wabi-Sabi and Long-Distance Friendship

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

A letter arrived from my old university friend Mariko on Friday afternoon. It was in a pale grey envelope with a Sapporo postmark, addressed in the slightly tilted hand I have known since we were nineteen. Inside were three small pages, written on washi paper, describing the late spring in Hokkaido — the cold mornings, the persimmon tree in her parents' garden which had unexpectedly survived another winter, the small radio in her kitchen that had finally stopped working after twenty years. The letter took her perhaps an hour to write. It will take me longer to reply. The previous letter from her arrived in late October. The one before that, in March of last year. We have been writing each other on this rhythm — three or four letters a year, no messaging apps in between — for almost a decade.

This essay is about a friendship that has been allowed to breathe for ten years across a thousand kilometres, about what the wabi-sabi tradition knows about the long silences between people, and about why a small number of slow friendships have come to feel, in my mid-thirties, like the only kind of closeness I now know how to keep well.

What we used to do, and what we stopped doing

Mariko and I met in our second year of university. We lived in the same student residence in Tokyo for two years, then in a small flat together for a third, and we wrote our undergraduate theses at the same kitchen table in the spring of our final year. After graduation she moved back to Sapporo to be near her parents, who were beginning to need her, and I stayed in Tokyo for an editorial job. The flat we shared was emptied in a single afternoon. The friendship was assumed to continue.

For the first three years it did, in the way friendships continue now — long messaging chains, photos sent back and forth, occasional video calls, a steady low-volume contact that meant we were never out of touch but were also, increasingly, never properly in touch. The messages were small. They were also relentless. By the third year I had begun to notice, with some quiet shame, that I was opening her messages with a kind of reluctant tiredness — not because I did not love her, but because the steady stream of small contact had begun to feel like maintenance work rather than friendship. I suspect she felt the same. Neither of us said so.

The shift happened, by accident, after a long trip I took to Kyoto in which I left my phone almost entirely alone. I came back to a backlog of perhaps eighty messages from her, accumulated over twelve days. I read them all. I could not, honestly, distinguish one from another. They had blurred into a single texture of her absence-presence. I sat at my desk that evening, opened a piece of paper, and wrote her a letter — a real letter, four pages, by hand. I posted it the next morning. Three weeks later her reply came back. Four pages, by hand, a different colour of ink. We have not used the messaging app for anything substantive since. That was nine years ago.

The wabi-sabi reading of distance

The wabi-sabi sensibility, as Sen no Rikyū articulated it in his tea practice, is partly a sensibility about spacing. The tea room is small and arranged with deliberate emptiness. The conversation in it is sparse. The silences between sips are not failures of conversation; they are the conversation. The ceremony rests on the recognition that closeness is not produced by saturation. It is produced by a particular quality of attention that requires room around it.

This is not the contemporary instinct about closeness. The contemporary instinct, shaped by a decade of always-on messaging, is that frequency equals love. The more often you contact a person, the more you must care about them. The reverse — that you must not care, because look how seldom you message — is the implicit accusation underneath any long silence in a modern friendship. I have lived inside both versions of this assumption. The first version makes friendships feel like work. The second version makes the silences feel like crimes.

What the wabi-sabi reading offers is a different framework entirely. The friendship is not measured in messages per week. It is measured in a kind of quiet that holds across time — the assurance that when the next contact happens, the connection will still be there, intact, perhaps deeper for the silence. Mariko and I do not message in between letters because the letters are doing the work of the friendship. The silences are not gaps. They are the page.

What the slowness has produced

Three things have come out of the nine-year experiment that I did not expect.

The first is that her letters have become some of the most valuable physical objects in my apartment. They are kept in a small wooden box under the desk, in chronological order, perhaps thirty of them now. I re-read them sometimes. The handwriting alone — the slight slant, the way she crosses her sevens — produces in me a recognition that no digital archive of messages ever has. The letters are her, in a way the messages were never quite her. I will keep this box for the rest of my life.

The second is that the letters themselves have got better. The first ones were experimental, slightly stiff, still in the register of someone who had spent years writing small messages. By the third or fourth year of the practice, both of us had relaxed into the longer form. The letters became, on both sides, a kind of small monthly journal addressed to a single trusted reader. Mariko's letter on Friday described her radio in a way I will remember for years. None of her messages from 2014 contained anything I now remember.

The third — and this is the one I had to learn slowly — is that the long silences have made me trust her more, not less. When you have been in regular small contact with a friend for years, every short silence is data; you wonder if something is wrong. When the baseline contact is three or four letters a year, no silence is data. There is no anxiety in not having heard from Mariko in five months. The five months are the rhythm. The trust sits underneath the rhythm and is unaffected by it.

What is hard about it

I do not want to make the practice sound easier than it is. There are real costs.

The most honest cost is that I do not know the small daily texture of Mariko's life. I do not know what she ate for dinner on Tuesday or what she is currently watching or whether she has had a difficult week. The messaging-friendship version provides this texture continuously; the letter-friendship version does not. When her father died three years ago I learned about it in the next letter, not on the day. There was a small grief inside the grief — that I had not been there in the moment, that she had absorbed the first weeks of it without me. We have talked about this, in subsequent letters, and arrived at a small carve-out: large life events trigger a phone call. The letters resume afterward. So far, in nine years, this carve-out has been used three times.

The other cost is that the practice does not work for every friendship. Mariko and I both have temperaments that take to slow correspondence — we are both readers, both writers in our daily work, both quiet in social situations. A friendship between two more spontaneous people might wither under the same constraints. I do not advocate the letter-friendship as a universal model. I advocate it as a possibility that almost no one in my generation considers, and that turns out, for some friendships, to be exactly the right shape.

What it has done to my other friendships

The Mariko correspondence has, over the years, slowly affected the way I keep my other friendships too. I have not converted everyone to letters. Most of my close friends still live in Tokyo and we see each other in person; the medium for them is meals and walks, not envelopes. But the principle — that closeness does not require constant contact — has migrated outward.

I have become much less responsive on messaging apps and much more present when I am actually with someone. The messaging silences that would once have felt like neglect are now, in my circle, generally read as ordinary. The friends who have stayed close are the ones for whom this works. The few who could not adjust have drifted, gently, to a different category of acquaintance. I do not regret the drift. The friendships that remain are deeper for the small reduction in their number, and the messaging app is no longer the place any of them live.

There is a passage in Sōseki's Kokoro, the early-twentieth-century novel, in which the narrator reflects that the friendships that survive into one's middle years are usually a small number that have been allowed to take their natural shape — neither forced into closeness by frequency nor abandoned for distance. I read this in my mid-twenties and did not understand it. I read it again last year and understood every word. The natural shape is what the slow practice protects.

The letter, considered before it is replied to

Mariko's letter is on the kitchen table, beside the laptop where I drafted it open in front of me last night and decided to wait until morning to write the reply. I will not write it tonight either. The reply will arrive in its own time, probably this weekend, in the chair by the window with a fresh sheet of the cream paper I keep for her letters and the same fountain pen I use for everything important.

I will tell her about the spring in Tokyo, about the small persimmon I tried to grow on the balcony and which has not yet decided whether to live, about the book I am reading slowly in the chair, about my mother's hands which I have been thinking about. I will not summarise the news of the past eight months. The letter is not a status update. It is one thing well noticed, another, another, and the small private register of a single mind addressing a single trusted reader.

By the time she opens it in Sapporo, perhaps a week from now, the letter will have travelled the slow physical distance between us and arrived, in her hand, as an object — a small grey envelope, my slightly upright handwriting, the modest weight of three pages. The friendship will continue. The next reply will come, in its own time, before the autumn.

I write more about these slow domestic and relational rhythms in my Substack, which goes out once a week from Tokyo and is, in some ways, a small public letter to a slightly larger circle of trusted readers. The cream paper for the private letters is a separate thing entirely.
