A Personal Wiki in Notion, Japanese Style

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The English word for what I want to describe does not quite exist. Personal wiki is the phrase closest to the practice, but it imports the wrong assumptions — an encyclopaedia in miniature, a neat taxonomy, a reference work for a readership of one. The Japanese tradition closer to the actual experience is zuihitsu (随筆), literally "following the brush": short prose pieces written as the attention moves, gathered over years, arranged loosely, reread at intervals. Sei Shōnagon's Pillow Book, written at the Heian court in the late tenth century, is the tradition's most famous instance. It is, among many other things, a personal wiki.

The other tradition I lean on is European: the commonplace book, the bound notebook in which readers from the sixteenth to the nineteenth century copied out passages, pasted in clippings, and wrote short responses. Locke kept one. Montaigne kept one. Virginia Woolf kept several. The commonplace book has the same organising principle as zuihitsu — follow the thought, not the category — and the two traditions, laid side by side, describe what I have tried to build in Notion over the past four years.

Why not an encyclopaedia

Most Notion personal-wiki templates you will find online are miniature encyclopaedias. A database of topics. A hierarchy of categories. A neat taxonomy of books, films, ideas, people. They are beautiful on first inspection. They are also, almost without exception, abandoned within six months. I have built two of them myself and I watched both die the same death.

The problem is that an encyclopaedia is designed for a reader who does not yet know the material. A personal wiki is read by its writer, who does. The effort of maintaining an encyclopaedia is therefore paid up front — every entry has to be placed, categorised, cross-referenced — for a reader who, being the writer, does not need the scaffolding. The payoff never arrives. The effort is pure overhead.

Zuihitsu inverts the relationship. The writer does not arrange; the writer simply writes, and the arrangement, such as it is, emerges from rereading. What Sei Shōnagon produced in the Pillow Book is closer to a long thread than a classified file. Topics recur, but not on a schedule. A meditation on snow returns, three chapters later, transformed. The reader — the writer, rereading — finds the connections as the connections occur.

A Notion personal wiki built on this principle has a very different shape from the encyclopaedic one. It has fewer databases. It has more short pages. It relies almost entirely on search, tags as an afterthought, and the writer's rereading as its primary organisational force.

The structure, such as it is

My wiki is three things: a single database called fragments, a smaller database called recurrent themes, and a handful of long-form pages that exist outside both.

The fragments database is where almost everything goes. Each row is a short piece of prose, usually between a hundred and five hundred words. The fields are minimal: title (often just the first few words, or a provisional heading), date, source (book, person, conversation, observation, dream), and long text. There is no category field. There is no tag field, initially. The absence of categorisation is deliberate, and it is the single hardest thing to maintain if you have spent any time on the productivity internet.

The recurrent-themes database is smaller. Each row is a theme that has appeared often enough in the fragments to have earned a page of its own. The theme page collects, through a linked view, every fragment that refers to it. I add a fragment to a theme only when I notice, during rereading, that it belongs. I do not categorise as I write. I categorise as I reread. This distinction, which sounds small, is the entire mechanism.

The long-form pages are what eventually becomes a Mindful Yen essay, a Substack piece, or an entry in a notebook I am keeping for myself. They borrow from the fragments and from the theme pages. They are the output of the wiki, not the wiki itself. Conflating the two is the most common failure mode.

How rereading does the work

Once a month — on the same Sunday evening I use for the kakeibo monthly reflection and the weekly habit review — I reread the fragments from the previous month. I read them in date order, slowly, and I notice what I notice.

What I notice is the material. Three fragments from different weeks, I now see, are circling the same idea from different sides. A theme page already exists for that idea, or a new one needs to be created. I add the three fragments to it. A fourth fragment, which seemed important when I wrote it, I now see is thin; I leave it where it is, unadded, because an unpromising fragment is still a record of what I thought at the time, and deleting it would be a small dishonesty.

This monthly rereading is where the wiki does its actual work. The act of writing fragments is the raw material. The act of rereading is the manufacturing. Most personal-wiki systems fail because they try to manufacture at the moment of writing — tag now, categorise now, link now. The zuihitsu tradition suggests, and my four years of experience confirms, that this is the wrong moment. Tag, categorise, and link while rereading. The writing itself should be as close to unselfconscious as possible.

What counts as a fragment

Almost anything. A passage I copied out of a book, with a brief note on why. A conversation overheard in a café, with a thought about it. An observation from a walk. A memory that surfaced while doing dishes. A paragraph I cut from an essay and did not want to lose. A small insight I had while teaching. A question I could not answer.

What does not count as a fragment: reference material (which goes in a separate "references" database — bibliographies, URLs, addresses, the things that need to be findable but are not thinking), and tasks (which go in the three-state system I described in the project management essay). Keeping these three streams separate is the other half of what makes the wiki sustainable. A database that tries to hold fragments, references, and tasks simultaneously collapses into an inbox, and an inbox is the opposite of a wiki.

A small digression on the commonplace book

The European commonplace book is worth considering alongside zuihitsu because it solves a problem the zuihitsu tradition does not emphasise: what to do with other people's words. Locke's method, published in 1706 as A New Method of Making Common-Place-Books, was a specific scheme of indexing by the first vowel after the first letter of a keyword — a system that sounds absurd until one realises it was built for books with finite pages and no search function. When the page was the constraint, the index had to be ingenious. When search is free, the index becomes unnecessary.

Notion has free search. The Locke index is, therefore, vestigial. What remains useful from the commonplace tradition is the habit of copying out passages and annotating them, rather than merely highlighting them in the source book. A highlight in a Kindle is a gesture; a transcribed passage in a Notion fragment is a thought. The transcription is where the reading becomes mine. I rarely highlight anymore. I transcribe instead, and the transcription rate is approximately one passage per reading session, which is a more honest measure of what actually stayed with me.

Tags, eventually

I said earlier that the fragments database has no tag field initially. After about a year of use, I added one — cautiously, with four values only: self, other, observation, borrowed. These are the four origins of any fragment I have ever written, and the four-valued field has turned out to be useful in ways the finer-grained taxonomies never were.

Self means a thought I had. Other means something someone said to me. Observation means something seen or overheard. Borrowed means something read. Filtering for any one of these, months later, produces a surprisingly coherent body of work. Filtering my own self fragments from the last year, for instance, gives me something close to the shape of my actual mind during that period, which is a thing worth having. Filtering borrowed gives me the reading I actually did — not the books on the shelf, but the passages that entered my thinking.

Four tags is enough. I have tested five and I have tested twelve. Twelve becomes administration; five becomes fiddly; four is the honest minimum. I suspect this is not a universal number — it depends on the shape of the wiki-keeper's life — but four has been stable for three years now, and I would not change it without a good reason.

What the wiki is for

The practical function of the wiki is that it feeds the publication. Mindful Yen essays begin, almost without exception, as a theme page that has accumulated enough fragments to be ready. When I sit down to draft an essay, I have already done two-thirds of the thinking, distributed across months of fragments, and the drafting becomes a matter of arrangement rather than invention. This is the productive face of the practice. It is real. It is also secondary.

The primary function is harder to name. It is something like keeping an honest record of what has been paid attention to. Sei Shōnagon's Pillow Book is not primarily a writer's notebook; it is a record of a specific mind at a specific court, preserved because it was written. A personal wiki, kept on this model, is the same kind of record. It will survive its writer's memory. It will show, when reread in ten years, what the ten-years-younger self actually thought, not what she remembers thinking. This is a service to the future self that no other kind of notebook provides, and it is, in the end, why I keep it.

A closing thought

A personal wiki built on the zuihitsu model asks less of the writer than the encyclopaedia model, and returns more, because it trusts rereading to do the work that categorisation cannot. The single decision that makes it sustainable is the decision not to arrange at the moment of writing. Everything else — the tags, the themes, the monthly review — follows from that.
