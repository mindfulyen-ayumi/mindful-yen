The Imperfect Notion Template: A Wabi-Sabi Approach

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The Notion workspace I actually use — the one I opened this morning at 6:08 to log a small coffee purchase and two lines of a reading note — has three databases, one dashboard page with no cover image, and a single header that reads, in lowercase, home. There is a misaligned callout block on the dashboard that I have been meaning to fix for about eighteen months. There is a column in the reading database that is empty because I decided two years ago that I did not need it, and I have never gotten around to deleting it. There is a small typographical error in the monthly-reflection template that I notice each time I open it and continue not to correct. This is my Notion. It is the best one I have ever built, and it is the worst-looking one I have ever built, and the two facts are not coincidence.

This essay is about why. It is also, practically, about a wabi-sabi approach to Notion templates — what the principle looks like in actual databases, actual property types, actual dashboard layouts — and why the polished, aesthetic, creator-economy template you see on Twitter is almost always the wrong tool for a life you want to spend a long time inside.

The template industry's standard mistake

The Notion template market has matured into something close to the stock-photo industry. There are beautifully-produced templates for second-brain systems, for life OS dashboards, for habit trackers with seventeen connected databases, for content calendars that look like the back office of a small magazine. Many are sold for between fifteen and two hundred dollars. Some are quite well-made.

The standard mistake these templates share is not a design flaw. It is a category error. They treat a personal Notion workspace as a product — something that can be finished, delivered, and adopted — when a personal Notion workspace is, in fact, a practice. You are not buying a tool. You are buying someone else's habit, in the hope that the habit will adopt you.

This rarely works. The workspace that looks like a polished magazine on the day you import it begins, within a week, to accumulate your actual life: half-finished notes, a task you never completed, a database field you realise you did not want, a dashboard module whose purpose you have forgotten. Within a month the template looks shabby. Within two, most people abandon it, not because the tool failed but because the gap between the original polish and their lived-in version has become uncomfortable to look at.

A wabi-sabi Notion template does not have this problem, because it is already shabby on day one.

What wabi-sabi means for a digital workspace

I have written elsewhere about the careful sense of wabi-sabi — not "perfectly imperfect," but the medieval aesthetic tradition rooted in Sōtō Zen ideas of impermanence (mujō), asymmetry (fukinsei), and the weathered beauty of things that bear the marks of use. Leonard Koren's 1994 book is the canonical English entry point; Andrew Juniper's is the more careful philosophical treatment.

Applied to a Notion workspace, the principle is not aesthetic minimalism. It is a refusal of the product logic. A wabi-sabi Notion template is built to be lived in rather than delivered. It expects to be incomplete. It is designed around the pages and databases you will actually touch, and it leaves visible room for the ones you have not yet discovered you need.

Concretely, this means four things.

Four principles of a wabi-sabi Notion template

1. Fewer databases than feels reasonable. The polished template has nine. The lived-in template has three or four. My own workspace has three: one for the kakeibo ledger, one for reading, one for notes. Everything else is plain pages. Pages are cheap; databases are expensive, because each one demands schema decisions you cannot fully reverse. A database you created in an ambitious moment and now under-use is a quiet friction every time you open the workspace.

2. Leave room for asymmetry. A dashboard does not need to be balanced. The Japanese garden tradition values fukinsei — the deliberate off-centreness that makes a space feel alive rather than arranged. My dashboard has one column with three items and another with one. I know what is in the short column, it is the thing I need most, and the asymmetry is exactly what lets me find it without thinking. A symmetrical dashboard requires you to remember which quadrant a thing lives in. An asymmetrical one exploits the fact that attention is not evenly distributed.

3. Let fields accumulate marks. Notion's rename history, its creation dates, its subtle indicators of edit frequency — these are the digital equivalent of the scratches on a brass lamp. A column renamed three times is a column that has been thought about. A page that was created two years ago and is still open is a page that has earned its place. Do not periodically "clean up" the workspace to remove these traces. They are the grain of the thing.

4. The template is a seed, not a tree. The single most important principle. A wabi-sabi Notion template gives you the minimum viable structure — enough to start logging something useful on day one — and then gets out of the way. It does not anticipate every need. It does not prescribe every relation. It is the bowl, not the meal. The meal will arrive slowly, one purchase and one reading note and one evening reflection at a time, and the bowl will take the shape of what is put in it.

What the template actually contains

The kakeibo-and-notes template I use, and which I will describe here in enough detail that you could build it yourself in an hour, has the following structure. It is not beautiful. It works.

A dashboard page called home. Three sections: today's log, this week's glance, this month's reflection. Each is a linked view into one of the three databases, filtered to the relevant time window. No images. No emoji. One small quote at the top that I change about once a year.

A kakeibo database with six properties: date, amount, category (survival / optional / culture / extra — the four I have settled on after years), one-line note, week number (formula), month (formula). That is all. There is no sub-category field. There is no tagging system. There is no linked-relationship to a goals database, because I do not keep a goals database.

A reading database with four properties: title, author, started, finished. A page per book for notes. No rating system. No "to-read" database (those live as a plain list on a single page; relations to a Goodreads-style wishlist were a time sink I eliminated in year two).

A notes database with two properties: date and one short tag field. The body of each page is where the work happens. The tags are there only so that I can, maybe twice a year, find a cluster of related thoughts.

That is the whole template. If you build it, it will look sparse in a way that feels wrong for about a week. By week three, it will feel like the only shape of workspace you want to live inside.

What to delete from the template you already have

If you are reading this inside a polished Notion workspace you imported from a creator and have been slowly drowning in, the wabi-sabi intervention is not to build a new one. It is to delete from the one you have, carefully, over two or three weekends.

Start with databases you have not opened in thirty days. These are your dead weight. There will be more of them than you think. Archive, do not delete; the archive is the digital version of the wooden box under the desk where I keep old notebooks.

Next, the dashboard modules you have not looked at in a week. A habit tracker you silently abandoned in February is not a tracker. It is a mirror of a small abandoned aspiration, and it taxes every dashboard visit. Remove it. You can build it again in ten minutes if you actually want it back.

Next, the fields inside your remaining databases that you do not fill in reliably. A "mood" field in a reading database that is populated for three books and empty for forty is producing no information and a small visual irritation. Delete the field. The books you loved you will remember.

Finally, any cover image that was not chosen by you. The polished templates ship with stock photos of Japanese temples and Scandinavian kitchens. These images are other people's aesthetic; they do not belong in a workspace that is supposed to be a room of your own attention. Replace them with nothing, or with a single photograph you actually took.

The danger of template churn

One pattern worth naming, because I have seen it in dozens of readers and lived it myself. Many people, when a Notion workspace stops feeling right, respond by finding a new template. They import it, migrate what they can, abandon what does not fit, and feel a fresh burst of productivity for ten days. Then the new template begins to accumulate the same unfinished edges the old one had, and the cycle repeats.

This is the Notion equivalent of the productivity hack cycle. The problem is not the template. The problem is the expectation that a tool can do the slow work of a practice. A wabi-sabi workspace short-circuits this by being, from the beginning, the kind of thing that does not promise polish. There is nothing to be disappointed by. The dashboard was never supposed to sparkle.

The same instinct applies in other domains I have written about. The kakeibo ledger works because it does not promise to make your finances pretty; it promises to make them visible, over time, in exchange for a daily minute. The wabi-sabi home office works because it does not promise a showroom; it promises a room you can spend ten years in. The Notion template that survives is the one that makes the same small promise.

On the question of aesthetics

Readers sometimes ask whether a wabi-sabi Notion workspace has to be visually austere. The answer is no, and the question slightly misses the principle. Austerity is a look; wabi-sabi is a relationship to time. A workspace with a warm colour palette, a few handwritten-feel headings, and a single photograph you love can be fully wabi-sabi if it is otherwise sparse and lived-in. A workspace with monochrome Inter type and perfect spacing can fail the principle entirely if it is overbuilt and anxious.

The test is not how the workspace looks on the day you finish it. The test is how the workspace looks two years in, after it has absorbed a life. A good wabi-sabi Notion template becomes more itself with use. A polished creator template becomes less itself.

The small dignity of a half-built workspace

The misaligned callout on my dashboard has been there for eighteen months. I could fix it in forty seconds. I do not fix it because, at some point, it stopped being a flaw. It became part of the texture of the workspace I have actually lived inside. It is the workspace equivalent of the chip on my donabe, or the dent in my lamp, or the curl on the corner of the washi paper above my desk. Each of these would have been a problem in a product. None of them are a problem in a life.

A half-built Notion workspace, kept long enough, is not a sign of incompleteness. It is a sign that the tool is serving its purpose — which is to hold a slow practice, not to display a finished self. The best template is the one you stopped trying to finish, and started living in.
