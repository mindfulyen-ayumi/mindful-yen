What My Grandmother's Kakeibo Taught Me

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

My grandmother kept a kakeibo for fifty-one years. I inherited the ledgers after she died — seventeen volumes in total, each one the soft cream colour of long-used paper, stacked in a cardboard box that had originally held a sewing machine. I did not open them for almost a year. When I finally did, on a quiet weekend in February, I found that what she had left me was not primarily a record of money. It was a record of a marriage, the kind of record a woman of her generation could leave in no other form. This essay is about what that record taught me, and about the quiet economic literacy that has been passed through Japanese women for most of the twentieth century using the ledger as its instrument.

I should say, because it matters, that my grandmother was not a writer. She wrote almost nothing besides the ledgers. A few short letters to her sister in Osaka, preserved in a separate bundle. A handful of notes inside cookbooks. The ledger pages, by contrast, constitute a near-daily record running from 1958 to 2009. If you imagine the document the wrong way, it is seventeen volumes of numbers. If you read it the right way, it is the clearest portrait of her I will ever have.

The early years — the first marriage entries

She began the first ledger in the autumn of 1958, a few months after her wedding. The handwriting is careful and uncertain, the letters slightly too large, as if she were writing with an audience she could not see. The categories are the standard four, but the entries in those first months have a particular quality I now recognise: she is writing as though someone will check the numbers, and the someone is my grandfather, and the act of writing is as much a performance of domestic competence as it is a record of spending.

By the end of 1959, the handwriting has relaxed. The entries are smaller, quicker. She has stopped performing and started observing. The optional column, in those first months, had been almost comically restrained — a few small entries for what the 1950s Japanese household would have called minor indulgences. From 1960 onward, the optional column grows slowly and steadily. She is learning, by watching her own data, what her actual household needs rather than what she had assumed a good wife's household should need. No one was teaching her this directly. The ledger was doing it.

This is the first lesson the inherited volumes gave me. Kakeibo is not only a financial practice. For a generation of Japanese women who were expected to manage household money without meaningful training, the ledger was also a silent curriculum in their own preferences and limits. It taught my grandmother what she actually thought was important — information that no conversation with her husband, her mother-in-law, or her contemporaries was likely to produce with any honesty, given the period she lived in. The page could hear what the room could not.

The 1970s — the negotiated household

The middle volumes, from roughly 1969 to 1979, are the ones I return to most. Something in her practice changed in that decade, and I can see it in the ledger even though she never wrote about it directly.

The change is small. The fourth question — how can I improve next month? — begins to produce answers in her handwriting that are less about cutting and more about negotiating. A 1972 entry notes, in her small careful hand, that the fabric purchases for the children's school uniforms had come in higher than expected and that next month she would speak with Tanaka-san about sharing bulk purchases. A 1975 entry mentions that the optional column's pattern suggested she was buying her husband's favourite tea more often than the household could afford, and that she would discuss this with him that evening. A 1978 entry, the one I cannot read without a small ache, simply notes that the monthly savings figure had fallen for the third consecutive month and that she had made a decision about it which she did not write down.

What I see in these entries is an economic literacy that predates any formal term for such literacy. She was observing her own household's financial dynamics, identifying the levers that mattered, and intervening through the specific conversational mechanisms available to a Japanese woman of her period and station. The ledger did not produce the literacy; it amplified and organised the literacy she already had. Without the ledger, those interventions might still have happened, but the pattern recognition that guided them — the awareness of three consecutive months, of more often than the household can afford — would have been far harder to sustain.

I think often, reading these volumes, about how much of Japan's twentieth-century economic resilience was produced inside thousands of similar kakeibo, by thousands of women whose formal employment was limited or non-existent and whose financial authority was therefore concentrated in the management of the household budget. A country whose households were systematically observed by competent domestic economists is a different country from one whose households were not. The statistics on Japanese household savings in the postwar period, which are remarkable on any international comparison, are almost certainly not separable from this practice. The ledger was not just personal. It was structural.

The 1980s — the bubble years

The Japanese asset-price bubble of the late 1980s appears in my grandmother's ledger in an unexpected way. The numbers do not change dramatically. The prices she records for groceries, utilities, and small purchases track ordinary inflation. What changes is the tone of the fourth-question reflections.

Through 1986, 1987, and 1988, her reflections begin to note, in increasingly sceptical language, the spending patterns of her neighbours and her younger sister. A 1987 entry observes that the Yamamoto household appeared to have purchased a second vehicle despite there being only one driver in it. A 1988 entry notes, drily, that her sister's Osaka flat had been redecorated for the second time in three years, and that the stated cost did not seem plausible given what she knew of her sister's husband's income. In 1989, at the height of the bubble, her reflections become shorter and, to my reading, almost protective in their restraint. She had, I think, decided not to write certain things down.

The 1990s, when the bubble burst, produced a different register. Her reflections remained calm. The household, it turns out, had been quietly saving at an above-normal rate throughout the late 1980s — the savings figure I can track through the monthly intake pages was rising steadily while the country around her was not saving. She never explained why. The closest thing to an explanation is a short reflection from 1985, well before the peak, in which she noted that her father had lived through the late 1920s in Tokyo and had mentioned, in his last years, that one could usually see a sudden quietness coming if one was willing to count.

This is the second lesson. The ledger, kept faithfully over decades, becomes a slow instrument of economic judgement that is not available to anyone who does not keep one. My grandmother saw the bubble clearly enough to protect her household's capital from it — not because she was a market analyst, but because she had been counting for thirty years, and the counting produced a sense of the normal against which the abnormal became visible. No one taught her to do this. The years of her own ledger taught her.

The later volumes — the widow years

My grandfather died in 2003. The ledger of the following year is the one I find hardest to read. The categories remain the standard four. The numbers contract appropriately. The fourth-question reflections, however, change entirely. They are shorter. They are sometimes only a sentence. For the first time in the archive, several months in late 2003 have no reflection at all — only the tally, and a blank space beneath it.

By 2004 the reflections return, gradually, but their content has shifted. She begins writing, for the first time in her half-century of keeping the ledger, about the emotional shape of the household rather than only its numerical shape. A 2005 entry notes that the culture column had grown because she was reading more in the evenings. A 2007 entry observes that she had stopped buying the particular brand of tea he had preferred and that the optional column had quietly contracted as a result. A 2009 entry, the last substantive one before her final illness, says simply that the month's savings figure was more than sufficient and that she would be donating part of it to the local school. She underlined the sentence. It is the only underlining in fifty-one years.

The third lesson the ledgers gave me is that kakeibo, carried long enough, becomes something other than a financial document. The numbers are still numbers, but they carry a life that the numbers alone cannot express. She had, over five decades, built a practice whose capacity had grown to meet the scale of the feelings she needed to hold. The ledger, in its final years, was not recording her spending. It was recording her.

What I have taken from her

I keep my own kakeibo more carefully because of hers. I write by hand, where once I had considered moving fully digital. I underline almost nothing, saving the gesture for entries I mean to be able to find again. I allow the fourth-question reflections to drift, sometimes, into content that is not strictly financial, because she taught me that the practice can hold more than it claims to.

I have also, for the past several years, kept a short parallel document — a small notebook in which I record, at the end of each year, the things my current ledger has taught me that I did not know at the beginning of that year. It is a thin volume. Some years' entries are a single sentence. Over time, however, the document is becoming something I hope my own descendants will find useful, not as advice but as evidence that a long-kept ledger produces a kind of knowing that cannot be produced any other way.

If you have just begun a kakeibo, the lesson from the inherited volumes is that the practice is worth keeping even when — especially when — its immediate financial rewards seem modest. The compounding is not only in the savings. It is in the quality of attention you will bring to your own life twenty and thirty years from now, and in the coherent record you will leave for anyone who loves you.

The longer essay on the Japanese budget method covers the structural mechanics that my grandmother used, unchanged, from 1958 to 2009. The four questions are the ones she answered fifty-one years in a row. The Mindful Yen Substack sometimes carries short extracts from her ledgers, translated and lightly annotated, when a particular month's entry feels worth sharing.

A final note

My grandmother did not consider her ledger important. She kept it because it was what a competent wife of her generation did, and because Hani's magazine had told her to when she was young, and because the practice had become the quiet architecture of her domestic evenings. She would have been surprised, I think, to learn that I read the volumes now, or that I write essays about them, or that the small daily act of writing down the price of vegetables turned out to be among the most enduring things she gave me. She once told me, when I was perhaps twelve and had asked her what she had learned from her marriage, that a woman who kept her own accounts could not easily be deceived about her own life. I understood her only partly at the time. I understand her better now.
