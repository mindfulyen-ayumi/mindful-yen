How Mottainai Changed the Way I Shop for Groceries

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The bag of salad was at the back of the bottom shelf, half-hidden behind a jar of pickles. It had been there for ten days. When I pulled it out, the leaves had gone the translucent amber of autumn, and the bag had that faint, sour exhalation of a thing that has been forgotten. I stood with it in my hand for a moment in the pale kitchen light, feeling the small, familiar failure — not an ethical failure, exactly, but something closer to a small breach of hospitality. I had invited this salad into my home and then abandoned it behind pickles.

Most of my changes in grocery shopping over the last five years have started at that shelf. I do not keep a perfect kitchen. The export image of Japan — the housewife who uses every fish bone for stock and every daikon peel for tsukemono — is real for some households and not for mine. What I have instead is a slow list of rules, arrived at by ordinary failure, that have made my weekly shop quieter and the back of my bottom shelf less of a graveyard.

This essay is those eight rules. They are not Japanese wisdom handed down. They are the practical residue of living inside a word — mottainai — and trying, over a long time, to bring a Tokyo supermarket habit into line with what my grandmother would have recognised.

Rule one: shop against the fridge, not against a recipe

For years I shopped against recipes. I would pick a dish on Sunday morning, list the ingredients, buy them, and come home with a paper bag in which two-thirds of the items were already duplicated, in smaller quantities, in the fridge. I now shop almost entirely against the fridge. Before I leave the apartment I open every shelf, every drawer, and I look. I do not make a list of what is there. I just let it register. Then I go to the shop and fill in the gaps.

The result is that the salad behind the pickles rarely happens anymore. Not because I am more organised — I am not — but because my shopping is now a continuation of what is already in the kitchen rather than an independent event.

Rule two: buy fewer fresh things, but closer to now

I used to do a large weekly shop. It looked efficient. It was, in practice, a guarantee that a third of what I bought would die quietly in the fridge. I now do two or three small shops a week — ten minutes each, on the way home from somewhere I was already going. Japan makes this easy; nearly every neighbourhood has a small supermarket within a five-minute walk. Many Western cities do not, and I understand that this rule does not travel cleanly. The underlying principle, though, does: buy fresh things closer to when you will eat them, in smaller quantities, even if each trip is slightly less "efficient" on paper.

Rule three: look at the back of the shelf first, every time

Japanese supermarkets, like supermarkets everywhere, put the freshest items at the back and the closer-dated items at the front. The cultural habit of reaching past the front row for the "newer" one is strong here too. I have reversed this in my own shopping. I look at the front row first. If the front row is within a day or two of its date and I will actually use it in that window, I take it, without ceremony. The supermarket near my station puts orange hangaku (half-price) stickers on its late-afternoon tofu and fish; I have arranged my errands, quietly, around them.

This saves a small amount of money. More importantly, it reframes the act of shopping from "I am selecting optimal newness" to "I am completing what is already here." That reframe is the entire mottainai pivot at the scale of the supermarket.

Rule four: the four-question sweep, before leaving the kitchen

Adapted from the kakeibo method I write about elsewhere. Before I leave for the shop, I ask myself four quick questions: What is already in the fridge? What is about to finish? What did I waste last week? What would I actually like to eat this week? The sweep takes under two minutes. It is almost the whole discipline.

The last question is underrated. Mottainai grocery shopping is sometimes sold as a kind of grim efficiency — use every scrap, never indulge. In practice, if you do not honour what you actually want to eat, you will buy the joyless version and then buy the thing you actually wanted on top of it, and both will rot. Ask what you want. Then buy it.

Rule five: one shelf, one species

This is a storage rule rather than a shopping rule, but it changes shopping behaviour. I have given each category of thing — vegetables, tofu and eggs, jars, dairy, leftovers — a single fixed place in the fridge. When I open the door I can see, at a glance, what I have. The salad does not hide behind the pickles because the pickles are somewhere else.

This sounds trivial. It is probably the single rule that has reduced my household food waste the most, because it removes the invisible spoilage problem at the source. If you only do one of these rules, do this one.

Rule six: finish-the-week Fridays

On Friday evenings I do not buy anything fresh. I look at what remains in the fridge and I cook it — whatever is closest to turning, in whatever combinations the ingredients permit. This is usually a quietly strange dinner. A cabbage that was meant for another dish becomes a simple stir-fry. The last third of the tofu goes into miso soup. A slightly tired piece of salmon is grilled plainly. The half-finished salad, honoured rather than abandoned, becomes a side.

These meals are rarely remarkable. Occasionally they are very good. Their real function is that they reset the fridge to nearly empty by Saturday morning, so that the week's shopping can begin from zero rather than from accumulated remainders. The accumulation is what kills a household's food. Finish-the-week Friday prevents the accumulation.

Rule seven: know your genuine convenience food

I write enough about slow kitchens that readers sometimes assume I do not use convenience food. I do. What I have learned to ask is not is this convenient but will this actually be used. There are specific convenience items in my kitchen at all times — a block of silken tofu, a packet of dried bonito flakes, frozen rice portions, a tin of mackerel in miso, three or four eggs — because they rescue dinners on the nights I come home too tired to cook. Buying them is mottainai-compatible because they are items I will certainly finish.

The non-compatible items are the ones I bought for an imaginary future self — the kale chips for a snack I never eat, the quinoa for a salad I never make, the rice-paper wrappers for the spring rolls I did not end up rolling. I have mostly stopped buying for imaginary selves. It was the most expensive category in my old shopping and the one with the highest rate of quiet death.

Rule eight: be honest about the Japanese paradox

If I am going to write about grocery shopping in Tokyo, I have to be honest about the packaging. My local supermarket sells onigiri triple-wrapped in plastic. It sells individual tomatoes on little foam trays. It sells cut vegetables in portioned bags designed for people cooking for one. Japan produces, by official count, over 5 million tonnes of edible food waste per year, and generates more per-capita plastic waste than almost any OECD country. The country I shop in is not the export image of mottainai. It is a country that loves its word and often fails it.

I do what I can inside this. I choose loose vegetables where they are available, which is not often. I carry a fabric bag and refuse the plastic one at the till. I buy the same block of tofu in the same plain plastic week after week, fully aware that this is the worst part of my shopping from an environmental standpoint, and that I do not know how to fix it given my neighbourhood's options. Mottainai does not require perfection. It requires honesty about where the practice meets the infrastructure of the country you actually live in.

What the shape of the week looks like now

Taken together, these eight rules have produced a grocery week that looks, from the outside, undramatic. I spend roughly twenty thousand yen a month on food for a two-person household — about average for Tokyo — but the fraction of that money that becomes amber-coloured salad at the back of the bottom shelf has dropped, as far as I can tell, to somewhere near zero. I am not measuring this precisely. I am going by the frequency with which I still feel that small, apologetic breach — the mottainai flinch — and it has become rare.

The rules are not elegant. They are the eight things that actually survived five years of trying. Take whichever ones match the shape of the shop and the fridge you actually have. Ignore the rest. Mottainai is not a system; it is the posture of not abandoning what you have already brought home.
