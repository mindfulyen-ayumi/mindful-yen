How to Track Cash Spending in a Kakeibo Without Going Mad

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

Cash is the hardest part of any honest household ledger. This is as true in Tokyo as it is anywhere. Japan is still, despite the slow crawl towards contactless payment, a meaningfully cash-based country — the small ramen shop near my flat takes only coins, my hairdresser prefers notes, and the vegetable stall by the station would be baffled by a QR code. A kakeibo that cannot account for cash cannot account for most of my month. And yet cash is the category where practitioners most often give up, not because the idea is hard but because the small administrative friction — the lost receipt, the forgotten vending-machine purchase, the ten-yen mystery at the end of the week — eventually grinds them down.

I have lived with this problem for the better part of a decade. What follows is not a system in any grand sense; it is the small set of habits that have allowed me to keep a truthful cash column without the practice becoming a second job. If you have tried to track cash and found it maddening, I suspect what you were missing is not willpower but ritual. The ritual is the whole trick.

Why cash resists tracking in the first place

A card purchase leaves a trail. Somebody, somewhere, has already logged it — your bank, the merchant, the card network — and the entry will eventually surface in a statement you can reconcile against. Cash leaves no such trail. The only record is the one you create in the moment, or reconstruct afterwards from memory and receipts. Both are fallible.

There is also a psychological factor. Cash feels lighter than it is. Hani, writing in 1904 about Meiji-era households, already noticed this: money that passes through the hand seems to leave it more easily than money that moves through an account. Modern behavioural research has confirmed this several times over. A coffee paid for in coins feels, somehow, like it happened to someone else. By evening the memory has dissolved. By Sunday the week's cash column is a ten-percent fiction.

The solution is not greater vigilance. Vigilance is a finite resource and you will run out of it in the third week. The solution is to make tracking so small, so patterned, and so attached to existing routines that it no longer asks anything of your attention.

The receipt ritual

The first habit, and the one that does the most work, is the receipt ritual. When I pay in cash for anything, I ask for the receipt. In Japanese this is a single phrase — reshiito onegaishimasu — and I say it without thinking, the way one says please. The receipt goes directly into the left-hand pocket of whatever I am wearing, not into the wallet, not into a bag. The left pocket is reserved for today's receipts only. This separation matters. A receipt that mixes with older receipts or with unrelated scraps of paper becomes invisible.

When I arrive home, before I take off my coat, I empty the left pocket onto the small tray by the entrance. This takes approximately four seconds. The receipts sit there until evening. I do not log them yet. I do not even look at them. They are simply collected.

Before bed, I log the day into my ledger — I use a paper notebook for daily entries and a Notion template for the monthly roll-up — and the receipts from the tray are the raw material. Each receipt becomes one line: date, amount, category, short note. Then the receipts go into a small envelope marked with the month. That is the whole evening process. It takes under three minutes on a normal day and perhaps six on a Saturday.

The critical piece is the pocket, not the notebook. Any system that relies on you remembering to reach into a bag or open an app at the counter will fail within a month. A pocket is passive. You do not have to remember it; you simply have to empty it.

The pocket notebook for the receiptless transactions

Not every cash transaction produces a receipt. Vending machines do not. Small donations to shrines during festivals do not. The hundred yen I give a colleague for a shared coffee does not. The tip I leave, in the rare context where tipping is appropriate, does not.

For these, I carry a small pocket notebook — the kind that fits in a shirt pocket, about the size of a business card holder. When I make a cash transaction that will not leave a receipt, I write one line immediately, before I have walked ten paces. Date, amount, what it was for, nothing else. The note takes perhaps fifteen seconds. The writing itself is the point; by the time I reach the next street corner, the transaction is already slipping from memory, and a note written on the corner is a note written from truth rather than reconstruction.

I used to resist this because it felt absurd — a grown woman standing on a pavement writing down that she bought a bottle of tea from a machine. I no longer feel absurd. Almost no one notices, and those who do tend to assume I am a journalist or a tourist. The notebook has saved more honesty than any other single habit in my kakeibo.

In the evening, the pocket notebook and the receipt tray are reconciled into the same day's page. Entries from the notebook are transcribed and then a small line is drawn through them. The notebook itself is replaced roughly once a quarter.

The weekly reconciliation

The receipt ritual and the pocket notebook together capture perhaps ninety-five percent of cash spending. The last five percent — the coins spent while distracted, the small purchase where the receipt was refused, the forgotten vending-machine drink — has to be handled structurally. This is what the weekly reconciliation is for.

On Sunday morning, with tea, I count the cash in my wallet. I compare it against what the wallet held the previous Sunday, minus the week's logged cash spending. If the figures match to within a few hundred yen, I am satisfied. If there is a larger gap, I do not attempt to chase down the missing entries. I write a single line in the ledger — unaccounted cash, Week of [date], ¥[amount] — and file it in the "extra" category.

This line is not a failure. It is a measurement. Over the course of a year, the size of my unaccounted-cash line tells me how faithful my practice has been. When the line is under one percent of monthly spending, I know the system is working. When it creeps above three percent, I know something in my routine has slipped — usually the pocket notebook has been left on a desk at home, or the receipt ritual has started to fray because I have been rushing.

The weekly reconciliation also serves another purpose. It forces me to handle the cash itself, physically, once a week. I see what is in the wallet. I notice if I have been carrying too much (which encourages casual spending) or too little (which encourages small card purchases that fragment the ledger). I adjust. Over time, I have learned that my own optimal wallet cash for a week is lower than I would have guessed — roughly the amount I will spend on food and transport with a small margin for the unexpected. More than that becomes drift.

The envelope at the end of the month

At month's end, the receipt envelope goes into a drawer with the previous months' envelopes. I do not throw it away. I do not scan it. I simply keep it, for the remainder of the fiscal year, and then dispose of it. The physicality matters. A stack of twelve small envelopes at year's end is a tangible object that represents a year of cash decisions. I have, twice in the past decade, opened a previous year's envelope to answer a specific question, and both times the receipts were there.

For readers who prefer an entirely digital version of this, the ritual can be adapted. A photograph of each day's receipts, taken before bed and filed into a monthly folder, works almost as well. The principle is the same: the raw material is captured at a specific moment, separate from the act of logging, and the logging happens against that raw material rather than against memory.

What to do when the system breaks

The cash-tracking habit will break. Not if, but when. A busy week, a trip away from home, an illness, a period of grief — any of these will interrupt the routine, and the routine once interrupted does not restart automatically. The handling of this moment is the difference between a practice that survives and one that does not.

The Japanese instinct here is to resume, not to repair. When I have missed three or four days of logging, I do not attempt to reconstruct them. I write a single line for the gap — gap, [dates], estimated ¥[amount] — and begin again the next evening. The temptation to reconstruct is, in my experience, the main reason practitioners abandon the system entirely. They miss a week, feel they cannot pick up without catching up, and then neither catch up nor pick up. Grant yourself the gap and resume. A ledger with three or four honest gap-lines per year is a working ledger. A ledger with none and a pristine appearance is usually a ledger that has been quietly edited after the fact.

If you are adapting this for a mostly cashless life — the Anglophone reader, especially — the same structures apply, with the receipt ritual shrunk. Cash purchases are rarer but also more distinctive; a pocket notebook entry for the occasional cash transaction, combined with a weekly glance at the wallet, is usually sufficient. The longer essay on the Japanese budget method covers the wider structure, and the four questions remain the same whether the money is paper or plastic.

The monthly template I use sits in a Notion workspace, with a small section at the top of each daily page for cash entries specifically. That separation — cash in its own row, card in its own row — has been the most useful small change I have made in the last three years.

A closing note

Cash tracking is the part of the kakeibo that most practitioners dread, and in my experience it is also the part that produces the most durable honesty. The electronic record will tell you what you bought. Only the cash column, faithfully kept, will tell you how you actually move through your days — the small coffees, the impulsive train-station snacks, the hundred-yen donations to shrines you happen to pass. That texture is what the Japanese budget method is really about. The card data is useful. The cash data is revealing.
