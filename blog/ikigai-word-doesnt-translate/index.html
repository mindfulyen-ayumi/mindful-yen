The Word Ikigai Doesn't Translate — And Why That's the Point

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The first time I was asked to translate the word ikigai into English, I was twenty-three, sitting across from a British editor at a café in Jimbocho, and I failed. I tried reason for living, which sounded grandiose. I tried sense of purpose, which sounded corporate. I tried that which makes life worth living, which was accurate and unwieldy. Eventually the editor, who had been patient, wrote down reason for being in her notebook and we moved on. I was dissatisfied with the answer and have been, in various ways, dissatisfied with every English rendering of the word since.

I am writing this essay because the failure of translation is not, I have come to think, a problem to be solved. It is a signal. The word ikigai does not cross into English cleanly, and the shape of the resistance tells you something real about what the word is actually doing in Japanese — something that the popular English version, the four-circle Venn diagram sold under the same name, has largely discarded.

The morphology, briefly

Let me begin with the word itself, because the English conversation tends to skip this part. Ikigai (生きがい) is a compound. The first element, 生き (iki), is the stem of the verb ikiru, to live. The second, がい (gai, written in kana but derived from the Chinese character 甲斐), is a suffix that carries a very specific sense — worth or value-commensurate-with-effort. You see the same gai in yarigai (the worth of doing something), hatarakigai (the worth of working), sodategai (the worth of raising, as of a child).

The gai morpheme does not exist in English. It is not worth in the sense of market value. It is not meaning in the sense of semantic content. It is something more textured — the quality a thing has when the effort you put into it returns something commensurate. A job that has hatarakigai is not necessarily well paid; it is work whose effort is not wasted on you. A life that has ikigai is not necessarily successful; it is a life whose living is not wasted on you.

English has no single word for this. It is possible to circumlocute — something that repays the effort of living — but the circumlocution is long, and the English reader quickly loses the thread. This is why every short English gloss of ikigai — reason to live, purpose, passion — flattens something essential. The flattening is not the translator's fault. It is the fault of the target language not containing the grammatical furniture.

Register: the small, not the grand

The second thing that does not translate is the register. In Japanese, ikigai is used at a level of ordinariness that English-language readers find startling. My aunt, who runs a small tea shop in Kanazawa, describes the first cup of the morning as part of her ikigai. My father describes a particular route he walks on Sunday mornings as his ikigai. None of this is hyperbolic. The word in Japanese is not reserved for the momentous.

English has a strong tendency to elevate borrowed words. When a foreign term enters English, it tends to arrive at the top of the register and stay there — zeitgeist, schadenfreude, saudade — even when in the source language the word is used as casually as any other. The same thing has happened to ikigai. English speakers, reaching for the word, reach upward; Japanese speakers, reaching for the word, reach across.

The psychiatrist Mieko Kamiya, in her 1966 Ikigai-ni-tsuite, is alert to this. She describes interviewing patients whose ikigai was visiting their grandchild, or feeding a cat, or waiting for a particular train. None of these answers is diminished by its smallness. The smallness is, in the Japanese grammar of the word, doing work. The neuroscientist Ken Mogi, in his 2017 book, makes a related point about what he calls kodawari — the quality of attending carefully to a small thing — as a precondition for ikigai rather than a substitute for it. Both writers take for granted a register of the word that English, when it borrows the term, almost always loses.

The Venn diagram as a translation error

It is worth naming, again, what happened when the word crossed the Pacific. In 2014, a British entrepreneur named Marc Winn took an existing Venn diagram about purpose — created in 2011 by the Spanish writer Andrés Zuzunaga — and swapped the centre label to ikigai. The diagram went viral. It now dominates English-language search results for the word.

The diagram is, structurally, a translation error. It equates ikigai with the overlap of what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. That equation is a reasonable gloss of a certain English-language career-coaching concept. It is not a gloss of the Japanese word. Nothing in the Japanese grammar of gai — the worth-commensurate-with-effort morpheme — requires that the worth be remunerated, or that it be large, or that it align with a market need. My father's Sunday walk satisfies the Japanese definition of ikigai and fails every circle of the Venn diagram simultaneously.

I have written a longer piece on the diagram's history elsewhere on this site. What I want to emphasise here is that the diagram's success in English is not a triumph of translation; it is what happened when a word whose native register was small and everyday was conscripted into a foreign register that was large and aspirational, because that is where the foreign audience's anxiety lived.

Why untranslatability is useful

The interesting move, when a word resists translation, is not to force it. It is to sit with the resistance. Untranslatable words are, in my experience as an editor, signposts pointing to blind spots in the receiving language. A language that has no single word for a concept often has no easy way of noticing the concept at all. When the word arrives from outside, it does not only import a meaning; it imports a capacity for attention that the receiving language was missing.

English, for all its vastness, has relatively weak vocabulary for the small, ongoing, unremarkable quality of a life that is worth its living. It has words for triumph, for ambition, for purpose, for calling. It has words for happiness and for satisfaction. What it lacks is a compact term for the quiet arithmetic of days that do not waste their liver. Ikigai fills, clumsily, precisely this gap. The fact that no English phrase quite catches it is not a defect of the word; it is a diagnosis of the language receiving it.

This is why, I think, the attempt to translate ikigai cleanly is misguided. The point is not to find the perfect English gloss. The point is to let the word's resistance teach English speakers to notice something the language had not given them a handle for. A word that translates easily does not change the speaker. A word that resists translation, held carefully, can.

What to do with an untranslatable word

A reader recently asked me, reasonably, what they were meant to do with all of this if they do not read Japanese. The answer is smaller than the question.

Use the word. Use it at the Japanese register, not the English one. When you ask yourself or a friend about ikigai, do not mean what is your life's grand purpose. Mean what, today, was worth the day. Let the answer be small. Let it be different tomorrow. Let it have nothing to do with your job. The word, used this way, begins to work in English, not because the translation has been solved, but because the user has stopped asking the translation to do something it cannot.

If you want to sit with this practice in more detail, the thirty short prompts I wrote for people who hate journalling are built on exactly this register. The weekly Substack continues the thread, often with short notes on Japanese words that similarly resist clean English translation.

Final thought

The word ikigai does not translate into English. It never quite has. The popular English version — the diagram, the career framework, the thirty-day programme — is not a translation so much as a substitution: an English-language concept wearing a Japanese name. The actual Japanese word is smaller, quieter, and more useful than the substitution has been. Its untranslatability is not an obstacle to understanding it; it is the first thing worth understanding about it. If you can hold the word lightly, without forcing it into an English equivalent, you are already closer to what it means in Japanese than most of the books on the subject. The resistance is the lesson.
