Kakeibo on Paper vs Notion vs Sheets: An Honest Comparison

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I have kept kakeibo on all three media over the years — paper ledger, Notion workspace, Google Sheets — sometimes in parallel, sometimes by phases. Readers ask me, often, which is best. The honest answer is that none is best in the abstract. Each medium does something that the others cannot, and each medium hides something that the others expose. The right question is not which medium is best but which medium reveals the thing your practice currently needs to see. This essay is my attempt to describe what each medium hides and reveals, based on several years of using all three.

I will not pretend to be neutral. I have a personal preference, which has shifted twice across my practice and may shift again. What I want to offer is not a ranking but a truthful map of what each medium's strengths and silences actually are, so that readers can choose well rather than choosing out of habit or fashion.

Paper: what it reveals, what it hides

Paper reveals weight. The act of writing an expense by hand, with a pencil, in a ledger that lives on your kitchen table, produces a measurable behavioural friction that no digital tool I have tested can replicate. The handwriting research I mentioned in the longer essay on the Japanese budget method — Mueller and Oppenheimer's work on deeper processing — is not an exotic finding. It is visible in one's own practice within about three weeks. Purchases logged by hand are, in my experience, about twenty percent fewer than the same life lived without handwriting. The difference is the implementation pause handwriting produces.

Paper also reveals narrative. An entry written by hand carries a small texture — the mood of the hand, the pressure of the pencil, the occasional crossed-out correction — that a clean digital row cannot. When I read back a paper month, I am reading a small diary of how that month felt, not only what it cost. Reading back a digital month feels different. The numbers are there, but the narrative is flattened.

What paper hides is aggregation. Running totals on paper require you to add by hand, which in practice means you do it less often than you should. The Saturday review I described elsewhere is more awkward on paper alone because the totals have to be recomputed each time. Paper also hides correlation. The seasonal pattern I identified in my own year of kakeibo — the autumn and spring spikes in the optional column — was not visible on paper. It became visible only when I ran the same year through a digital aggregation.

Paper hides, finally, search. Twelve months of paper entries cannot be queried. If you want to know how much you spent on books in the third quarter, you will have to read through three months of entries and total them yourself. This is fine for practices that rely on narrative. It is bad for practices that rely on pattern.

Notion: what it reveals, what it hides

Notion reveals structure. A well-built kakeibo workspace in Notion — the kind I have iterated on over the years, and which the template at the link I keep is based on — shows the four questions, the four categories, the running totals, and the monthly reflection prompts in a single consistent layout. The structure is itself a kind of discipline. It is much harder to skip the monthly reflection when the template is quietly waiting for it on the first of each month.

Notion also reveals linkage. The database structure allows a daily entry to carry not only amount and category but also tags for the person, place, or occasion — my own practice tags entries with the neighbourhood they occurred in, because I like to see which parts of Tokyo my spending clusters around — and these tags can be queried across months in ways paper cannot support. The linkage is most useful for the freelance adaptation I described in the freelance kakeibo essay, where client-side and household-side money need to sit inside one structured workspace.

What Notion hides is weight. Clicking an entry into a database is fast, and fast is the wrong thing for the daily log. The implementation pause that paper produces is almost entirely absent in a well-designed Notion input form. I have tested my own behaviour on this carefully. My Notion-only months show about fifteen percent higher optional spending than my paper-primary months, controlling for season. The template is not the problem. The speed is.

Notion also hides permanence. A Notion workspace that is deleted, or that loses its subscription, or that corrupts — all of which I have seen happen — takes the entire practice with it. Paper does not disappear because a company changed its pricing. This is a small but real argument for paper's durability against the longer time horizons the practice asks for.

Sheets: what it reveals, what it hides

Google Sheets, or Excel, reveals calculation. A spreadsheet is, at heart, a surface for computing, and the kakeibo's more quantitative elements — the rolling averages, the variance across categories, the comparison of budgeted to actual — are all at home in a spreadsheet. If your practice is drifting toward the numerical side, a spreadsheet gives you the most flexibility to see what the numbers are doing.

Sheets also reveals history. A single sheet file, well-structured, can hold many years of daily entries in a way that is always searchable and always queryable. I know practitioners who have kept the same kakeibo spreadsheet for a decade, adding a new tab each year, and whose decade-long records are genuinely analysable in ways no paper ledger could match. For a practitioner who cares about long-horizon trend analysis, sheets is the right medium.

What sheets hides is the reflection. Spreadsheets are, in my experience, the worst of the three media for the monthly reflection. The interface pushes you toward cells and formulas, which is the wrong posture for full-sentence reflection. Practitioners who use sheets as their primary medium tend, over time, to let the reflection atrophy. The numbers survive; the writing does not. This is a meaningful loss, because the writing — not the numbers — is what the fourth kakeibo question is asking for.

Sheets also hides, oddly, beauty. A well-kept paper ledger and a well-designed Notion workspace both look like something you might want to open. A spreadsheet, however cleanly built, rarely invites you to open it for pleasure. Over a year, this matters. The practice has to be approached willingly.

The hybrid approach, honestly described

My own settled practice, after iterating, is a hybrid. Paper handles the daily log and the monthly reflection. Notion handles the running totals, the quarterly rolling averages, the category dashboards, and the freelance buffer tracking. Sheets does not play a role in my current practice, though I keep a separate spreadsheet for my freelance tax calculation, which sits outside the kakeibo.

The hybrid is not elegant. It involves re-keying daily entries from paper into Notion at some point each week, usually during the Saturday review I described in the Saturday reset essay. The re-keying feels redundant at first. Over time I have come to see it as part of the practice's value. Re-keying an entry is a second pass of attention, a second small pause to consider the transaction. The month I looked at during the year of kakeibo where optional spending quietly crept up — I caught it during re-keying, not during the original logging.

For readers who want a single-medium practice, my honest recommendation is: paper first, for the first three months. Paper's behavioural weight is what builds the habit. After three months, add a digital layer if you need aggregation. Most practitioners do; some do not. If you do, I would lean Notion over Sheets for the reflection-preservation reasons above, but either works if the structure is designed honestly.

A few practical notes on each

For paper, the ledger format matters less than most writers about kakeibo suggest. An A5 bound notebook with a ribbon marker is my preference, but graph-ruled or lined works, and the four-category structure can be drawn in by hand in the first ten minutes of a new month. A printable kakeibo journal is available for readers who want the structure preprinted, but a plain notebook is equally valid.

For Notion, the main mistake I see in reader templates is over-engineering. A kakeibo in Notion should be three pages: the monthly dashboard, the daily log database, and the monthly reflection template. More than that, and the structure starts to obscure the practice rather than support it. The free Notion plan is sufficient for indefinite use; the paid tier adds no meaningful capability for this workload.

For Sheets, the main mistake is pretending the reflection can live inside a cell. It cannot. If you use Sheets as your primary medium, keep a separate document — a plain text file, a Google Doc, a paper journal — for the monthly reflection. The numbers and the writing need different surfaces.

What your medium should be

The short version. If you are just starting, buy a notebook. If you are keeping kakeibo for six months and want to see patterns across months, add Notion. If you are three years in and your practice has become primarily analytical — the freelance household running rolling averages, the household reviewing a decade of data — consider a Sheets layer alongside, but keep the paper journal for the reflection.

The medium is not the practice. The four questions, answered honestly in pencil once a month, are the practice. Any medium that supports honest answering is the right medium for you. Any medium that encourages evasion — the fast input form, the clean spreadsheet that hides the weight, the Notion template with too many dashboards — is the wrong medium, regardless of how elegant it looks.

For occasional notes on the medium question and how it shifts across a practice's lifespan, I write about this on the Mindful Yen Substack from time to time. Readers have written in with their own hybrids, and the variety is genuinely larger than I had imagined before I started asking. There is no orthodoxy. There is only what each person's honest answer requires.
