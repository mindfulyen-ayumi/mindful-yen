A 30-Day Ikigai Reflection for People Who Hate Journaling

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

I have never been a good journaller. I own three unused notebooks, all bought in a spasm of optimism after reading a morning-pages essay, and all abandoned somewhere around day nine. Each time, the shape of the failure was the same. The first few days were earnest. Then the prompts began to feel like homework. Then I missed a day, then two, then I closed the notebook and did not open it again.

So I want to say, at the start of this, that I am not writing as someone who has solved journaling. I am writing as someone who gave up on the confessional, three-page, full-hearted version of it and built a much smaller practice that I have actually kept. What follows are thirty short prompts, one a day for a month, designed specifically for readers who hate journaling. Each prompt is meant to be answered in one or two sentences. If you need ten minutes, you are doing it wrong.

The frame is ikigai — not the four-circle Venn diagram, which I have written about separately, but the small, everyday Japanese sense of the word. What, today, was worth it. Thirty small answers to thirty small questions, and at the end of the month, a quiet shape emerges.

Why short prompts work when long ones fail

The problem with most journal templates is that they ask too much. A prompt like describe a time you felt most alive and reflect on what it means for your life's direction is a short essay, not a question. It presupposes that you arrived at your notebook with the energy to write an essay, on a Tuesday, after work, before bed. Most days, you did not. You came with the energy to write one sentence.

Mieko Kamiya, the Japanese psychiatrist whose 1966 book Ikigai-ni-tsuite remains the serious Japanese work on this subject, wrote that the felt sense of meaningfulness — what she called ikigai-kan — is built out of accumulated small noticings rather than dramatic revelations. The accumulation is the practice. A single sentence a day, for thirty days, produces thirty sentences. Thirty sentences, read back in one sitting at the end of the month, say something a three-page morning page does not. They show you what was actually there when you were not trying.

The writer and neuroscientist Ken Mogi, in his 2017 book The Little Book of Ikigai, makes a related point about what he calls the pillar of starting small. The Japanese bias, when it comes to daily practice, is toward the manageable. A tea master does not begin by mastering the full ceremony. They begin by learning to fold a cloth. Thirty one-sentence prompts are, in that sense, a folded cloth. Do that first.

How to use the prompts

The instructions are deliberately minimal, because complicated instructions are the second reason journalling practices fail.

  1. Choose a time of day. Evening is traditional but not required. I do mine before I brush my teeth.
  2. Read the day's prompt. Answer in one or two sentences. Stop.
  3. If nothing comes, write nothing came today, close the book, and move on. Do not force it. A missed prompt is not a failure; a missed prompt is data.
  4. On day thirty, read all thirty answers in one sitting. Notice what clusters. Do not analyse.

If you would prefer a digital version rather than a paper one, I keep mine in a simple Notion page with thirty checkboxes and a short text field for each. The Notion template I use is here for readers who want a ready-made version on the free plan. Paper works equally well. The tool is not the practice.

The thirty prompts

Week one: small noticings.

  1. What, today, was worth the day?
  2. Name one thing you saw this morning.
  3. What did you eat that you actually tasted?
  4. Who did you speak to that you were glad to speak to?
  5. What was the best five minutes of the day?
  6. What small thing almost made you stop?
  7. Where did the hour go when you were not watching?

Week two: the shape of the days.

  1. What did you do today that you would be happy to do again tomorrow?
  2. What did you do today that you would rather not repeat?
  3. When did you feel most like yourself?
  4. When did you feel least like yourself?
  5. What did your body tell you, and did you listen?
  6. What did you postpone that you are glad you postponed?
  7. What did you postpone that is now heavier?

Week three: people and places.

  1. Whose presence made the day lighter?
  2. Whose presence did you miss?
  3. Which room in your home treated you kindly today?
  4. Was there a street, a view, a window that held your attention?
  5. What did someone say that you are still thinking about?
  6. What did you say that you wish you had said better?
  7. Who would you like to write to this week?

Week four: the quiet shape.

  1. What small thing, done today, do you suspect you will remember in a year?
  2. What did you make — a sentence, a meal, a plan, a mark — that you are glad to have made?
  3. What would you like tomorrow to contain?
  4. What would you like tomorrow not to contain?
  5. If today were the whole month, what would the month be about?
  6. What have you been avoiding that wants attention?
  7. What is one thing you now know that you did not know on day one?
  8. Read back your answers from days one through twenty-eight. What repeats?
  9. Looking at what repeats — what, quietly, seems to be your ikigai this month?

What the pattern usually looks like

I have now run this sequence three times, and I have persuaded perhaps a dozen friends to run it, and the shape that emerges is remarkably consistent. Very few people end the month discovering a grand vocation. Almost everyone ends the month with a short list of small, recurring things that turn out to have been carrying the weight of their days — a particular window, a particular hour, a particular person, a specific kind of work.

A friend who ran the thirty days last winter told me that her list, when she read it back, was embarrassingly simple. Morning coffee before her children woke. A twenty-minute walk between two train stations she had been taking for years without thinking. A weekly phone call to her sister. She had been convinced, going in, that she would discover she needed to change careers. What she discovered instead was that her days already contained her ikigai, and she had simply not been noticing.

This is, I think, the most honest thing I can tell you about the practice. It will probably not reveal a new direction. It will probably reveal what was already there. The revelation is unspectacular and is exactly the point.

What to do on day thirty-one

A common question I get is what to do after the thirty days are up. The honest answer is: very little. The practice has done its work by then. You have thirty sentences, a shape, and a slightly sharpened attention. You do not need to start a new thirty-day cycle immediately. Some readers find it useful to repeat the sequence each quarter; others do it annually; some do it once and never again, and that seems fine too.

What I would gently suggest is the small ritual I have kept myself: on the first evening of each new month, write a single sentence answering the first prompt — what, today, was worth the day — and leave it at that until the next thirty-day round, whenever that comes. One sentence a month is not a journalling practice. It is barely anything. But it keeps the attention that the thirty days produced from quietly dissolving. Final thought

Journalling, as the self-help industry sells it, is an overcommitted practice aimed at people who were not going to keep it. The thirty-day version offered here is deliberately small — one sentence, one prompt, one month — because small is what gets kept. The ikigai it uncovers will not look like the Venn diagram. It will look like your actual days, slightly better seen. That is more useful than the diagram ever was.
