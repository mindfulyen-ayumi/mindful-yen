Wabi-Sabi in the Kitchen: Three Tools I'd Replace, Three I Wouldn't

By Ayumi Sato · Tokyo

The kettle on my stove this morning is a small enamelled iron one in a deep oxblood colour, with a chip on the spout the size of a fingernail. The chip happened in my second year of marriage, when I dropped it onto the tile floor while reaching for the salt. I painted over the bare metal with a small dot of black enamel from a model-paint shop in Asakusa, and the repair has held for almost a decade. Most mornings I do not notice it. Some mornings — when the steam rises and the early light from the kitchen window catches the dark patch — I notice it and feel the small private satisfaction of a thing kept.

This is an essay about a wabi-sabi kitchen, which is to say a kitchen built on the assumption that some things are worth keeping forever and some things are not, and that the difference between them is rarely the price.

What a wabi-sabi kitchen is not

A wabi-sabi kitchen is not a beige Pinterest board with linen aprons and a single sprig of rosemary in a brown vase. It is not the absence of plastic, or a vow against modern appliances, or a curated row of identical glass canisters. The wabi-sabi tradition — the one that runs through Sen no Rikyū's tea practice, through Sōtō Zen's reading of mujō (impermanence), through the boro mending tradition of patched indigo cloth — does not actually have aesthetic rules in the way Pinterest does. What it has is a sensibility. Things are honest about their material. Things are allowed to age. Things that have been used for a long time, with care, become more valuable, not less. Crispin Sartwell's Six Names of Beauty calls this "wabi" — beauty that emerges from modesty and a kind of weathered austerity — and treats it as a category of beauty in its own right, distinct from the Greek classical line.

In a kitchen, this means the test of an object is not whether it photographs well. It is whether you would replace it if it broke. The honest answer to that question is what sorts your shelves.

The three I would replace tomorrow

I keep a quiet running list, in the back of one of my notebooks, of objects in my kitchen I would not be sad to see go. The list is shorter than I would have guessed. Three items have been on it for years.

1. The plastic rice scoop that came with the rice cooker

It is white. It is faintly textured. It has discoloured to a pale beige along the edge that touches the rice. It is functionally fine and aesthetically dead. I have meant for years to replace it with a small wooden shamoji from a kitchenware shop in Kappabashi — the kind made from a single piece of cypress, slightly rough on the back, smoother on the front from a hundred years of being held. The cypress one would cost about fifteen hundred yen. I have not bought it because I have not yet been to Kappabashi when the right shop was open. The plastic scoop continues to do its job. It will be replaced.

2. The non-stick frying pan whose coating is failing

The non-stick coating on my medium frying pan started to lift along the edge about a year ago. It still cooks. It is also no longer non-stick in any meaningful sense, and the slow flaking is the kind of slow flaking that makes me uneasy. A wabi-sabi sensibility is not romantic about decay; it distinguishes between the patina that improves a thing (the chip on the kettle) and the degradation that ruins it (a coating breaking down). The frying pan is in the second category. It will be replaced with a carbon-steel pan that will get better with use, blacken with seasoning, and outlive me. I have been waiting for the right one — several Etsy sellers I follow make hand-forged Japanese-style pans — and when it arrives the non-stick goes in the metal recycling bin without ceremony.

3. The matching set of glass tumblers

Six identical glasses. Bought at a department store in our first year here. They are perfectly fine. They are also perfectly forgettable, and I notice that the two I reach for most often are not from this set at all — they are a pair of small uneven tumblers a friend gave us, blown by a glass-maker in Niigata, slightly different from each other in height and thickness. The matching set will be slowly retired, glass by glass, as the uneven ones I prefer accumulate. Six identical objects on a shelf is a kind of refusal to choose. A wabi-sabi shelf chooses.

The three I would never replace

The other side of the list is shorter still, and the items on it are not expensive. They are simply the ones that have earned their place.

1. The enamelled iron kettle

The one with the chip and the dot of black model-paint. It boils water in about three minutes. It rests on the back of the stove between uses. It has a presence that no replacement could buy, because the presence is the decade of mornings, not the kettle itself. A new identical kettle would feel like a stranger in the kitchen. This is what wabi-sabi means by an object becoming a thing — the long, slow accumulation of use until the object is no longer interchangeable with another of its kind. Sōtō Zen, in its quieter moments, would call this aji — the flavour an object acquires through long companionship.

2. The donabe

My donabe — the round earthenware pot for rice and stews — is the heaviest single object in the kitchen. It was made by a small kiln in Iga, and it has the pale unglazed exterior of clay that has been fired at a high temperature with very little decoration. The lid has a small chip from the day I lifted it too quickly with one hand. The interior has darkened over the years from the residues of countless meals. I cook rice in it twice a week, and the rice it produces is, by a margin I cannot explain in food-science terms, better than rice from any electric cooker I have used. The donabe will be passed on. Earthenware of this kind is built to last several human lifetimes, and the staining and small chips that worry new owners are simply the donabe becoming itself.

3. The wooden cutting board

A single thick slab of hinoki cypress, about thirty centimetres wide. Bought twelve years ago. It has been re-planed once, by a man in our neighbourhood who does this work for a small fee, and is now slightly thinner than it began. The grain has darkened around the centre where I do most of my chopping. It dries in the rack on its long edge. It will outlast the kitchen it is in. I have written elsewhere about the consumer logic of buying once, well, and the cutting board is the cleanest example I own. The first cheap plastic board I had as a student lasted two years. This one will reach forty.

The principle that decides between them

If you read the two lists side by side, the principle is not hard to see. The objects that earn permanence are the ones whose material can age honestly — iron, earthenware, cypress, glass blown by hand. The objects that do not are the ones whose material can only degrade — plastic that yellows, coatings that flake, glass moulded to be identical to ten thousand others.

This is not snobbery about materials. A plastic spatula that does its job and remains visually neutral is fine. The issue arises when a material pretends to be something it is not, or when its decline is ugly rather than dignified. The non-stick pan pretends to be a permanent surface and then is not. The matching tumblers pretend to be a considered set and are in fact a default. The plastic scoop pretends to be neutral and is in fact slowly browning at the edge.

Andrew Juniper, in Wabi Sabi: The Japanese Art of Impermanence, makes the point that the wabi-sabi sensibility is fundamentally about truth to material — letting clay be clay, wood be wood, iron be iron. A kitchen edited along these lines is not aesthetically extreme. Most of it looks ordinary. But the objects in it have, over time, all settled into being honestly themselves.

How to begin, if you would like to

I would not recommend a weekend purge. Wabi-sabi kitchens are not made by a single grand decision. They are made by twenty small ones, spread across a year, each one taken when an existing object reaches the end of its useful life.

The practice I would suggest instead is the running list. Keep a small page in a notebook — the back of your kakeibo works well — with two columns. Would replace. Would not. Walk through your kitchen once a month with the notebook open and add what comes to mind. Do not act on the list. Just keep it.

Within three months, the pattern of your own kitchen will become visible to you. You will see that certain objects keep migrating to the would replace column and that you have been quietly tolerating them for years. You will also see that certain objects you had never thought about before keep ending up in the other column — small, ordinary, irreplaceable. The list is the practice. The replacements happen on their own, slowly, when the moment arises.

For readers who keep a small Mindful Yen shelf at home — the kakeibo journals, the printable wabi-sabi cards — the same logic applies. The objects you find yourself returning to are the ones to keep. The rest can drift.

A note on what gets photographed

Most of what I have described in this essay would not photograph well. The kettle is dark and small. The cutting board has a stain. The donabe is the colour of unwashed clay. There is no styled flat-lay here, no morning-light shot of the perfect breakfast spread. This is the wabi-sabi instinct working against the contemporary kitchen, which has been photographed into a particular kind of brightness that has very little to do with how a kitchen actually feels at five in the evening on a Wednesday.

The kettle is whistling now. I will turn off the gas and pour. The chip on the spout is exactly where it has always been. I write more about these small kitchen rituals in my Substack, for readers who would like a quieter weekly letter from Tokyo.
